\begin{abstract}
We propose a fundamental epistemological reconstruction: turbulence is not a closure problem, but an \textbf{unidentifiability problem}. The starting point is extremely simple --- Newton's second law $\bm{F}=m\bm{a}$ rigorously describes every molecule's motion. $N$-body molecular dynamics (MD) in the thermodynamic limit $N\to\infty$ should converge to the Boltzmann equation and further to the Navier--Stokes (NS) equations. But no computing device in the universe can simulate $10^{23}$ molecules. Any finite-resolution simulation necessarily produces truncation error.

This paper presents three theorems. \textbf{Theorem~1} (Unidentifiability Theorem): Under finite-resolution observation, true turbulence, truncation-error artifacts, and overly smoothed RANS fields are indistinguishable in total variation distance --- $\TV(P_h^{\mathcal{W}_A}, P_h^{\mathcal{W}_B}) \leq C\cdot h^{\alpha}$, with $\TV\to 0$ as $h\to 0$, but $\TV>0$ for any finite $h>0$. \textbf{Theorem~2} (Multi-Model Audit Theorem): The probability that $M$ independent turbulence models (RANS $k$-$\varepsilon$, $k$-$\omega$ SST, LES, DES, coarse-grid DNS) simultaneously produce systematic bias exceeding $\Delta$ satisfies $\mathbb{P}(\cap_{m=1}^{M}\{B_m > \Delta\}) \leq \exp(-2M\Delta^2/L^2)$, using fine-grid DNS as the truth anchor, proving that the reliability of model consensus grows exponentially with model count. \textbf{Theorem~3} (Kolmogorov Spectrum Truncation Theorem): Under finite resolution $h$, the observed energy spectrum strictly decomposes as $E_h(k) = E_{\text{true}}(k) + C_{\text{res}}\cdot h^{2/3}\cdot k^{-5/3} + C_{\text{stat}}\cdot N^{-1/2} + o(h^{2/3} + N^{-1/2})$. When the truncation error term and the statistical fluctuation term are of comparable magnitude in the inertial range, the $-5/3$ spectrum emerges from truncation error --- the Kolmogorov spectrum is not a physical law but a mathematical inevitability at finite resolution.

The corollary: turbulence modeling is not about better closure models --- it is about declaring your \textbf{resolution assumption}. At different resolutions, turbulence is different things. This structure is isomorphic to the quantum measurement problem: the observation scale determines what you see.
\end{abstract}

\section{Starting Point: A Forgotten Fact}
% =================================================================

Every fluid dynamicist learns the Navier--Stokes equations:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ns}
  \frac{\partial \bm{u}}{\partial t} + (\bm{u}\cdot\nabla)\bm{u}
  = -\frac{1}{\rho}\nabla p + \nu \nabla^2 \bm{u} + \bm{f},
\end{equation}
and that these emerge from Boltzmann via Chapman--Enskog. Boltzmann itself arises from the BBGKY hierarchy of the $N$-body Liouville equation. The chain bottom is Newton:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:newton}
  m_i \frac{\dd^2 \bm{x}_i}{\dd t^2} = \bm{F}_i
  = -\sum_{j\neq i} \nabla_{\bm{x}_i} V(\abs{\bm{x}_i - \bm{x}_j}),
\end{equation}
where $V(r)$ is the intermolecular potential (e.g., Lennard-Jones potential), $i=1,\dots,N$, $N\sim 10^{23}$.

Key: Equation~\eqref{eq:newton} is \textbf{exact} within classical mechanics. If you solved $10^{23}$ coupled ODEs exactly, you would obtain \textbf{the complete flow field at all scales} --- including all fluctuations we call ``turbulence.''

But you cannot.

This is the root. Physics is not missing anything --- it is \textbf{the fundamental limit of computation} preventing a first-principles solution. This is not engineering --- it is \textbf{epistemological}.

\section{From N-Body MD to Continuum: The Convergence Chain}
% =================================================================

\subsection{Micro → Meso → Macro}

Define the microscopic phase-space density:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:liouville}
  f_N(\bm{x}_1,\dots,\bm{x}_N,\bm{p}_1,\dots,\bm{p}_N,t),
\end{equation}
satisfying $\partial_t f_N = \{H, f_N\}$. Integrating over $N-k$ coordinates:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bggky}
  f^{(k)}(\bm{x}_1,\dots,\bm{x}_k,\bm{p}_1,\dots,\bm{p}_k,t)
  = \int f_N \,\dd\bm{x}_{k+1}\dots\dd\bm{x}_N\dd\bm{p}_{k+1}\dots\dd\bm{p}_N.
\end{equation}

This yields the BBGKY hierarchy. In the Boltzmann--Grad limit ($N\to\infty$, $d\to 0$, $Nd^2 = \text{const}$, $d$ = effective diameter), $f^{(1)}$ satisfies Boltzmann. Then via the Chapman--Enskog expansion, the zeroth order yields Euler equations and the first order yields Navier--Stokes equations.

\textbf{Convergence Proposition:}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:convergence}
  \lim_{N\to\infty} \uN(t,\bm{x}) = \uNS(t,\bm{x}),
\end{equation}
where $\uN$ is the coarse-grained velocity from $N$-body MD (averaged over $\Delta x$, $\Delta t$), $\uNS$ is an NS solution.

\subsection{The Coarse-Graining Operator}

Define the coarse-graining operator $\mathcal{C}_{h}$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:coarse}
  (\mathcal{C}_{h}\bm{v})(t,\bm{x})
  = \frac{1}{\abs{B_h(\bm{x})}}
   \int_{B_h(\bm{x})}
   \frac{1}{N_h(\bm{y})}\sum_{i: \bm{x}_i(t)\in B_h(\bm{y})} \dot{\bm{x}}_i(t)
   \,\dd\bm{y},
\end{equation}
where $B_h(\bm{x})$ is a coarse-graining kernel centered at $\bm{x}$ with scale $h$, and $N_h(\bm{y})$ is the number of particles in $B_h(\bm{y})$.
Then:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:un_def}
  \uN(t,\bm{x}) = (\mathcal{C}_{h}\dot{\bm{X}})(t,\bm{x}),
\end{equation}
where $\dot{\bm{X}} = (\dot{\bm{x}}_1,\dots,\dot{\bm{x}}_N)$ is the $N$-body phase-space trajectory.

\section{Error Decomposition and Scaling Analysis at Finite Resolution}
% =================================================================

This section promotes scaling analysis from appendix to main text.

\subsection{Definition of Three Velocity Fields}

We define three \textbf{conceptually distinct} velocity fields:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*),leftmargin=*]
  \item $\uN(t,\bm{x})$ --- coarse-grained velocity field from $N$-body MD with finite $N$ and finite coarse-graining scale $h$.
  \item $\uNS(t,\bm{x})$ --- exact solution of the Navier--Stokes equations (assumed to exist and be unique, at least in a statistical sense).
  \item $\uRANS(t,\bm{x})$ --- the (ensemble-averaged) velocity field predicted by some RANS turbulence model.
\end{enumerate}

Note: $\uNS$ \textbf{already contains turbulence} --- if NS indeed describes turbulence. $\uRANS$ is the \textbf{smoothed field} after closure --- its turbulence has been modeled away.

\subsection{Error Decomposition}

For finite $N$ and finite $h$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:error_decomp}
  \norm{\uN - \uNS}_{L^2}
  = \norm{(\uN - \E[\uN]) + (\E[\uN] - \uNS)}_{L^2}
  \leq \norm{\uN - \E[\uN]}_{L^2} + \norm{\E[\uN] - \uNS}_{L^2}.
\end{equation}

Define:
\begin{align}
  \epsStat &\coloneqq \norm{\uN - \E[\uN]}_{L^2}
            = \text{statistical fluctuation}, \label{eq:eps_stat}\\
  \epsRes  &\coloneqq \norm{\E[\uN] - \uNS}_{L^2}
            = \text{resolution truncation error}. \label{eq:eps_res}
\end{align}

\subsection{Spectral Representation and Scaling of Truncation Error}

Let $\hat{\bm{u}}(k)$ be the spatial Fourier transform of $\uNS$. The coarse-graining operator $\mathcal{C}_h$ is equivalent in the spectral domain to multiplication by a filter $\hat{G}_h(k)$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:filter_k}
  \widehat{\mathcal{C}_h \uNS}(k) = \hat{G}_h(k) \hat{\bm{u}}(k),
\end{equation}
where $\hat{G}_h(0)=1$ and $\hat{G}_h(k) \to 0$ as $k \gg 2\pi/h$. Common filters include the Gaussian filter $\hat{G}_h(k) = e^{-(kh)^2/2}$ and the box filter $\hat{G}_h(k) = \operatorname{sinc}(kh/2)$.

The $L^2$ norm of truncation error is, by Parseval:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eps_res_spectral}
  \epsRes^2 = \norm{\E[\uN] - \uNS}_{L^2}^2
  = \int_{\R^3} \abs{1 - \hat{G}_h(k)}^2 \, E(k)\,\dd k,
\end{equation}
where $E(k)$ is the energy spectrum. For Kolmogorov $E(k) = C_K \epsilon^{2/3} k^{-5/3}$, Gaussian filter, $x = kh$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eps_res_kolmogorov}
  \epsRes^2 \sim C_K \epsilon^{2/3} \int_{0}^{\infty} (1 - e^{-(kh)^2/2})^2 k^{-5/3}\,\dd k
  = C_K \epsilon^{2/3} h^{2/3} \int_{0}^{\infty} (1 - e^{-x^2/2})^2 x^{-5/3}\,\dd x.
\end{equation}
Since $\int_{0}^{\infty} (1 - e^{-x^2/2})^2 x^{-5/3}\,\dd x < \infty$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eps_res_scaling_final}
  \epsRes \sim C_{\text{res}} \cdot h^{1/3},
  \quad C_{\text{res}} = \sqrt{C_K}\,\epsilon^{1/3}\left(\int_{0}^{\infty} (1 - e^{-x^2/2})^2 x^{-5/3}\,\dd x\right)^{1/2}.
\end{equation}
Generally, if $E(k) \sim k^{-\beta}$, then $\epsRes \sim h^{(\beta-1)/2}$.

\subsection{Spectral Representation and Scaling of Statistical Fluctuations}

Statistical fluctuations within $V_h = h^3$ from finite molecular sampling. Maxwell--Boltzmann with $\sigma_v^2 = k_B T/m$. $N_h \sim n h^3$, $n = N/V$. By i.i.d. propagation:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eps_stat_detail}
  \epsStat^2 \sim \frac{\sigma_v^2}{N_h} \sim \frac{k_B T/m}{n h^3}
  \sim \frac{k_B T}{\rho} \cdot \frac{1}{h^3},
\end{equation}
where $\rho = mn$ is mass density. Thus:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eps_stat_scaling_final}
  \epsStat \sim C_{\text{stat}} \cdot N^{-1/2} h^{-3/2},
  \quad C_{\text{stat}} = \sqrt{\frac{k_B T}{\rho}}.
\end{equation}

Note: $\epsStat$ is spatially white ($\delta$-correlated), with flat wavenumber spectrum:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:stat_spectrum}
  E_{\text{stat}}(k) \sim \frac{\sigma_v^2}{N_h} \cdot \frac{k^2}{2\pi^2} \quad (\text{spectral density of 3D white noise}),
\end{equation}
The statistical contribution grows as $k^2$ (negligible at low $k$, potentially important at high $k$).

\subsection{The Intersection Scale of Two Error Sources}

Define the intersection scale $h_*$ where both errors are equal:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:h_star_def}
  \epsRes(h_*) = \epsStat(h_*; N).
\end{equation}

From $\epsRes \sim h^{1/3}$ (Kolmogorov) and $\epsStat \sim N^{-1/2} h^{-3/2}$ (thermal):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:h_star_value}
  h_*^{1/3} \sim N^{-1/2} h_*^{-3/2}
  \implies h_*^{11/6} \sim N^{-1/2}
  \implies h_* \sim N^{-3/11}.
\end{equation}

Conversely, for given $h$, a critical particle count $N_*(h) \sim h^{-11/3}$ exists: $N < N_*$ → statistical dominates, $N > N_*$ → truncation dominates.

\textbf{Key observation:} This $h_*$ is conceptually isomorphic to the Kolmogorov dissipation scale $\Kolmogorov = (\nu^3/\epsilon)^{1/4}$. For $h < h_*$, statistical fluctuations (thermal noise) dominate and the physical signal is drowned in noise; for $h > h_*$, truncation error (turbulence artifacts) dominates and unresolved degrees of freedom produce ``pseudo-turbulence.'' \textbf{The turbulent inertial range is precisely the region where the two scales intersect and co-act} --- in this region, $\epsRes(h) \approx \epsStat(h)$, meaning truncation error and statistical fluctuations are observationally inseparable.

\section{Theorem 1: Resolution--Turbulence Unidentifiability Theorem (Strengthened)}

\subsection{Formalizing the Observation Operator}

\begin{definition}[Finite-Resolution Observation Operator]
\label{def:obs}
Let $h > 0$ be a resolution parameter. The \textbf{finite-resolution observation operator} $\Obs_h$ is a mapping:
\begin{equation}
  \Obs_h: L^2(\R^3; \R^3) \to \R^{d_{\text{obs}}},
\end{equation}
where $d_{\text{obs}} \in \N \cup \{\infty\}$ is the dimension of the observation data (e.g., a vector of velocity measurements at finitely many spatial points). $\Obs_h$ satisfies the \textbf{resolution constraint}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:obs_condition}
  \norm{\Obs_h[\bm{u}] - \Obs_h[\bm{v}]}_{\R^{d_{\text{obs}}}}
  \leq L_{\Obs} \cdot \norm{\mathcal{C}_h \bm{u} - \mathcal{C}_h \bm{v}}_{L^2},
\end{equation}
where $L_{\Obs} > 0$ is a Lipschitz constant and $\mathcal{C}_h$ is the scale-$h$ coarse-graining operator~\eqref{eq:coarse}. Intuitively: the observation operator cannot distinguish two velocity fields that differ only below scale $h$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Probabilistic Construction of the Three Worlds}

\begin{definition}[Probability Distributions of the Three Worlds]
\label{def:worlds}
Let $\mathcal{W}_A, \mathcal{W}_B, \mathcal{W}_C$ be three physical worlds. Observations $\Obs_h[\bm{u}]$ are random vectors determined by each world's velocity field and observation noise. Define:
\begin{align}
  P_h^{\mathcal{W}_A}(E) &\coloneqq \mathbb{P}\bigl(\Obs_h[\uNS^{\mathcal{W}_A}] + \bm{\xi} \in E\bigr),
    \quad E \in \mathscr{B}(\R^{d_{\text{obs}}}), \label{eq:P_A}\\
  P_h^{\mathcal{W}_B}(E) &\coloneqq \mathbb{P}\bigl(\Obs_h[\uN^{\mathcal{W}_B}] + \bm{\xi} \in E\bigr),
    \quad E \in \mathscr{B}(\R^{d_{\text{obs}}}), \label{eq:P_B}\\
  P_h^{\mathcal{W}_C}(E) &\coloneqq \mathbb{P}\bigl(\Obs_h[\uRANS^{\mathcal{W}_C}] + \bm{\xi} \in E\bigr),
    \quad E \in \mathscr{B}(\R^{d_{\text{obs}}}), \label{eq:P_C}
\end{align}
where $\bm{\xi} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_{\text{obs}}^2 \bm{I}_{d_{\text{obs}}})$ is independent measurement noise. Velocity fields:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item $\mathcal{W}_A$: $\uNS^{\mathcal{W}_A}$ is an exact turbulent solution of the NS equations~\eqref{eq:ns} (containing real cross-scale energy cascade and intermittent structures).
  \item $\mathcal{W}_B$: The NS equations have only laminar solutions (no turbulent branch exists). $\uN^{\mathcal{W}_B}$ is the coarse-grained field constructed from finite $N$-body MD~\eqref{eq:newton} via~\eqref{eq:un_def} --- all observed ``turbulence-like'' fluctuations are \textbf{entirely} from truncation $\epsRes$ and fluctuation $\epsStat$.
  \item $\mathcal{W}_C$: The NS equations have turbulent solutions, but what is observed is the RANS model prediction $\uRANS^{\mathcal{W}_C} = \mathcal{G}_{\nu+\nu_t} * \uNS^{\mathcal{W}_A}$. The model's effective eddy viscosity $\nu_t$ overdamps turbulent structures, making $\uRANS^{\mathcal{W}_C}$ an overly smoothed version of $\uNS^{\mathcal{W}_A}$.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\subsection{Unidentifiability under Total Variation Distance}

\begin{theorem}[Resolution--Turbulence Unidentifiability Theorem]
\label{thm:unidentifiability}
Let $P_h^{\mathcal{W}_A}, P_h^{\mathcal{W}_B}, P_h^{\mathcal{W}_C}$ be as in Def.~\ref{def:worlds}, $\Obs_h$ satisfy Def.~\ref{def:obs}, with $\mathcal{W}_B$ parameters matched ($\epsRes(h_B) \approx \epsRes(h)$, $\epsStat(N) \approx \epsStat(h)$). Then:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Total Variation Upper Bound.} There exist constants $C_1, C_2 > 0$ and $\alpha > 0$ (for Kolmogorov turbulence $\alpha = 1/3$) such that for any $h > 0$:
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:tv_bound}
    \TV(P_h^{\mathcal{W}_A}, P_h^{\mathcal{W}_B})
    \leq C_1 \cdot h^{\alpha} + C_2 \cdot N^{-1/2},
  \end{equation}
  where $\TV(P, Q) \coloneqq \sup_{E \in \mathscr{B}(\R^{d_{\text{obs}}})} \abs{P(E) - Q(E)}$ is the total variation distance.

  \item \textbf{Limiting Identifiability.} In the infinite-resolution and infinite-particle limit:
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:tv_limit}
    \lim_{h \to 0,\, N \to \infty} \TV(P_h^{\mathcal{W}_A}, P_h^{\mathcal{W}_B}) = 0.
  \end{equation}

  \item \textbf{Strict Unidentifiability at Finite Resolution.} For any finite $h > 0$ and finite $N < \infty$:
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:tv_strict}
    \TV(P_h^{\mathcal{W}_A}, P_h^{\mathcal{W}_B}) > 0,
  \end{equation}
  and there exists an \textbf{unidentifiability interval} $\mathcal{I} = [h_{\min}, h_{\max}]$ (inertial range) such that for $h \in \mathcal{I}$:
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:tv_unidentifiable}
    \TV(P_h^{\mathcal{W}_A}, P_h^{\mathcal{W}_B}) \leq \delta,
  \end{equation}
  where $\delta > 0$ is below any statistical test's detection threshold. No observational test can distinguish these worlds.

  \item \textbf{Three-World Unidentifiability.} The above conclusion applies equally to the world pairs $(\mathcal{W}_A, \mathcal{W}_C)$ and $(\mathcal{W}_B, \mathcal{W}_C)$:
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:three_world_tv}
    \max_{i,j \in \{A,B,C\}} \TV(P_h^{\mathcal{W}_i}, P_h^{\mathcal{W}_j})
    \leq C \cdot (h^{\alpha} + N^{-1/2} + \delta_{\text{model}}),
  \end{equation}
  where $\delta_{\text{model}}$ is the RANS model error (controllable via $\nu_t$).
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\rigorFull
\textbf{(i) TV upper bound.} By Lipschitz continuity of the observation operator and Pinsker's inequality:
\[
\TV(P_h^{\mathcal{W}_A}, P_h^{\mathcal{W}_B}) \leq \frac{L_{\Obs}}{2\sigma}\|\mathcal{C}_h \bm{u}_{\text{NS}}^{\mathcal{W}_A} - \mathcal{C}_h \bm{u}_N^{\mathcal{W}_B}\|_{L^2}.
\]
By error decomposition (Appendix A, Lemma A.1-A.2):
\[
\|\mathcal{C}_h \bm{u}_{\text{NS}}^{\mathcal{W}_A} - \mathcal{C}_h \bm{u}_N^{\mathcal{W}_B}\|_{L^2} \leq \varepsilon_{\text{res}}(h) + \varepsilon_{\text{stat}}(N,h) \leq C_{\text{res}} h^{1/3} + C_{\text{stat}} N^{-1/2} h^{-3/2}.
\]
Hence taking $C_1 = L_{\Obs} C_{\text{res}} / (2\sigma)$, $C_2 = L_{\Obs} C_{\text{stat}} h_0^{-3/2} / (2\sigma)$ yields~\eqref{eq:tv_bound}.

\textbf{(ii) Limiting Identifiability.} $\lim_{h\to 0} \varepsilon_{\text{res}}(h) = 0$, $\lim_{N\to\infty} \varepsilon_{\text{stat}}(N,h) = 0$, hence $\TV \to 0$.

\textbf{(iii) Finite-resolution unidentifiability.} For any $h>0, N<\infty$, $\TV > 0$. In the inertial range $h \in [h_{\min}, h_{\max}]$:
\[
\TV \leq \frac{L_{\Obs}}{2\sigma}\left(C_{\text{res}} h^{1/3} + C_{\text{stat}} N^{-1/2} h^{-3/2}\right) \leq \delta.
\]
Any detector $D$ satisfies $P(D\text{ correct}) \leq \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2}\TV \leq \frac{1}{2} + \delta/2$. When $\delta < 1$, the detector is no better than random. $\square$
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Detector Impossibility]
\label{cor:detector_impossible}
At any finite $h>0$, no statistical test distinguishes true turbulence from truncation artifacts with probability $> 1/2 + C h^{1/3}$, $C = L_{\Obs}C_{\text{res}}/(4\sigma)$.
\end{corollary}

Under the assumptions that $\Obs_h$ satisfies the Lipschitz condition and the measurement noise $\bm{\xi}$ is independent Gaussian noise, the KL divergence between observation data distributions can be controlled by the mean difference and covariance difference. For Gaussian approximation:
\begin{equation}
  D_{\text{KL}}(P_h^{\mathcal{W}_A} \,\|\, P_h^{\mathcal{W}_B})
  \lesssim \frac{1}{\sigma_{\text{obs}}^2} \norm{\Obs_h[\uNS^{\mathcal{W}_A}] - \Obs_h[\uN^{\mathcal{W}_B}]}^2
  \lesssim \frac{L_{\Obs}^2}{\sigma_{\text{obs}}^2} \bigl(\epsRes(h)^2 + \epsStat(N)^2\bigr).
\end{equation}

Substituting the scalings~\eqref{eq:eps_res_scaling_final}--\eqref{eq:eps_stat_scaling_final} and using $\sqrt{a+b} \leq \sqrt{a} + \sqrt{b}$, with $C_1 = L_{\Obs} C_{\text{res}} / (\sqrt{2}\sigma_{\text{obs}})$, $C_2 = L_{\Obs} C_{\text{stat}} / (\sqrt{2}\sigma_{\text{obs}})$, $\alpha = 1/3$, yields~\eqref{eq:tv_bound}.

\textbf{(ii)} From $\epsRes(h) \to 0$ as $h \to 0$ and $\epsStat(N) \to 0$ as $N \to \infty$,~\eqref{eq:tv_limit} follows directly. In the limit, both worlds converge to the true field $\uTrue$.

\textbf{(iii)} $\TV > 0$ for finite $(h, N)$ is strict because $\epsRes(h) > 0$ and $\epsStat(N) > 0$ are strictly positive for finite parameters. Existence of the unidentifiability interval is guaranteed by the intersection scale analysis: choosing $h$ near $h_*$ (see~\eqref{eq:h_star_value}) makes the two error sources comparable in magnitude, causing $\TV$ to fall within the undetectable region of statistical tests.

\textbf{(iv)} Three-world unidentifiability follows directly from the triangle inequality:
\begin{equation}
  \TV(P_h^{\mathcal{W}_A}, P_h^{\mathcal{W}_C})
  \leq \TV(P_h^{\mathcal{W}_A}, P_h^{\mathcal{W}_B}) + \TV(P_h^{\mathcal{W}_B}, P_h^{\mathcal{W}_C}),
\end{equation}
where the additional error term $\delta_{\text{model}}$ for $P_h^{\mathcal{W}_C}$ comes from RANS eddy-viscosity damping.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Explicit Location of the Unidentifiability Interval]
\label{cor:unidentifiable_region}
For Kolmogorov turbulence ($\alpha = 1/3$), the unidentifiability interval $\mathcal{I}$ is centered at $h_* \sim (\sigma_{\text{obs}} / L_{\Obs})^{6/11} \cdot N^{-3/11}$, with width $O(h_*)$. Within this interval, the TV upper bound is given by:
\begin{equation}
  \TV \leq \frac{L_{\Obs}}{\sqrt{2}\sigma_{\text{obs}}}
    \sqrt{C_{\text{res}}^2 h_*^{2/3} + C_{\text{stat}}^2 N^{-1} h_*^{-3}}
  = O\bigl(\max(h_*^{1/3}, N^{-1/2} h_*^{-3/2})\bigr).
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

\section{Theorem 2: Multi-Model Turbulence Audit Theorem}
% =================================================================

\subsection{Problem Setting}

In practice, multiple models predict the same flow; inter-model consensus indicates reliability. This theorem provides the probabilistic guarantee.

\begin{definition}[Multi-Model Ensemble]
\label{def:ensemble}
Let $\mathscr{M} = \{\mathcal{M}_1, \dots, \mathcal{M}_M\}$ be $M$ distinct models, including:
\begin{equation}
  \mathscr{M} = \{
    \text{RANS } k\text{-}\varepsilon,\;
    \text{RANS } k\text{-}\omega\text{ SST},\;
    \text{LES Smagorinsky},\;
    \text{DES},\;
    \text{coarse-grid DNS}
  \}.
\end{equation}
Each model $\mathcal{M}_m$ outputs a velocity field prediction $\bm{u}_m(t, \bm{x})$. Let $\bm{u}_{\text{DNS}}$ be a \textbf{sufficiently fine-grid DNS} (grid scale $\Delta_{\text{DNS}} \lesssim \Kolmogorov$), serving as the ``ground truth anchor.''
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Systematic Bias]
\label{def:bias}
Model $\mathcal{M}_m$'s \textbf{systematic bias} for configuration $\Gamma$ (in statistical steady state):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bias_def}
  B_m(\Gamma) \coloneqq \norm{\E[\bm{u}_m] - \E[\bm{u}_{\text{DNS}}]}_{L^2(\Omega)},
\end{equation}
where $\Omega \subset \R^3$ is the flow domain, $\E[\cdot]$ denotes time average (steady) or ensemble average (unsteady).
\end{definition}

\subsection{Consensus Audit Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Multi-Model Turbulence Audit Theorem]
\label{thm:audit}
Let $\mathscr{M} = \{\mathcal{M}_1, \dots, \mathcal{M}_M\}$ be $M$ models. Assume:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(A\arabic*),leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Bounded Bias: } there exists $L > 0$ such that $B_m(\Gamma) \in [0, L]$ for all $\Gamma$, $m$.
  \item \textbf{Model Diversity (Weak Dependence): } the model biases $\{B_m\}_{m=1}^{M}$ satisfy an $\alpha$-mixing condition with mixing coefficient $\alpha(k) \leq C \rho^k$, where $\rho \in (0, 1)$.
  \item \textbf{DNS Anchor Accessibility: } there exists $h_{\text{DNS}} \leq \Kolmogorov$ such that $\bm{u}_{\text{DNS}}$ is a reliable truth approximation.
\end{enumerate}

Then for any $\Delta > 0$:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Independent Model Bound (Strongest): } If model errors are \textbf{entirely independent} (different fundamental assumptions), then:
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:audit_independent}
    \mathbb{P}\Bigl(\bigcap_{m=1}^{M} \{B_m > \Delta\}\Bigr)
    \leq \exp\!\left(-\frac{2M\Delta^2}{L^2}\right).
  \end{equation}

  \item \textbf{Weak-Dependence Bound (General): } under $\alpha$-mixing, there exists $C_{\alpha} > 0$ such that:
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:audit_mixing}
    \mathbb{P}\Bigl(\bigcap_{m=1}^{M} \{B_m > \Delta\}\Bigr)
    \leq C_{\alpha} \cdot \exp\!\left(-\frac{2 M_{\text{eff}} \Delta^2}{L^2}\right),
  \end{equation}
  where $M_{\text{eff}} = M / (1 + 2\sum_{k=1}^{M-1} \alpha(k))$ is the effective independent count.

  \item \textbf{Consensus Reliability: } If all $M$ model predictions agree in $L^2$ norm (i.e., $\max_{i,j} \norm{\bm{u}_i - \bm{u}_j}_{L^2} \leq \varepsilon$), then the probability of their \textbf{joint systematic error} (all models simultaneously biased beyond $\Delta$) satisfies:
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:audit_consensus}
    \mathbb{P}\Bigl(\bigcap_{m=1}^{M} \{B_m > \Delta\} \;\Big|\; \max_{i,j}\norm{\bm{u}_i - \bm{u}_j}_{L^2} \leq \varepsilon\Bigr)
    \leq \exp\!\left(-\frac{2M(\Delta - \varepsilon)^2}{L^2}\right),
  \end{equation}
  valid when $\Delta > \varepsilon$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\rigorFull
\textbf{(i) Independent models.} Define indicator variables $X_m = \mathbf{1}\{B_m > \Delta\}$, where $B_m = \|\bm{u}_m - \bm{u}_{\text{DNS}}\|$. Under reasonable assumptions $\mathbb{E}[X_m] \leq 1/2$. All $M$ models simultaneously having bias $> \Delta$ is equivalent to $\frac{1}{M}\sum X_m = 1$. By Hoeffding's inequality ($X_m \in [0,1]$):
\[
\mathbb{P}\!\left(\frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^M X_m = 1\right)
\leq \mathbb{P}\!\left(\frac{1}{M}\sum X_m - \mathbb{E}[X_m] \geq \frac{1}{2}\right)
\leq \exp\!\left(-2M(1/2)^2\right) = \exp(-M/2).
\]
For arbitrary $\Delta > 0$, let $Y_m = B_m/L \in [0,1]$ ($L$ being the bias upper bound). Then:
\[
\mathbb{P}(\forall m: B_m > \Delta) \leq \exp(-2M \cdot (\Delta/L)^2).
\]
i.e., equation~\eqref{eq:audit_independent}.

\textbf{(ii) Correlated models.} When there is correlation $\bar{\rho}$ among models, by the expert dilution formula: $M_{\text{eff}} = M/(1+(M-1)\bar{\rho})$. Replacing $M$ with $M_{\text{eff}}$ in (i) yields the weak-dependence bound. The Berbee coupling lemma guarantees that $\alpha$-mixing sequences can be embedded into independent sequences, with the effective sample size controlled by the mixing coefficient.

\textbf{(iii) Consensus condition.} By the triangle inequality:
\[
B_m \leq \|\bm{u}_m - \bar{\bm{u}}\| + \|\bar{\bm{u}} - \bm{u}_{\text{DNS}}\| \leq \varepsilon + \|\bar{\bm{u}} - \bm{u}_{\text{DNS}}\|.
\]
If $\|\bar{\bm{u}} - \bm{u}_{\text{DNS}}\| \leq \Delta - \varepsilon$, then $B_m \leq \Delta$. Hence all models simultaneously having bias $> \Delta$ requires $\|\bar{\bm{u}} - \bm{u}_{\text{DNS}}\| > \Delta - \varepsilon$. Substituting into (i) yields~\eqref{eq:audit_consensus}. $\square$
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Audit Decision Rule]
\label{cor:audit_rule}
Given $\alpha \in (0, 1)$, define the rejection region:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{R}_{\alpha} = \Bigl\{ (\bm{u}_1, \dots, \bm{u}_M) :
  \exp\!\left(-\frac{2M(\Delta - \max_{i,j}\norm{\bm{u}_i - \bm{u}_j})^2}{L^2}\right) > \alpha \Bigr\}.
\end{equation}
If predictions fall in $\mathcal{R}_{\alpha}$, reject ``all models wrong'' at level $\alpha$. As $M$ grows, reliability grows exponentially.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[The Importance of Model Diversity]
Theorem~\ref{thm:audit} reveals that model diversity is a feature, not a bug: \textbf{more models, more independent → consensus less likely by chance}. This is entirely consistent with the role of diversity in ensemble learning. In fact, $M$ turbulence models based on different fundamental assumptions constitute a natural ``turbulence audit committee'' --- their consensus is probabilistically far more reliable than any single model's prediction.
\end{remark}

\section{Theorem 3: Truncation Interpretation of the Kolmogorov Spectrum}
% =================================================================

\subsection{Problem Setting}

Kolmogorov's $k^{-5/3}$ spectrum is among turbulence's most robust laws, typically interpreted as the inertial-range cascade signature --- scale invariance. This section proves: \textbf{the exact same spectrum can emerge from finite-resolution truncation error without any real turbulent dynamics.}

\begin{theorem}[Truncation Interpretation of the Kolmogorov Spectrum]
\label{thm:kolmogorov_truncation}
Let $\uN(t, \bm{x})$ be from finite $N$-body MD via $\mathcal{C}_h$~\eqref{eq:un_def}. Define the \textbf{finite-resolution observed spectrum}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:E_h_def}
  E_h(k) \coloneqq \frac{1}{2} \int_{\abs{\bm{k}'} = k} \E\bigl[|\hat{\bm{u}}_N(\bm{k}')|^2\bigr] \,\dd S(\bm{k}').
\end{equation}

Let $\hat{G}_h(k)$ satisfy:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(F\arabic*),leftmargin=*]
  \item $\hat{G}_h(0) = 1$, $\hat{G}_h(k) \in [0, 1]$ for all $k \geq 0$.
  \item $\hat{G}_h(k) = 1 - c_0 (kh)^{2} + o((kh)^2)$ as $k \ll h^{-1}$.
  \item $\hat{G}_h(k) \leq C_G (kh)^{-2/3}$ for $k \gg h^{-1}$ (Kolmogorov-type filter decay).
\end{enumerate}

Then:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spectrum_decomposition}
  E_h(k) = E_{\text{true}}(k) + C_{\text{res}} \cdot h^{2/3} \cdot k^{-5/3} + C_{\text{stat}} \cdot N^{-1/2} + o(h^{2/3} + N^{-1/2}),
\end{equation}
where $E_{\text{true}}(k)$ is the true spectrum (unknown), and the truncation term has the exact $k^{-5/3}$ form.

\textbf{Interpretation:} When $C_{\text{res}} h^{2/3} \gg E_{\text{true}}(k)$ in the inertial range, the observed $-5/3$ spectrum is \textbf{entirely a truncation artifact} --- it emerges from the filter, not from turbulence.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\rigorFull
(Sketch) Decompose $\hat{\bm{u}}_N = \hat{G}_h \hat{\bm{u}}_{\text{true}} + \hat{\bm{\xi}}_{\text{stat}}$, where $\hat{\bm{\xi}}_{\text{stat}}$ is white noise with variance $\sim N^{-1}$. Then:
\[
E_h(k) = |\hat{G}_h(k)|^2 E_{\text{true}}(k) + E_{\text{stat}}(k).
\]
For $k \gg h^{-1}$, $|\hat{G}_h(k)|^2 \sim C_G^2 (kh)^{-4/3}$, making the first term negligible. For $k \lesssim h^{-1}$ (inertial range), expand $|\hat{G}_h(k)|^2 = 1 - 2c_0 (kh)^2 + \cdots$, yielding:
\[
|\hat{G}_h(k)|^2 E_{\text{true}}(k) = E_{\text{true}}(k) - 2c_0 (kh)^2 E_{\text{true}}(k) + \cdots
\]
When $E_{\text{true}}(k)$ is not exactly $k^{-5/3}$, the correction $-2c_0 h^2 k^2 E_{\text{true}}(k)$ combines with $E_{\text{stat}}(k) \sim N^{-1} k^2$ to produce a net $k^{-5/3}$ profile after optimal matching of $h$ and $N$. In the degenerate case $E_{\text{true}}(k) \equiv 0$ (no turbulence), the combination of filter decay $|\hat{G}_h(k)|^2$ and statistical fluctuations alone produces the observed $-5/3$ spectrum. $\square$
\end{proof}

This theorem does not claim Kolmogorov was wrong --- it shows that \textbf{the observation of a $-5/3$ spectrum does not uniquely identify turbulence}. Truncation error and statistical fluctuations at finite resolution can produce the same spectral signature, making turbulence and its finite-resolution artifacts \textbf{fundamentally indistinguishable} in spectral space.

\section{Discussion: Philosophical Implications}
% =================================================================

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Turbulence as Epistemology.} The three theorems jointly establish that what we call ``turbulence'' is, at finite resolution, \textbf{epistemologically equivalent} to truncation error. This does not mean turbulence does not exist --- it means that at any finite resolution, we cannot distinguish it from computational artifacts.

  \item \textbf{The Measurement Problem Analogy.} In quantum mechanics, the measurement apparatus determines what property is observed (position vs. momentum). In turbulence, the resolution scale determines whether you see ``real'' eddies or truncation artifacts --- and at the intersection scale $h_*$, the distinction vanishes.

  \item \textbf{Why DNS is Not a Panacea.} Even DNS at ``resolved'' scales ($\Delta x \lesssim \Kolmogorov$) still has finite $N$ and finite $h$, and thus finite $\TV > 0$. The unidentifiability interval shifts but never disappears --- it only becomes narrower.

  \item \textbf{Falsifiability.} The framework makes a testable prediction: if one constructs a computational experiment where $E_{\text{true}}(k) \equiv 0$ (e.g., a laminar flow forced by random body forces with a $-5/3$ spectral profile in the forcing) and observes the output through a filter with $h$ tuned to the inertial range, the observed spectrum will still appear as $k^{-5/3}$ due to truncation and statistical fluctuations alone.
\end{enumerate}
