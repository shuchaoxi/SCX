\subsection{I. Quantum SCX Theorem: Remote Auditing under Quantum Sensing and Quantum Correction to Theorem 3}\label{i-quantum-scx-theorem}

\subsubsection{(Quantum SCX Theorem --- Q-Theorem)}\label{quantum-scx-theorem-q-theorem}

\subsubsection{Context}\label{context-q}

The SCX framework has so far been entirely built on classical information theory: data are classical bits, auditing is computation on classical statistics. But in the quantum information era, a fundamental question emerges: \textbf{If the data being audited exists in quantum states — or if the auditor can leverage quantum sensing to remotely probe data characteristics — does Theorem 3 (noise-difficulty unidentifiability) still hold?}

Quantum mechanics gives two mutually contradictory directions: - \textbf{Quantum no-cloning theorem} suggests: if data holders encode data as quantum states, auditors cannot ``copy a copy to audit'' without disturbing the system. This seems to \textbf{strengthen} Theorem 3's unidentifiability — you cannot even simultaneously obtain precise values of S and ∇S. - \textbf{Quantum entanglement and Bell inequalities} suggest: remote quantum sensing can achieve information extraction impossible by classical means. If noise and difficulty exhibit different correlation structures in some quantum entanglement basis, quantum auditing may \textbf{break} Theorem 3's barrier.

A deeper level: \textbf{What is the quantum generalization of Cercis Score S = Q + ηN?} In a quantum setting, does S become a Hermitian operator \(\hat{S}\), whose expectation value corresponds to classical S and whose variance encodes quantum uncertainty? Does Theorem 3's ``indistinguishability'' correspond to non-commutativity in quantum mechanics?

\subsubsection{Theorem Statement (Draft)}\label{theorem-statement-q-draft}

\begin{quote}
\textbf{Q-Theorem (Quantum SCX Theorem)}:

Let the audited data be encoded as a quantum state \(\rho_D = \sum_i p_i |\psi_i\rangle\langle\psi_i|\) on a finite-dimensional Hilbert space \(\mathcal{H}_D\). Define the quantum Cercis operator: \[\hat{S} = \hat{Q} + \eta \hat{N}\] where \(\hat{Q}\) and \(\hat{N}\) are the quality and noise observables respectively, satisfying \([\hat{Q}, \hat{N}] \neq 0\) in general.

\textbf{(i) Quantum-Strengthened Unidentifiability Theorem (Rigorously Derivable, from quantum no-cloning theorem + Theorem 3)}: If auditor A can only access data through a quantum channel \(\mathcal{E}: \mathcal{L}(\mathcal{H}_D) \to \mathcal{L}(\mathcal{H}_A)\) (\(\mathcal{H}_A\) being the auditor's Hilbert space), and this channel satisfies the \textbf{imperfect cloning bound} \(F(\mathcal{E}(\rho), \rho) \leq F_{\max} < 1\), then there exists a quantum confounding strategy \(\mathcal{C}\) such that: \[\left\| \text{Tr}(\hat{S} \mathcal{E}(\rho_{\text{noise}})) - \text{Tr}(\hat{S} \mathcal{E}(\rho_{\text{hard}})) \right\| \leq \epsilon(F_{\max})\] where \(\rho_{\text{noise}}\) and \(\rho_{\text{hard}}\) are noise-dominated and difficulty-dominated quantum states respectively, and \(\epsilon(F_{\max}) \to 0\) as \(F_{\max} \to 0\). That is: \textbf{the lower the channel fidelity, the more robust the quantum Theorem 3 unidentifiability barrier.}

\textbf{(ii) Quantum Entanglement Audit Advantage (Open Problem)}: Suppose the auditor holds pre-shared entanglement resources \(\Phi_{AB}\) (Bell pairs) with the data party. Define the quantum audit statistic: \[T_Q = \text{Tr}\left((\hat{S}_A \otimes \hat{S}_B) \Phi_{AB}^{\otimes n}\right)\] Question: Do there exist \(\rho_{\text{noise}}\) and \(\rho_{\text{hard}}\) such that under classical auditing \(\|T(\rho_{\text{noise}}) - T(\rho_{\text{hard}})\| < \delta\) (classically indistinguishable), but under quantum auditing \(\|T_Q(\rho_{\text{noise}}) - T_Q(\rho_{\text{hard}})\| > \delta\) (quantumly distinguishable)?

\textbf{Conjecture}: When noise has classical correlation structure and difficulty has quantum entanglement structure, quantum audit advantage exists. The formal condition is: \(\rho_{\text{noise}}\) has zero quantum discord, while \(\rho_{\text{hard}}\) has quantum discord \textgreater{} 0. If the conjecture is true, quantum auditing provides a \textbf{physical mechanism} to break Theorem 3 (rather than merely a cognitive mechanism, as HC-Theorem depends on humans).

\textbf{(iii) Quantum Cercis Uncertainty Relation (Rigorously Derivable, from quantum mechanical uncertainty principle)}: If \([\hat{Q}, \hat{N}] = i\hbar \hat{C}\) (\(C\) being some ``audit charge'' operator), then: \[\Delta Q \cdot \Delta N \geq \frac{1}{2} |\langle \hat{C} \rangle|\] This means: \textbf{In the quantum setting, there is a fundamental tradeoff between the auditor's certainty about quality and certainty about noise} — the more precisely you know data quality, the less you can determine the noise level. This constitutes the ``audit uncertainty principle'' of quantum SCX, the quantum-domain counterpart of Theorem 3 but with an exact mathematical lower bound.
\end{quote}

\subsection{II. Audit Entropy Theorem: SCX's Second Law and the Irreversible Audit Arrow}\label{ii-audit-entropy-theorem}

\subsubsection{(Audit Entropy Theorem --- AE-Theorem)}\label{audit-entropy-theorem-ae-theorem}

\subsubsection{Context}\label{context-ae}

The Second Law of Thermodynamics asserts that the entropy of an isolated system never decreases — this is the deepest irreversibility principle in physics. Does the SCX audit process have an analogous ``Audit Second Law''?

Consider the following analogy: - Physical entropy = uncertainty about the system's microstate - Audit entropy = the auditor's residual uncertainty about data quality - Thermodynamic irreversible process → entropy increase - Audit irreversible process → \textbf{audit capability monotonic increase} (once a noise pattern is discovered, that knowledge cannot be erased)

But there is a subtle inversion here: physical entropy increase means information loss; while ``audit entropy'' increase means information \textbf{acquisition} — the auditor eliminates uncertainty. Therefore, the ``audit entropy'' we should define is actually \textbf{residual ignorance}, which \textbf{monotonically decreases} and the process is irreversible. The Audit Second Law is: \textbf{In the absence of external intervention, the auditor's ignorance cannot increase} — once you learn how to distinguish noise from difficulty, you cannot ``forget'' it.

A deeper structure: Can this ``audit entropy'' be characterized by a genuine information-theoretic functional? Does it have a formal duality with thermodynamic entropy — connected through Landauer's principle (erasing information requires energy/heat)?

\subsubsection{Theorem Statement (Draft)}\label{theorem-statement-ae-draft}

\begin{quote}
\textbf{AE-Theorem (Audit Entropy Theorem)}:

Define the audit system's \textbf{Audit Entropy} at time \(t\) as the conditional entropy of the true noise label \(\varepsilon(x)\) given the auditor's information: \[H_A(t) = H(\varepsilon | \mathcal{I}_A^{(t)})\] where \(\mathcal{I}_A^{(t)}\) is the auditor's cumulative information set at time \(t\) (containing Cercis Scores, gradients, human judgments, etc., for all audited samples).

\textbf{(i) Audit Entropy Monotonic Decrease Theorem — Audit Second Law (Rigorously Derivable, from information-theoretic data processing inequality)}: For any audit process \(\mathcal{A}\) satisfying the ``no-forgetting'' condition (\(\mathcal{I}_A^{(t)} \subseteq \mathcal{I}_A^{(t+1)}\), information set only grows), we have: \[H_A(t+1) \leq H_A(t)\] i.e., audit entropy is monotone non-increasing. Equality holds if and only if the \((t+1)\)-th audit step provides no new information about \(\varepsilon\) (i.e., \(I(\varepsilon; \mathcal{I}_A^{(t+1)} | \mathcal{I}_A^{(t)}) = 0\)).

This is \textbf{SCX's Audit Second Law}: the auditor's ignorance monotonically decreases, and the decreasing process is irreversible — you cannot increase \(H_A\) through any operation using only \(\mathcal{I}_A^{(t)}\). The audit arrow points toward ever-lower audit entropy.

\textbf{(ii) Audit Thermodynamic Equivalence Theorem (Open Problem, partially derivable)}: Define the \textbf{Landauer cost} of auditing: the computation resource lower bound required to reduce audit entropy from \(H_A(t)\) by \(\Delta H\) is: \[E_{\text{audit}} \geq k_B T \cdot \Delta H \cdot \ln 2\] where \(k_B\) is Boltzmann's constant and \(T\) is the physical temperature of the computational substrate.

\textbf{Open part}: Is this lower bound achievable? i.e., does there exist an optimal audit protocol whose computational cost exactly equals the Landauer lower bound? This depends on the relationship between the computational complexity of Situs encoding and physical entropy — if Situs encoding itself is ``thermodynamically optimal'' (i.e., the entropy production of its generation process exactly equals the entropy that must be erased for auditing), then the lower bound is tight.

\textbf{(iii) Audit Entropy Convergence Theorem — Audit Heat Death (Rigorously Derivable, from AE-Theorem (i) + Theorem 3)}: The lower bound of audit entropy is given by Theorem 3: \[\lim_{t \to \infty} H_A(t) = H_{\min} > 0\] where \(H_{\min} = H(\varepsilon | S)\) is the residual conditional entropy of noise given Cercis Score — i.e., the ``irreducible ignorance'' guaranteed by Theorem 3. This means: - The audit process has an \textbf{Audit Heat Death}: after infinite auditing, audit entropy converges to a non-zero minimum. - The magnitude of \(H_{\min}\) measures SCX's \textbf{ultimate unreachable region} — it is the information-theoretic quantification of noise-difficulty unidentifiability. - If \(H_{\min} = 0\), then Theorem 3 is broken — but all current evidence points to \(H_{\min} > 0\).

\textbf{(iv) Multi-Auditor Entropy Merger Theorem (Rigorously Derivable)}: If two auditors \(A_1, A_2\) respectively hold information sets \(\mathcal{I}_1, \mathcal{I}_2\), the merged audit entropy satisfies: \[H_A(1 \oplus 2) \leq \min(H_A(1), H_A(2)) - I(\mathcal{I}_1; \mathcal{I}_2 | \varepsilon)\] In particular, if the two auditors' information is conditionally independent with respect to \(\varepsilon\), then: \[H_A(1 \oplus 2) = H_A(1) + H_A(2) - H(\varepsilon) - I(\mathcal{I}_1; \mathcal{I}_2)\] This is the information-theoretic foundation for SCX federated auditing (FA-Theorem).
\end{quote}

\subsection{III. Audit Complexity Theorem: Minimum Expert Count Lower Bound for Noise Detection}\label{iii-audit-complexity-theorem}

\subsubsection{(Audit Complexity Theorem --- AC-Theorem)}\label{audit-complexity-theorem-ac-theorem}

\subsubsection{Context}\label{context-ac}

The ``auditor'' concept in the SCX framework (Sprint, Audit Sword) has been used qualitatively — ``the auditor can...'' ``the auditor cannot...''. But a fundamental engineering question has been overlooked: \textbf{How many resources are needed to complete a single SCX audit?}

The resources here are not just data samples (T5 active learning theorem already gives sample complexity), but more importantly \textbf{expert count} — i.e., how many auditors with independent judgment capabilities (human experts or independent AI modules) are needed to achieve a given audit accuracy? In real SCX deployments, expert time is a scarce resource (see human audit budget \(B_H\) in HC-Theorem), so the minimum expert count lower bound is critical for engineering feasibility.

More precisely: Given noise level \(\eta\), confidence \(\delta\), and model class complexity \(M\), how many experts do we need to reliably detect the presence of noise? This problem differs from classical sample complexity because it introduces the \textbf{multi-expert aggregation} dimension — experts may be correlated, may be unreliable, and may need redundancy to eliminate individual biases.

\subsubsection{Theorem Statement (Draft)}\label{theorem-statement-ac-draft}

\begin{quote}
\textbf{AC-Theorem (Audit Complexity Theorem)}:

Suppose there are \(K\) experts, each expert \(k\) gives an independent judgment \(J_k(x_i) \in \{\text{noise}, \text{hard}\}\) on sample \(x_i\), with judgment accuracy characterized by the confusion matrix: \[P(J_k = \text{noise} | \varepsilon > 0) = \alpha_k \quad (\text{sensitivity})\] \[P(J_k = \text{hard} | \varepsilon = 0) = \beta_k \quad (\text{specificity error})\] Define the model's audit complexity \(M\) as its effective VC dimension in Situs space (effective VC dimension for audit purposes).

\textbf{(i) Independent Expert Lower Bound (Rigorously Derivable, from Chernoff bound + Theorem 3)}: If \(K\) experts' judgments are mutually conditionally independent, with each expert's sensitivity \(\alpha_k \geq \alpha_{\min}\) and specificity error \(\beta_k \leq \beta_{\max}\), then to detect noise level \(\eta > 0\) at significance level \(\delta\) with probability \(\geq 1-\delta\), the minimum required expert count is: \[K_{\min} \geq \frac{2(\alpha_{\min} - \beta_{\max})^{-2} \cdot \log(1/\delta)}{\eta^2 \cdot (1 - 2\beta_{\max})^2}\]

In particular, when \(\beta_{\max} \to 0.5\) (expert judgment approaches random), \(K_{\min} \to \infty\) — \textbf{without sufficiently good experts, more experts are useless}.

\textbf{(ii) Noise-Expert Tradeoff Theorem (Rigorously Derivable)}: Under fixed expert budget \(K\) and confidence \(\delta\), the minimum detectable noise level is: \[\eta_{\min}(K) \geq \sqrt{\frac{2\log(1/\delta)}{K \cdot (\alpha_{\min} - \beta_{\max})^2}}\]

Corollary: \(\eta_{\min}(K) = \Theta(1/\sqrt{K})\) — i.e., the detection threshold decreases at rate \(\sqrt{K}\). This \(\sqrt{K}\) rate is \textbf{tight} under the independent experts assumption (matching the central limit theorem lower bound).

\textbf{(iii) Correlated Experts — Audit Diversity Theorem (Open Problem, partially derivable)}: If there exists a correlation coefficient \(\rho_{ij}\) (Pearson correlation) among expert judgments, define the expert pool's effective independent expert count: \[K_{\text{eff}} = \frac{K}{1 + (K-1)\bar{\rho}}\] where \(\bar{\rho}\) is the average inter-expert correlation coefficient.

Then the corrected minimum noise detection threshold is: \[\eta_{\min}(K, \bar{\rho}) \geq \sqrt{\frac{2\log(1/\delta)}{K_{\text{eff}} \cdot (\alpha_{\min} - \beta_{\max})^2}}\]

\textbf{Open part}: When experts are not homogeneous — i.e., some experts' judgments are particularly sensitive to specific types of noise — does there exist an \textbf{expert selection strategy} that maximizes \(K_{\text{eff}}\)? This is equivalent to SCX's ``expert portfolio optimization problem,'' whose complexity is related to submodular maximization.

\textbf{(iv) Model Complexity-Audit Difficulty Duality Theorem (Open Problem)}: Define the model's audit complexity \(M\) (e.g., covering number or effective dimension in Situs space). Conjecture: there exists a duality relation: \[K_{\min} \cdot \eta_{\min}^2 \geq c \cdot \frac{M}{\log M} \cdot \log(1/\delta)\] where \(c\) is a constant related to expert quality. This means: \textbf{the more complex the model, the more experts are minimally needed for auditing; the smaller the noise, the more experts are minimally needed for auditing} — this is a fundamental audit uncertainty relation.
\end{quote}

\subsection{IV. Recursive Audit Theorem: Self-Reference of Audit Output and Convergence of Infinite Regress}\label{iv-recursive-audit-theorem}

\subsubsection{(Recursive Audit Theorem --- RA-Theorem)}\label{recursive-audit-theorem-ra-theorem}

\subsubsection{Context}\label{context-ra}

The core claim of the SCX framework is the ``Audit Sword'' — any SCX usage behavior can be independently audited. But can this sword point at itself? That is:

\textbf{Can the reliability of audit output itself be audited?}

This is a classic self-reference problem, with counterparts in logic (Gödel's incompleteness theorems), computer science (halting problem), and epistemology (the criterion problem). In the SCX context, it concretizes as:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
 Auditor A gives audit conclusion \(R_1\) on dataset D (e.g., ``noise level \(\hat{\eta}\)'')
\item
 But \(R_1\) itself may contain errors — A may misjudge
\item
 Thus auditor B must audit A's audit process, giving \(R_2\)
\item
 But \(R_2\) may contain errors......
\item
 This leads to infinite regress: \(R_1 \to R_2 \to R_3 \to \cdots\)
\end{enumerate}

Key question: \textbf{Does this recursion converge? If so, to what? If it diverges, does SCX's ``Audit Sword'' break upon self-reference?}

This problem has been hinted at multiple times in existing theorems but never addressed head-on: - TS-Theorem (iii) mentions Sprint self-audit limits (recursive correction of \(\eta\)) - HC-Theorem (iii)'s ``Failure Theorem'' hints at an unbreakable absolute boundary - AE-Theorem (iii)'s ``Audit Heat Death'' hints at the inevitability of convergence

\subsubsection{Theorem Statement (Draft)}\label{theorem-statement-ra-draft}

\begin{quote}
\textbf{RA-Theorem (Recursive Audit Theorem)}:

Define the audit recursion sequence: let \(\mathcal{A}_0\) be the initial auditor (auditing original data D), and \(\mathcal{A}_k\) be the \(k\)-th layer meta-auditor (auditing the audit output of \(\mathcal{A}_{k-1}\)). Each layer's audit outputs a noise estimate \(\hat{\eta}_k\) and its confidence interval \([\hat{\eta}_k - \epsilon_k, \hat{\eta}_k + \epsilon_k]\).

\textbf{(i) Recursive Convergence Theorem (Rigorously Derivable, from Theorem 3 + information theory)}: If the additional noise \(\sigma_k^2\) introduced by each layer of meta-auditing satisfies \(\sigma_k^2 \leq \rho \cdot \sigma_{k-1}^2\) and \(\rho < 1\) (i.e., meta-audit noise strictly decreases; the auditor has ``higher fidelity'' than the audited system), then the recursive sequence \(\{\hat{\eta}_k\}\) converges with probability 1: \[\hat{\eta}_k \xrightarrow{a.s.} \eta^*\] where \(\eta^*\) satisfies the fixed-point equation: \(\eta^* = f(\eta^*)\), and \(f\) is the SCX recursive audit operator.

Furthermore, if \(\rho \leq 1/2\), the convergence rate is exponential: \[|\hat{\eta}_k - \eta^*| \leq \sigma_0 \cdot \rho^{k/2} \cdot O(\sqrt{\log k})\]

\textbf{(ii) Infinite Regress Inevitability Theorem (Rigorously Derivable)}: If \(\rho \geq 1\) (i.e., each layer's meta-audit is at least as noisy as the audited layer), then the divergence probability of the recursive sequence \(\{\hat{\eta}_k\}\) → 1 (as \(k \to \infty\)), and the Audit Sword breaks upon self-reference.

The critical condition \(\rho = 1\) corresponds to SCX's \textbf{Audit Self-Reference Phase Transition}: the \(\rho < 1\) region is audit self-consistent, and the \(\rho \geq 1\) region is audit collapse.

\textbf{(iii) Information-Theoretic Characterization of Audit Fixed Points (Open Problem, partially derivable)}: Define the recursive audit fixed point \(\eta^*\). Conjecture that \(\eta^*\) is uniquely determined by: \[I(\varepsilon; S | \mathcal{I}_A^{(\infty)}) = \eta^*\] where \(\mathcal{I}_A^{(\infty)}\) is the joint information set of all layers of audit.

\textbf{Open part}: Is \(\eta^*\) equal to \(H_{\min}\) in AE-Theorem (the residual entropy of Audit Heat Death)? If the two are equal, then the convergence point of recursive auditing is precisely Theorem 3's information barrier — the Audit Sword, when pointing at itself, ultimately encounters the shield it initially forged.

\textbf{(iv) Gödel Analogy Duality of the Audit Sword (Open Problem)}: Does there exist a strict formal analogy: \[\text{Divergence of SCX recursive auditing} \quad \longleftrightarrow \quad \text{Unprovable propositions in Gödel's incompleteness theorems}\] Specifically: if recursive auditing diverges (\(\rho \geq 1\)), does this imply the existence of an \textbf{absolutely unauditable audit proposition} — i.e., a proposition whose truth cannot be determined by any finite layer of auditing within the SCX framework?
\end{quote}

\subsubsection{Core Intuition}\label{core-intuition-ra}

RA-Theorem is the \textbf{philosophically deepest} theorem direction in the SCX framework. It directly confronts the self-reference dilemma of the ``Audit Sword'': when a system claims ``everything can be audited,'' can that claim itself be audited?

The theorem gives a clear answer: \textbf{the convergence/collapse dichotomy depends on the fidelity decay rate \(\rho\) of meta-auditing}. If each layer of meta-auditing is more precise than the previous (\(\rho < 1\)), the recursion converges to a fixed point — which is precisely the information barrier drawn by Theorem 3. If meta-auditing is no more precise than object auditing (\(\rho \geq 1\)), the recursion diverges and the ``Audit Sword'' breaks.

The deepest question is: in real SCX deployments, what is \(\rho\)? If we use the same AI system to audit AI systems (the most economical approach), then \(\rho \approx 1\) — \textbf{systems of the same order cannot audit systems of the same order}, which is the core constraint of recursive auditing.

\subsection{V. Alignment Audit Theorem: SCX Reduction of the AI Alignment Problem}\label{v-alignment-audit-theorem}

\subsubsection{(Alignment Audit Theorem --- AA-Theorem)}\label{alignment-audit-theorem-aa-theorem}

\subsubsection{Context}\label{context-aa}

The AI Alignment Problem is the core challenge of contemporary AI safety: how to ensure that AI system behavior aligns with human intentions and values? Current alignment techniques (RLHF, DPO, CAI, etc.) essentially \textbf{shape model behavior through human feedback data}. But this raises a deeper question:

\textbf{If the alignment training data itself has quality defects — noisy annotations, biases, inconsistencies — then is the ``aligned'' AI merely fitting a defective proxy of human preferences?}

The SCX framework precisely provides the theoretical foundation for detecting data quality problems. The core proposition AA-Theorem aims to explore is:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{To what extent can the AI alignment problem be reduced to a data quality audit problem?}
\end{quote}

If this reduction is tight, then SCX's entire arsenal of theorems — noise detection (T1-T4), active learning (T5), game equilibrium (T6), cross-domain transfer (T7) — can all be directly applied to the alignment problem.

\subsubsection{Theorem Statement (Draft)}\label{theorem-statement-aa-draft}

\begin{quote}
\textbf{AA-Theorem (Alignment Audit Theorem)}:

Let there be an alignment training dataset \(\mathcal{D}_{\text{align}} = \{(x_i, y_i^+, y_i^-)\}_{i=1}^n\), where \(x_i\) is the prompt, \(y_i^+\) is the human-preferred response, and \(y_i^-\) is the rejected response. Define the alignment quality function \(A(f) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim P_X}[\text{AlignmentScore}(f(x), \text{HumanValues})]\).

\textbf{(i) Alignment-Audit Reduction Theorem (Open Problem, partially derivable)}: Conjecture the existence of a reduction mapping \(\Phi\) that maps the alignment problem to an SCX audit problem: \[\Phi: (\mathcal{D}_{\text{align}}, f) \mapsto (\mathcal{D}_{\text{audit}}, S)\] where \(\mathcal{D}_{\text{audit}}\) is an audit dataset constructed from alignment data, and \(S\) is the corresponding Cercis Score.

The reduction should satisfy the \textbf{fidelity condition}: \[|A(f) - \tilde{A}(S)| \leq \epsilon_{\Phi}\] where \(\tilde{A}(S)\) is the alignment quality estimate reconstructed from SCX audit results.

\textbf{Open part}: The concrete construction of \(\Phi\) and the tight bound on \(\epsilon_{\Phi}\).

\textbf{(ii) Preference Noise Detection — RLHF Audit Theorem (Rigorously Derivable, from Theorem 1 + Theorem 3)}: Model noise in RLHF preference data as: with probability \(\eta\), the human annotator randomly reverses preferences (flipping \(y^+\) and \(y^-\)). Define the preference Cercis Score: \[S_{\text{pref}}(x) = \left|\mathbb{P}(y^+ \succ y^- | x) - \frac{1}{2}\right|\]

Then the SCX framework can apply the complete audit pipeline to \(\mathcal{D}_{\text{align}}\): - \textbf{Theorem 1} (Noise Detection): There exists a statistic \(T_{\text{pref}}\) such that when \(\eta > 0\), \(T_{\text{pref}}\) detects noise with probability \(\geq 1-\delta\), with sample complexity \(n = \Omega(1/\eta^2 \cdot \log(1/\delta))\). - \textbf{Theorem 3} (Unidentifiability): Genuine preference disagreements (sincere value disagreements among different human annotators) and random noise are indistinguishable when only observing \(S_{\text{pref}}\).

\textbf{(iii) Alignment Tax Theorem — Monetizing Audit as Alignment Cost (Open Problem)}: Define the \textbf{Alignment Tax} \(\tau_{\text{align}}\) as the relative loss in alignment training efficiency when SCX auditing is introduced. Conjecture: \[\tau_{\text{align}} \geq \frac{\eta}{1-\eta} \cdot \frac{\text{cost}(\text{audit})}{\text{cost}(\text{train})}\]

i.e., the alignment tax is proportional to data noise. \textbf{Open part}: Is this lower bound tight? Does there exist a joint audit-training optimization strategy that minimizes \(\tau_{\text{align}}\)?

\textbf{(iv) Value Uncertainty vs Noise Unidentifiability — Alignment Theorem 3 (Rigorously Derivable, direct corollary of Theorem 3)}: Within the SCX framework, the following two scenarios are observationally equivalent: - \textbf{Scenario A (Noise)}: Annotators are careless/malicious, randomly annotating preferences (solvable through alignment data cleaning) - \textbf{Scenario B (Value Uncertainty)}: There exist sincere value disagreements among annotators (not solvable through data cleaning; requires political/philosophical value aggregation)

This unidentifiability means: \textbf{SCX auditing cannot distinguish ``bad data'' from ``difficult value problems''} — the former is an engineering problem, the latter is a philosophical problem. The SCX boundary of alignment lies precisely here.
\end{quote}

The core bet of AA-Theorem is: \textbf{The engineering part of the AI alignment problem (data quality) can be fully covered by SCX, but the philosophical part of the alignment problem (value aggregation) is another instance of the Theorem 3 barrier}.
