\subsection{I. Causal Discovery Theorem: Separability of Label Noise vs. Causal Confounding}\label{i-causal-discovery-theorem}

\subsubsection{(Causal Discovery Theorem --- CD-Theorem)}\label{causal-discovery-theorem-cd-theorem}

\subsubsection{Context}\label{context-cd}

Theorem 3 (Uncertainty Principle) asserts: within the SCX audit framework, \textbf{noise and intrinsic difficulty are indistinguishable}. Formally, given Cercis Score S = Q + ηN, an auditor cannot decompose the quality component Q and noise component N from observed S values alone — there is an irreducible information barrier.

But Theorem 3 left one critical gap unclosed: \textbf{Are label noise (measurement noise, annotation noise) and unobserved confounders also indistinguishable?} This is a deeper question. Label noise is random perturbation of the supervision signal; causal confounding is spurious correlation caused by unobserved common causes. The two may exhibit identical statistical signatures in the observed distribution, but their intervention implications are fundamentally different — the former can be mitigated through data cleaning, while the latter must be resolved through causal identification (do-calculus, instrumental variables, etc.).

\subsubsection{Theorem Statement (Draft)}\label{theorem-statement-cd-draft}

\begin{quote}
\textbf{CD-Theorem (Causal Separability Theorem)}: Let D = \{(xᵢ, yᵢ)\} be the audit dataset, where yᵢ = f*(xᵢ) + εᵢ + β·zᵢ, with εᵢ being independent label noise, zᵢ being an unobserved confounder, and β being the confounding strength vector. Define the auditor's accessible information set as I\_A = \{S(x), ∇S(x), H\_S(x)\} (Cercis Score and its first and second-order gradients).

\textbf{(i) Weak Separability (Open Problem)}: Using only I\_A, there exists a test statistic τ(I\_A) such that when β ≠ 0, the power function of τ strictly exceeds the significance level under the pure label noise case, i.e.: P(τ \textgreater{} c\_α | β ≠ 0) \textgreater{} α for some β ≠ 0 and bounded ε. Whether this conclusion holds depends on whether η (Sprint noise parameter) has non-zero mutual information with z: I(η; z) \textgreater{} 0.

\textbf{(ii) Strong Inseparability (Rigorously Derivable, direct corollary of Theorem 3)}: If η and z are independent (i.e., I(η; z) = 0), then label noise and causal confounding are \textbf{absolutely indistinguishable} within the SCX framework. No auditor can construct any classifier better than random guessing to distinguish ε-dominated samples from z-dominated samples.

\textbf{(iii) Relay Theorem (Rigorously Derivable, from T7)}: If there exists a source domain D\_s satisfying the front-door criterion, and T7 cross-domain transfer ε-fidelity conditions hold, then in the target domain D\_t, the Cercis deviation ‖ΔS‖ of confounding effect β has an upper bound: ‖ΔS‖ ≤ g(ε, δ) + O(1/√n), where δ is the Situs distance measuring source-target distribution divergence.
\end{quote}

\subsection{II. Federated Audit Theorem: Zero-Knowledge Multi-Party Audit Completeness}\label{ii-federated-audit-theorem}

\subsubsection{(Federated Audit Theorem --- FA-Theorem)}\label{federated-audit-theorem-fa-theorem}

\subsubsection{Context}\label{context-fa}

The ``Audit Sword'' establishes SCX's core power: \textbf{any SCX usage behavior can be independently audited}. But this sword currently assumes the auditor has full data access. In federated scenarios — where multiple parties hold private data and interact only through gradients or summary statistics — the Audit Sword encounters a joint lock: how do you audit a dataset you cannot see?

Federated learning already has tools like Secure Aggregation, Differential Privacy (DP), and Multi-Party Computation (MPC). But they solve ``preventing leakage,'' not ``enabling auditability.'' SCX's goal is the inverse: we want to \textbf{make audit conclusions have the same statistical power as full-data audits, without exposing raw data}.

\subsubsection{Theorem Statement (Draft)}\label{theorem-statement-fa-draft}

\begin{quote}
\textbf{FA-Theorem (Federated Audit Theorem)}: Let there be K data parties, each holding a private dataset D\_k, k = 1,\ldots,K. The federated aggregator outputs a global model M\_Π via protocol Π. The auditor A's accessible information set is: I\_Fed = \{S\_k, ∇S\_k, H\_{S,k}\}\_{k=1}\^{}K ∪ \{M\_Π\}, where S\_k are each party's Cercis Scores (computable locally, then encrypted for sharing).

\textbf{(i) Federated Audit Completeness (Open Problem, partially derivable)}: Define the full-information audit statistic T\_full = T(D\_1,\ldots,D\_K) and the federated audit statistic T\_fed = T\_F(I\_Fed). If protocol Π satisfies: (a) each party's submitted S\_k is zero-knowledge proven honest (i.e., ∃ π\_ZK : Verify(π\_ZK, S\_k, Commit(D\_k)) = 1); (b) Situs encodings are homomorphic across parties: Situs(∪D\_k) ≅ ⊕\_k Situs(D\_k) (⊕ being some commutative composition operator), then an ε-audit equivalence exists: |Power(T\_fed) - Power(T\_full)| ≤ ε(K, δ), where δ is each party's differential privacy budget (if DP not used, δ = 0 and ε → 0 as n → ∞).

\textbf{(ii) Information Lower Bound (Rigorously Derivable, from Theorem 3)}: If ∃ k such that the label noise parameter η\_k of D\_k is completely unknown to the auditor (i.e., no π\_ZK verification), then for any federated audit statistic T\_fed, there exists a confounding strategy such that: sup\_{D} |E{[}T\_fed{]} - E{[}T\_full{]}| ≥ const · η\_k · |D\_k|/|D\_total|, i.e., audit power loss is proportional to the proportion of unverified noise in total data.

\textbf{(iii) Telegraph Commons Extension (Open Problem)}: If the ``Telegraph Commons'' (data storage as infrastructure) extends to the federated layer, i.e., a decentralized storage layer commits Situs hashes of all D\_k, then the π\_ZK requirement in FA-Theorem (i) can be replaced by Proof of Storage, further tightening the ε upper bound.
\end{quote}

\subsection{III. Adversarial Robustness Theorem: Is Adversarial Perturbation a Special Case of ``Difficulty'' or a Third Category?}\label{iii-adversarial-robustness-theorem}

\subsubsection{(Adversarial Robustness Theorem --- AR-Theorem)}\label{adversarial-robustness-theorem-ar-theorem}

\subsubsection{Context}\label{context-ar}

Theorem 3 established a binary unidentifiability framework of noise vs difficulty. But the discovery of adversarial examples creates trouble for this binary framework: adversarial perturbation is not random noise — it is the result of directed optimization targeting model gradients; nor is it ``natural difficulty'' — humans effortlessly correctly classify adversarial examples. This raises a fundamental question:

\textbf{In SCX's Cercis taxonomy, does ``adversariality'' exist as a third fundamental category alongside Q (quality) and N (noise), i.e., S = Q + ηN + γA?}

\subsubsection{Theorem Statement (Draft)}\label{theorem-statement-ar-draft}

\begin{quote}
\textbf{AR-Theorem (Adversarial Robustness Theorem)}: Let adversarial perturbation δ\_adv = argmax\_{\{‖δ‖≤ε\}} L(f(x+δ), y), with ‖δ\_adv‖ measured by Situs distance within the SCX audit framework. Define Adversarial Exposure A(x) = ‖∇\_x S(x)‖ · ε / S(x).

\textbf{(i) Reduction Theorem (Open Problem, partially derivable)}: If model f's Cercis gradient field ∇S satisfies the Lipschitz condition in the Situs neighborhood of x and the Lipschitz constant L \textless{} S(x)/ε, then adversarial perturbation can be absorbed into the ``noise'' component of Theorem 3: A(x) ≤ η\_eff(x), where η\_eff = η + L·ε/S(x) (effective noise rate). Conversely, if L ≥ S(x)/ε, then adversarial perturbation constitutes a \textbf{third-type singularity} that cannot be absorbed by Theorem 3.

\textbf{(ii) Audit Detectability (Rigorously Derivable, from T5 Active Learning Theorem)}: Define the auditor's adversarial detection statistic: D\_adv(x) = ‖∇S(x)‖ / Var\_{\{x'∈N(x)\}}{[}S(x'){]}, where N(x) is the Situs local neighborhood. If γ \textgreater{} 0 (i.e., a third-type adversarial component exists), then when the auditor adopts T5 active learning strategy to maximize I(S; D\_adv), the detection sample complexity is: n\_detect ≥ Ω(1/γ² · log(1/δ)), and this lower bound is tight for the optimal active learning strategy.

\textbf{(iii) Phase Transition Theorem (Open Problem)}: There exists a critical Cercis threshold S\_crit such that: - When S(x) \textgreater{} S\_crit: adversarial perturbation is a special case of ``difficulty'' (reduced to Theorem 3 framework), - When S(x) ≤ S\_crit: adversarial perturbation manifests as a third category, and the A component is orthogonal to the N component in ∇S space. The analytic form of S\_crit and its relationship to model architecture are open.
\end{quote}

\subsection{IV. Temporal SCX Theorem: Audit Drift under Non-Stationary Distributions}\label{iv-temporal-scx-theorem}

\subsubsection{(Temporal SCX Theorem --- TS-Theorem)}\label{temporal-scx-theorem-ts-theorem}

\subsubsection{Context}\label{context-ts}

The static SCX framework assumes a fixed data distribution P(X,Y). Spring SE-1 introduced dynamic evolution (model update → data distribution drift → SCX parameter η drift), but SE-1 currently only handles the scenario of ``model update as exogenous shock.'' Real-world distribution drift is more complex: - \textbf{Concept drift}: P(Y|X) changes; - \textbf{Covariate shift}: P(X) changes; - \textbf{Label shift}: P(Y) changes.

More critically, \textbf{Sprint's core parameter η (noise-difficulty coupling coefficient) itself drifts over time} — this is the ``audit of the audit'' problem: who audits Sprint itself?

\subsubsection{Theorem Statement (Draft)}\label{theorem-statement-ts-draft}

\begin{quote}
\textbf{TS-Theorem (Temporal SCX Theorem)}: Let time index t = 0,1,\ldots,T. At each time t, define data distribution P\_t(X,Y), corresponding Cercis Score S\_t, Sprint parameter η\_t, and Situs encoding U\_t = Situs(P\_t). Assume η\_t evolves as: η\_{t+1} = η\_t + Δη(P\_t, M\_t) + ξ\_t, where Δη is deterministic drift (jointly determined by model M\_t and data distribution P\_t), and ξ\_t is random shock (mean 0, variance σ²\_ξ).

\textbf{(i) Drift Detectability (Rigorously Derivable, from T7 Cross-Domain Transfer)}: If the Situs distance between two time points d\_Situs(U\_t, U\_{t+1}) ≥ δ\_min, then at significance level α, the auditor can detect η drift with probability ≥ 1-β, requiring sample sizes satisfying: n\_t + n\_{t+1} ≥ 2(σ²\_ξ + κ·δ²\_min)·(z\_{α/2} + z\_β)² / δ²\_min, where κ is the Lipschitz constant from Situs distance to η drift (inherited from T7).

\textbf{(ii) Drift Attribution (Open Problem)}: Once η drift is detected, can it be decomposed into: Δη = Δη\_concept + Δη\_covariate + Δη\_label + Δη\_model, i.e., attributed to the individual contributions of concept drift, covariate shift, label shift, and model update? Without additional assumptions (such as a partially observable validation set), this decomposition is \textbf{unidentifiable} within the SCX framework. However, if there exists an anchor set A ⊂ X satisfying P\_t(Y|X∈A) ≈ P\_{t+1}(Y|X∈A) (concept-stable anchors), then Δη\_concept can be independently estimated.

\textbf{(iii) Sprint Self-Audit Limit Theorem (Open Problem, partially derivable)}: Can Sprint SE-1's self-correction mechanism (η → η' via model retraining) always pull η back to equilibrium? Define the Lyapunov function V(η) = (η - η*)\^{}2, where η* is the optimal noise parameter. If there exists a compact set Ω ⊂ {[}0,1{]} such that for η\_t ∈ Ω, we have: E{[}V(η\_{t+1}) | η\_t{]} ≤ ρ·V(η\_t), ρ \textless{} 1, then the Sprint process is geometrically ergodic and η\_t converges to η*. But if the distribution drift rate v\_drift satisfies v\_drift \textgreater{} (1-ρ)/Δt, then Sprint diverges — self-audit capability has a \textbf{rate upper bound}.
\end{quote}

\subsection{V. Human-AI Collaborative Audit Theorem: The Cognitive Boundary of Theorem 3}\label{v-human-ai-collaborative-audit-theorem}

\subsubsection{(Human-AI Collaborative Audit Theorem --- HC-Theorem)}\label{human-ai-collaborative-audit-theorem-hc-theorem}

\subsubsection{Context}\label{context-hc}

Theorem 3's core claim is: noise and difficulty are indistinguishable within SCX's automated audit framework. But the two most critical words in this sentence are: \textbf{``within the framework''}. The operational boundary of the audit framework depends on what information processor we equip the auditor with. If the auditor is merely an automated system based on Cercis Score, then Theorem 3 is ironclad. But what if the auditor can be a \textbf{hybrid of human expert + AI system}?

What do humans possess that AI auditors do not? - \textbf{Prior causal knowledge} (non-statistical knowledge about how the world operates); - \textbf{Counterfactual reasoning ability} (``if the annotator were more careful, would this sample's label change?''); - \textbf{Metacognitive judgment} (``I'm not even sure about this sample myself, so it may genuinely be hard'').

Theorem 3's boundary is precisely the line where human cognition can intervene and break the noise-difficulty symmetry.

\subsubsection{Theorem Statement (Draft)}\label{theorem-statement-hc-draft}

\begin{quote}
\textbf{HC-Theorem (Human-AI Collaborative Audit Theorem)}: Define the audit system as A = (AI, H, Π), where AI is the SCX-based automated audit module, H is the human auditor, and Π is the human-AI interaction protocol. Define the human audit budget B\_H (maximum samples a human can review), and the human judgment function J\_H: X → \{noise, hard, uncertain\}.

\textbf{(i) Collaborative Gain Theorem (Open Problem, directional conclusion)}: If the following two conditions are met: (a) \textbf{Information Asymmetry Condition}: there exists a sample subset X\_H ⊂ X such that human judgment J\_H(x) and Cercis Score S(x) satisfy in conditional distribution: P(J\_H=noise | S=s) ≠ P(J\_H=hard | S=s) for some s (i.e., human judgment carries additional information beyond S); (b) \textbf{Calibration Condition}: human ``uncertain'' judgments are themselves calibrated, i.e., E{[}J\_H=uncertain | S=s{]} is monotone increasing as s → 0, then the noise-difficulty separation power of human-AI collaborative auditing Ω\_collab strictly outperforms pure AI auditing: Ω\_collab \textgreater{} Ω\_AI = 0 (under Theorem 3).

\textbf{(ii) Budget Decay Law (Rigorously Derivable, from Theorem 3)}: When the human audit budget B\_H → 0, collaborative auditing degenerates to pure AI auditing, with convergence rate: Ω\_collab(B\_H) = Ω\_AI + c·√(B\_H/n)·Δ\_I(S; J\_H) + o(√(B\_H/n)), where Δ\_I is the information increment between human judgment and Cercis Score (measured by mutual information).

\textbf{(iii) Failure Theorem — Theorem 3's Absolute Boundary (Rigorously Derivable)}: Even with arbitrary human audit capability introduced, noise and difficulty remain \textbf{absolutely indistinguishable} under the following condition: If and only if ∀x, I(J\_H(x); ε(x) | S(x)) = 0, i.e., human judgment and label noise carry absolutely no mutual information given S.

Equivalently, the necessary and sufficient condition for humans to break Theorem 3 is: there exists at least one sample whose label noise statistical signature leaves traces in the human cognitive representation beyond S. If the noise is ``perfect noise'' (uncorrelated with any observable features, including human prior knowledge), then Theorem 3's barrier holds for humans as well.
\end{quote}
