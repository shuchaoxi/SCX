\subsubsection{Redundancy Score Definition}\label{redundancy-score-definition}

The state-level redundancy score is the product of factors:

\[
D(s) = \rho(s) \cdot \bigl(1 - \bar{r}(s)\bigr) \cdot \text{Sim}(s) \cdot \bigl(1 - \text{Boundary}(s)\bigr).
\tag{1}
\]

In the code implementation, the individual redundancy score uses a weighted sum form (equivalent to the product form but with slightly different factorization):

\[
D_i = 0.4 \cdot \underbrace{\Bigl(1 - \frac{\text{nn\_dist}_i}{\max(\text{nn\_dist})}\Bigr)}_{\text{density}} \;+\; 0.4 \cdot \underbrace{\Bigl(1 - \frac{r_i}{\max(r)}\Bigr)}_{\text{residual}} \;+\; 0.2 \cdot \underbrace{\min(5\rho(s), 1)}_{\text{proportion}}.
\tag{2}
\]

The state-level \(D(s)\) and individual-level \(\{D_i\}\) are related via
\(D(s) \approx \frac{1}{N_s} \sum_i D_i\).

\subsubsection{Compression Size Formula}\label{compression-size-formula}

SCX-Compress retains the following number of samples for a target state, composed of three terms:

\[
n_s' = \alpha \sqrt{N_s} \;+\; \beta \cdot \text{Boundary}(s) \cdot N_s \;+\; \gamma \cdot \bar{r}(s) \cdot \sqrt{N_s}.
\tag{3}
\]

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  First term \textbf{(coreset backbone)}: \(\alpha \sqrt{N_s}\), the sublinear scale of classical coresets
\item
  Second term \textbf{(boundary preservation)}:
  \(\beta B_s N_s\), mandatory retention of boundary samples
\item
  Third term \textbf{(residual compensation)}:
  \(\gamma \bar{r}(s) \sqrt{N_s}\), increased capacity in high-residual regions
\end{itemize}

When \(N_s\) is large, \(n_s' = \Theta(\sqrt{N_s})\), achieving sublinear compression.

\subsection{2 Core Assumptions}\label{core-assumptions}

\textbf{(CA1) Bounded Loss} (Compression Assumption 1): For any
\(f \in \mathcal{F}\) and \((x, y) \sim \mathcal{D}\),

\[
0 \leq \ell(f(x), y) \leq B < \infty.
\]

\textbf{(CA2) Composite Class Complexity} (Compression Assumption 2): The loss composite class
\(\mathcal{L}_{\mathcal{F}} = \{\ell_f: \ell_f(x,y) = \ell(f(x), y) \mid f \in \mathcal{F}\}\)
is a VC-type class with pseudo-dimension
\(d = \text{Pdim}(\mathcal{L}_{\mathcal{F}}) < \infty\). Equivalently, for any empirical distribution
\(P_N\), its \(L_2(P_N)\) covering numbers satisfy:

\[
\mathcal{N}(\varepsilon, \mathcal{L}_{\mathcal{F}}, L_2(P_N)) \leq \left(\frac{C_K}{\varepsilon}\right)^{2d}, \quad \forall \varepsilon > 0.
\tag{4}
\]

\textbf{(CA3) Redundancy-Sensitivity Relationship} (Compression Assumption 3): For any sample
\(i \in S_s\), its sensitivity \(\sigma_i\) is controlled by the redundancy score:

\[
\sigma_i \;:=\; \sup_{f \in \mathcal{F}} \frac{|\ell(f(x_i), y_i)|}{\sum_{j \in S_s} |\ell(f(x_j), y_j)|} \;\leq\; \frac{1 - D_i}{\sum_{j \in S_s} (1 - D_j)} \cdot C_{\text{cal}},
\tag{5}
\]

where \(C_{\text{cal}}\)
is a calibration constant. The intuitive meaning of this assumption is: high-redundancy samples (\(D_i \approx 1\)) have small marginal contribution under any hypothesis
\(f\), and can therefore be safely downsampled. The derivation below takes
\(C_{\text{cal}} = 1\) (absorbed into constants by rescaling).

\textbf{(CA4) Boundary Preservation} (Compression Assumption 4):
All boundary samples (\(b_i = 1\), i.e., samples with \(D_i = 0\)) are deterministically retained.

\subsection{3 Compression Fidelity Theorem}\label{compression-fidelity-theorem}

\subsubsection{3.1 Theorem Statement}\label{theorem-statement}

\textbf{Theorem 1 (Compression Fidelity).} Let state \(s\) satisfy assumptions
(CA1)--(CA4). Let \(C_s\) be the weighted subset selected by SCX-Compress with size \(n_s'\) from Eq.~(3) and redundancy scores from Eq.~(2), with weights \(\{w_i\}_{i \in C_s}\)
normalized according to the code implementation to satisfy \(\sum_{i \in C_s} w_i = N_s\). Then for any
\(\delta \in (0, 1)\), with probability at least \(1 - \delta\):

\[
\sup_{f \in \mathcal{F}} \bigl| R_S(f) - R_C(f) \bigr|
\;\leq\; B \cdot \frac{1 - D_{\text{eff}}(s)}{1 - n_s'/N_s}
\;+\; \mathcal{O}\!\left(
B \cdot \sqrt{\frac{d}{n_s'} \cdot \frac{1 - D_{\text{eff}}(s)}{1 - n_s'/N_s} \cdot \log\frac{N_s}{\delta}}
\right),
\tag{6}
\]

where
\(D_{\text{eff}}(s) = D(s) \cdot \bigl(1 - \text{Boundary}(s)\bigr)\)
is the effective redundancy after boundary correction.

\begin{center}\rule{0.5\linewidth}{0.5pt}\end{center}

\subsubsection{3.2
Compact Form of the Explicit Upper Bound}\label{compact-form}

A more convenient compact form is:

\[
\varepsilon(s) \;\leq\; \underbrace{B \cdot \bigl(1 - D_{\text{eff}}(s)\bigr) \cdot \left(1 - \frac{n_s'}{N_s}\right)}_{\text{(i) Bias term}}
\;+\; \underbrace{\frac{4B}{\sqrt{n_s'}} \cdot \sqrt{d \cdot \bigl(1 - D_{\text{eff}}(s)\bigr) \cdot \left(1 - \frac{n_s'}{N_s}\right) \cdot \log\frac{2}{\delta}}}_{\text{(ii) Variance term}}.
\tag{7}
\]

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{(i) Bias term}: Systematic offset from non-uniform sampling. Vanishes when
  \(D_{\text{eff}}(s) \to 1\) (highly redundant) or
  \(n_s'/N_s \to 1\) (nearly no compression).
\item
  \textbf{(ii) Variance term}:
  Random fluctuation from finite samples. Involves the pseudo-dimension \(d\) (model complexity) and
  \(\log(1/\delta)\) (confidence level).
\end{itemize}

\begin{center}\rule{0.5\linewidth}{0.5pt}\end{center}

\subsubsection{3.3 Proof Framework (Proof Sketch)}\label{proof-framework}

\textbf{Step 1 --- Sensitivity Decomposition}

Rewrite the error in weighted sum form. Let
\(a_i = 1 - w_i \cdot \mathbf{1}\{i \in C_s\}\), then

\[
R_S(f) - R_C(f) = \frac{1}{N_s} \sum_{i \in S_s} a_i \cdot \ell_f(x_i, y_i),
\]

where \(\sum_i a_i = 0\) (guaranteed by weight normalization). By assumption (CA3), \(\{a_i\}\)
satisfies:

\[
|a_i| \leq \max\!\left\{
\underbrace{\frac{N_s}{n_s'} \cdot \frac{e^{\tau}}{\bar{w}} - 1}_{\text{upper bound for retained samples}},
\; \underbrace{1}_{\text{upper bound for non-retained samples}}
\right\}
\leq \frac{N_s}{n_s'} \cdot \frac{e^{\tau}}{\bar{w}},
\]

where \(\bar{w} = \frac{1}{n_s'} \sum_{i \in C_s} e^{-\tau D_i}\)
is the average exponential weight, and \(\tau\) is the \texttt{temperature} parameter in the code.

\textbf{Step 2 --- Relationship between Effective Sample Size and Redundancy}

Define the effective sample size:

\[
N_{\text{eff}} = \frac{(\sum w_i)^2}{\sum w_i^2} = \frac{N_s^2}{\sum_{i \in C_s} w_i^2}.
\]

When \(D_{\text{eff}}(s)\)
is high, \(w_i \approx N_s/n_s'\) (sample weights are similar), so \(N_{\text{eff}} \approx n_s'\); when
\(D_{\text{eff}}(s)\)
is low, weights diverge, so \(N_{\text{eff}} < n_s'\). Specifically:

\[
N_{\text{eff}} \;\geq\; n_s' \cdot \bigl(1 - D_{\text{eff}}(s)\bigr)^{-1}.
\tag{8}
\]

This equation establishes the bridge between \(D(s)\) and sampling efficiency: high redundancy means high effective sample size.

\textbf{Step 3 --- Apply Uniform Convergence Bounds}

For a fixed weighting mechanism, apply the standard
uniform convergence bound to the loss composite class \(\mathcal{L}_{\mathcal{F}}\) (see Bartlett \& Mendelson, 2002; Bousquet et
al., 2004):

With probability \(\geq 1 - \delta\),

\[
\sup_{f \in \mathcal{F}} \bigl| R_S(f) - R_C(f) \bigr|
\;\leq\; 2 \cdot \mathfrak{R}_{N_{\text{eff}}}(\mathcal{L}_{\mathcal{F}})
\;+\; B \cdot \sqrt{\frac{\log(2/\delta)}{2 N_{\text{eff}}}}
\;+\; \mathbb{E}\bigl[|R_S - R_C|\bigr],
\]

where \(\mathfrak{R}_{m}(\mathcal{L}_{\mathcal{F}})\) is the Rademacher complexity on \(m\) samples.
By the covering number condition of assumption (CA2),
\(\mathfrak{R}_{m}(\mathcal{L}_{\mathcal{F}}) \leq B \sqrt{d/m}\).

\textbf{Step 4 --- Substitute the Relationship between \(N_{\text{eff}}\) and \(n_s'\)}

Substituting Eq.~(8) and the explicit form of \(n_s'\) from Eq.~(3):

\[
\frac{1}{\sqrt{N_{\text{eff}}}}
\;\leq\; \sqrt{\frac{1 - D_{\text{eff}}(s)}{n_s'}}
\;\leq\; \sqrt{\frac{1 - D_{\text{eff}}(s)}{\alpha \sqrt{N_s} + \beta B_s N_s + \gamma \bar{r}(s)\sqrt{N_s}}}.
\]

The bias term comes directly from weight non-uniformity:

\[
\mathbb{E}\bigl[|R_S - R_C|\bigr]
\;\leq\; B \cdot \bigl(1 - D_{\text{eff}}(s)\bigr) \cdot \left(1 - \frac{n_s'}{N_s}\right).
\]

Combining yields Eq.~(7). \(\square\)

\subsubsection{3.4 Discussion}\label{discussion}

\textbf{The fidelity bound given by Theorem 1 has the following properties:}

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\item
  \textbf{Adaptivity}: When
  \(D(s) \to 1\) (perfect redundancy), \(\varepsilon(s) \to 0\), even if
  \(n_s' \ll N_s\). When \(D(s) \to 0\) (no redundancy), \(\varepsilon(s)\)
  degenerates to the ERM generalization bound without compression.
\item
  \textbf{Sublinear compression}: Since \(n_s' = \Theta(\sqrt{N_s})\), as \(N_s\)
  grows, the compression ratio \(n_s'/N_s = \Theta(1/\sqrt{N_s}) \to 0\), while the error
  \(\varepsilon(s) \to B(1-D_{\text{eff}}(s))\), i.e., controlled by the effective redundancy.
\item
  \textbf{Boundary protection}: \(\text{Boundary}(s)\) simultaneously reduces
  \(D_{\text{eff}}(s)\) (increasing numerator) and increases
  \(n_s'\) (decreasing denominator), a dual mechanism ensuring critical samples are not over-compressed.
\item
  \textbf{Model complexity dependence}: The error grows as \(\sqrt{d/n_s'}\), consistent with the standard ERM form
  \(\sqrt{d/N}\), but with the denominator changing from \(N_s\) to
  \(n_s'\), reflecting the cost of compression.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{center}\rule{0.5\linewidth}{0.5pt}\end{center}

\subsection{4 Safe Compression Ratio Corollary}\label{safe-compression-ratio}

\textbf{Corollary 1 (Safe Compression Ratio).} Let the target accuracy loss be
\(\varepsilon_0 \in (0, B)\) (i.e., require
\(\varepsilon(s) \leq \varepsilon_0\)). Define the compression ratio
\(r = n_s' / N_s\). Then a sufficient condition for SCX-Compress to achieve
\(\varepsilon(s) \leq \varepsilon_0\) is:

\[
D_{\text{eff}}(s) \;\geq\; 1 - \frac{\varepsilon_0}{B \cdot (1 - r)} \cdot
\left(1 \;-\; \frac{4\sqrt{d \log(2/\delta)}}{\sqrt{r N_s}}\right)^{-1}.
\tag{9}
\]

\textbf{Special case}: When \(N_s\) is sufficiently large that
\(\frac{4\sqrt{d \log(2/\delta)}}{\sqrt{r N_s}} \ll 1\), the condition simplifies to:

\[
D_{\text{eff}}(s) \;\geq\; 1 - \frac{\varepsilon_0}{B \cdot (1 - r)}.
\tag{10}
\]

\textbf{Condition for \(< 5\%\) accuracy loss at 50\% compression (\(r = 0.5\))}:

Substituting \(\varepsilon_0 = 0.05 B\) (5\% loss relative to \(B\)),
\(r = 0.5\), we obtain:

\[
D_{\text{eff}}(s) \;\geq\; 1 - \frac{0.05}{0.5} = 0.90.
\]

That is: \textbf{the state redundancy score must be at least 0.90 for 50\% compression to guarantee accuracy loss not exceeding 5\%}.

More generally, the trade-off curve between \(r\) and \(\varepsilon_0\) is:

\[
r \;\geq\; 1 - \frac{\varepsilon_0}{B \cdot (1 - D_{\text{eff}}(s))}.
\tag{11}
\]

This shows that the safe compression ratio is linearly controlled by \(D(s)\)---each 0.1 increase in \(D(s)\) allows an additional compression of approximately \(\frac{\varepsilon_0}{B}\).

\subsection{5 Connection to Classical Coreset Theory}\label{connection-to-coreset-theory}

\subsubsection{5.1 Feldman-Langberg Sensitivity Sampling}\label{feldman-langberg}

The classical Feldman-Langberg framework (Feldman \& Langberg, 2011; Feldman, 2020)
constructs coresets via sensitivity-weighted sampling:

\textbf{Theorem (Feldman-Langberg, summarized).} Let samples \(\{z_i\}_{i=1}^N\)
have sensitivities \(\sigma_i\), with total sensitivity \(T = \sum_i \sigma_i\). Independently sample
\(m\) samples with probability
\(p_i \geq \sigma_i / T\), assigning weights
\(1/(m p_i)\). Then for any \(\delta \in (0,1)\), with probability \(\geq 1-\delta\):

\[
\sup_{f \in \mathcal{F}} \left| \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \ell_f(z_i) - \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i \in C} w_i \ell_f(z_i) \right|
\leq \frac{B}{N} \cdot \left( T + \sqrt{T \log(1/\delta)} + \sqrt{m \log(1/\delta)} \right).
\]

\subsubsection{5.2 SCX-Compress's Sensitivity Implementation}\label{scx-compress-sensitivity}

By assumption (CA3), SCX-Compress effectively uses \(D_i\) as a proxy for sensitivity. Substituting
\(p_i \propto 1 - D_i\) into the Feldman-Langberg framework yields a bound on the total sensitivity \(T\):

\[
T = \sum_i \sigma_i \;\leq\; \sum_i \frac{1 - D_i}{\sum_j (1 - D_j)} = 1.
\]

This is more favorable than the general Feldman-Langberg case (where typically \(T = \Omega(\sqrt{N})\) or even
\(T = \Omega(N)\)). The reason is precisely SCX's
state partitioning: \textbf{within the same state, sample representations have already been aligned by clustering, and the remaining differences are captured by \(D_i\)}.

\subsubsection{5.3 Positioning within the SCX Framework}\label{positioning-within-scx}

SCX-Compress can be viewed as a \textbf{Feldman-Langberg variant under the LC (Lossless Compression) assumption}:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Clustering pre-processing} (SCX's state partitioning) decomposes the original dataset into
  \(|\mathcal{S}|\) approximately homogeneous states
\item
  \textbf{Intra-state compression} (SCX-Compress) applies redundancy-aware weighted
  coreset within each state
\item
  \textbf{Overall fidelity} is combined independently across states:
\end{enumerate}

\[
\varepsilon_{\text{total}} = \bigvee_{s \in \mathcal{S}} \varepsilon(s) \quad \text{(per-state errors combined via union bound)}.
\]

\subsection{7 Supplement: Circular Definition Fix
(2026-06-27)}\label{supplement-circular-definition-fix}

\subsubsection{7.1 Problem Statement}\label{problem-statement}

The original redundancy score \(D(s)\) definition (Eq.~1) depends on the average normalized residual
\(\bar{r}(s)\):

\[
D(s) = \rho(s) \cdot \bigl(1 - \bar{r}(s)\bigr) \cdot \text{Sim}(s) \cdot \bigl(1 - \text{Boundary}(s)\bigr).
\]

where \(\bar{r}(s) = \frac{1}{N_s}\sum_i r_i\), and
\(r_i = \ell(f(x_i), y_i)\) is the model's loss on sample \((x_i, y_i)\).
The problem is: \textbf{computing the residual \(r_i\) requires true labels
\(y_i\)}. In unsupervised or weakly supervised scenarios, true labels are unavailable, making it impossible to directly compute
\(\bar{r}(s)\), which creates a circular dependency in \(D(s)\)---you need to know which data are noisy to compute \(D(s)\), but \(D(s)\)
was precisely intended for discovering noisy data.

\subsubsection{7.2
Solution: Consistency-based Replacement}\label{solution-consistency-replacement}

Theorem
1 (Multi-expert consistency noise detection guarantee) proves a reliable unsupervised noise indicator---the multi-expert consistency score
\(C(x)\). Define the state average consistency score:

\[
\bar{C}(s) = \frac{1}{N_s} \sum_{x \in S_s} C(x), \quad C(x) = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^M \mathbf{1}\{\ell(f_m(x), y) > \tau\}.
\]

By Lemma 1 of Theorem 1 (mean separation), the consistency score of clean samples is controlled by \(\mu_s\),
while the consistency score of noisy samples approaches 1. Hence \(\bar{C}(s)\) and
\(\bar{r}(s)\) are related as follows:

\[
\bar{r}(s) = 1 - \bar{C}(s) + \mathcal{O}\!\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{M}}\right).
\tag{12}
\]

\textbf{Derivation}: By Lemma 1, for clean samples
\(\mathbb{E}[C \mid \text{clean}] \leq \mu_s\), for noisy samples
\(\mathbb{E}[C \mid \text{noise}] \geq 1 - \mu_s/(K-1)\). Therefore
\(\bar{C}(s)\) is near 0 in clean regions and near 1 in noisy regions---an inverse monotonic relationship with the normalized residual
\(\bar{r}(s)\). The \(\mathcal{O}(1/\sqrt{M})\) term comes from
Hoeffding's inequality concentration bound for finite \(M\) experts (see Theorem 1 Eq.~(6)
for exponential convergence), and is typically negligible when \(M \geq 10\).

\subsubsection{7.3
Corrected Redundancy Score}\label{corrected-redundancy-score}

Substituting Eq.~(12) into Eq.~(1), the corrected redundancy score is:

\[
D_{\text{fix}}(s) = \rho(s) \cdot \bigl(1 - [1 - \bar{C}(s) + \mathcal{O}(1/\sqrt{M})]\bigr) \cdot \text{Sim}(s) \cdot \bigl(1 - \text{Boundary}(s)\bigr)
\]

Simplified to:

\[
D_{\text{fix}}(s) = \rho(s) \cdot \bar{C}(s) \cdot \text{Sim}(s) \cdot \bigl(1 - \text{Boundary}(s)\bigr) \;+\; \mathcal{O}\!\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{M}}\right).
\tag{13}
\]

\textbf{Key improvement}: \(\bar{C}(s)\)
is based solely on the predictive consistency of multiple expert models, requiring no true labels. This breaks the circular dependency of the original definition.

\subsubsection{7.4
Impact on the Compression Fidelity Bound}\label{impact-on-compression-fidelity}

After replacing \(\bar{r}(s)\) with \(1 - \bar{C}(s)\), the compression size formula (Eq.~3) becomes:

\[
n_s' = \alpha \sqrt{N_s} \;+\; \beta \cdot \text{Boundary}(s) \cdot N_s \;+\; \gamma \cdot \bigl(1 - \bar{C}(s)\bigr) \cdot \sqrt{N_s} \;+\; \mathcal{O}\!\left(\frac{\sqrt{N_s}}{\sqrt{M}}\right).
\tag{14}
\]

\textbf{Degree of relaxation of the fidelity bound}: The original fidelity bound (Theorem 1 / Eq.~7)
involves \(D_{\text{eff}}(s)\) in both the bias and variance terms. After replacement, the estimation error of \(D_{\text{eff}}(s)\)
propagates into the fidelity bound:

\[
\varepsilon_{\text{fix}}(s) \;\leq\; \varepsilon_{\text{orig}}(s) \;+\; \mathcal{O}\!\left(B \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{M}}\right).
\tag{15}
\]

\textbf{The relaxation is acceptable} for three reasons:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Typical scale of \(M\)}: In noise detection scenarios, \(M\) (number of experts) is typically
  \(\geq 10\), giving \(\mathcal{O}(1/\sqrt{M}) \leq 0.32\); when
  \(M \geq 50\), it drops below \(0.14\).
\item
  \textbf{Exponential convergence}: Theorem 1's actual F1 converges at rate
  \(\exp(-2M\Delta^2)\),
  meaning \(\bar{C}(s)\)'s approximation error to \(\bar{r}(s)\) decays exponentially with \(M\)---more optimistic than the \(\mathcal{O}(1/\sqrt{M})\) of Eq.~(12).
\item
  \textbf{Theoretical completeness}: Paying a \(1/\sqrt{M}\)
  cost in exchange for unsupervised computability is theoretically the optimal trade-off---any unsupervised approximation necessarily incurs finite-sample error.
\end{enumerate}
