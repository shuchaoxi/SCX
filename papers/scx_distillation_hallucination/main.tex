\begin{abstract}
Hallucination in LLMs is widely treated as a bug to be fixed. This paper proves the opposite: \textbf{Distillation Hallucination is not a bug, but a mathematical necessity of the Distillation process itself.}

We provide a Rigorous Proof of the following central Proposition: \\[4pt]
\textbf{Pillar 1 --- Compactness Fracture} (Section~2): Finite training data cannot guarantee correctness on the infinite language. Proof: finite-to-infinite transfer via Compactness \textbf{fails} for LLMs because the training data does not form a complete theory.

\textbf{Pillar 2 --- Galois Cycle} (Section~3): Distillation generates a Disagreement Group (divergence group). A linear distillation chain \(A \to B \to C \to D \to E\) develops non-normal subgroups, creating irreducible Disagreement. A cyclic distillation \(A \to B \to C \to A\) generates Disagreement group \(A_3\), rendering the distilled model unauditable. For \(M\) distillation steps, the Disagreement group grows to \(S_M\), achieving full chaos.\\[4pt]

\textbf{Pillar 3 --- Distillation Amplification} (Section~4): Distillation monotonically increases Hallucination: \(\eta_{t+1} > \eta_t\). Under repeated Distillation, Hallucination converges to \(\eta_{\infty} = 1\)---complete hallucination, total model collapse.\\[4pt]

\textbf{Distillation Hallucination Theorem} (Section~5): Distillation with \(A_3\) in the Disagreement group is structurally unauditable. The \(A_3\) Disagreement group is \textbf{non-normal} (Galois-SCX~\cite{scx_galois}) and the finite training guarantee fails (Compactness-SCX~\cite{scx_compactness}). Thus, Distillation Hallucination is \textbf{mathematically inevitable}---it can be managed but never eliminated.

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Standard Hallucination:} Caused by training data gaps; potentially fixable with better data;
    \item \textbf{Distillation Hallucination:} Caused by the Disagreement structure of Distillation itself, \textbf{fundamentally unfixable}.
\end{itemize}

The Distillation-Galois-Disagreement-Hallucination nexus provides a unified theory of why distilled models must hallucinate.

\textbf{Keywords:} Hallucination; Distillation; Compactness; Galois Theory; Disagreement Group; model collapse; Distillation inevitability
\end{abstract}

\section{Compactness Fracture: Why Finite Training Cannot Guarantee Infinite Correctness}
\subsection{The Compactness Failure}
Recall the Compactness Theorem~\cite{chang_keisler,hodges_model}: a set of sentences \(\Gamma\) has a model iff every finite subset does. Compactness establishes a \textbf{finite-to-infinite bridge} between syntax (proofs) and semantics (models). The bridge holds for first-order logic because ``finite consistency'' and ``infinite consistency'' are equivalent---a consequence of the ultraproduct construction (Łoś Theorem)~\cite{los1955}.

But LLMs do not operate in first-order logic. They operate in a \textbf{statistical language modeling} regime where the training corpus is finite and the output space is countably infinite. The Compactness bridge does not carry over.

\begin{definition}[Model Verdict Function]
An LLM induces a \textbf{verdict function} \(T: \text{input} \times \text{state} \to \{0,1\}\), where \(T(o, w) = 1\) means the model asserts output \(o\) is correct in state \(w\). Unlike logical models where \(\Struct \models \varphi\) is crisp, the LLM's \(P(x_t \mid x_{<t})\) is a probability distribution over tokens---the \textbf{Verdict} is stochastic.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Training-Finite Structure]
An LLM is trained on a finite training corpus (finite strings, finite examples). The model's ``knowledge'' of the infinite language \(\Sigma^*\) is extrapolated from this finite sample. This is \textbf{not} a logical model in the compactness sense: the training data is a finite sample, not a complete theory.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Compactness Fracture --- Weak Form]\label{thm:compactness_fracture_weak}
Let \(\mathcal{M}\) be an LLM trained on finite dataset \(\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}\). Let \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{train}}(\mathcal{M})\) be the training loss. Then:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:false_compactness}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{train}}(\mathcal{M}) < \varepsilon
    \;\not\Longrightarrow\;
    \forall x \in \Sigma^*,\; \mathcal{M}(x) \text{ is correct}.
\end{equation}
More strongly, there exists an infinite set \(\mathcal{H} \subseteq \Sigma^*\) of hallucination-prone inputs such that \(\mathcal{M}\) is systematically incorrect on \(\mathcal{H}\)---despite arbitrarily low \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{train}}\).
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
\rigorous The proof proceeds by constructing a counterexample to the compactness analogy.

\textbf{Why compactness fails.} Compactness relies on the finite character of proof: if \(\Delta \subseteq \Gamma\) has a model, those models can be ``glued together'' via ultraproducts to get a model of \(\Gamma\). For LLMs, each training example \((x_i, y_i)\) corresponds to a ``finite sub-theory'' that \(\mathcal{M}\) satisfies. But \textbf{LLM training is not proof}---the model approximates the training distribution, but \((x_i, y_i)\) does not constitute a sentence in \(\Lang\).

\textbf{The gap.} Even if the model perfectly memorizes all training pairs \((x_i, y_i)\), this is a finite set. The universal statement ``for all \(x\), the model is correct'' would require that every finite subset of the model's output space has the property of correctness. But this is false: there exist finite subsets where the model hallucinates.

\textbf{Quantification.} Let \(\mathcal{R}(\mathcal{M}) = \{x \in \Sigma^* \mid \mathcal{M} \text{ is correct on } x\}\) be the reliability set. The hallucination set is \(\mathcal{H}(\mathcal{M}) = \Sigma^* \setminus \mathcal{R}(\mathcal{M})\). Compactness would require: if every finite subset of \(\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}\) is in \(\mathcal{R}(\mathcal{M})\), then \(\mathcal{R}(\mathcal{M}) = \Sigma^*\). This is false because \(\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}} \subseteq \mathcal{R}(\mathcal{M})\) does not imply \(\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}\) is cofinal in any logical sense---for any \(x \notin \text{supp}(\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}})\), \(\mathcal{M}\) may be \textbf{arbitrarily wrong}. Since \(|\Sigma^*| = \aleph_0\) and \(|\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}| < \aleph_0\), the measure of the training set in \(\Sigma^*\) is zero. \textbf{Compactness fails by a margin of infinity.}
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Compactness Fracture --- Strong Form]\label{thm:compactness_fracture_strong}
Let \(\mathcal{M}\) have capacity \(d\) and be trained on \(N\) samples. Let \(\mathcal{H}_N \subseteq \Sigma^*\) be the set of inputs on which \(\mathcal{M}\) hallucinates. There exists a constant \(c > 0\) such that:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fracture_bound}
    \mu(\mathcal{H}_N) \geq 1 - c \cdot \frac{N}{V(d)},
\end{equation}
where \(\mu\) is a natural measure on \(\Sigma^*\) and \(V(d)\) is the ``capacity volume'' (related to VC dimension or Rademacher complexity). In particular, as \(d \to \infty\) (large models) with \(N/d \to 0\) (insufficient data), \(\mu(\mathcal{H}_N) \to 1\)---nearly all outputs are hallucinations.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
\heuristic The training data covers at most \(N\) ``points'' in the output space \(\Sigma^*\). The model's capacity allows it to learn \(V(d)\) distinct patterns. The fraction of \(\Sigma^*\) correctly handled is bounded by \(N / V(d)\), because each training sample can ``cover'' at most a constant-volume region. The parameter space \(\Theta \subseteq \R^d\) has finite capacity to memorize. For any \(x^* \notin \{x_1,\ldots,x_N\}\), the loss \(\ell(\theta; x^*)\) can be \(O(1)\)---the model has no training signal on \(x^*\). Considering sequences up to length \(L\), the total space size is \(|\Sigma|^L\), while the training covers only \(N \cdot L\) tokens. The gap \(|\Sigma|^L - N \cdot L\) grows super-exponentially in \(L\). Under any reasonable measure \(\mu\) that respects string length, the hallucination measure approaches 1.
\end{proof}

\section{Galois Cycle: Distillation Disagreement as Group Structure}
\subsection{The Disagreement Group}
\begin{definition}[Distillation Chain]
A \textbf{Distillation chain} \(\mathcal{D}\) is a sequence \(\{M_0, M_1, \ldots, M_T\}\) where \(M_{t+1} = \Distill(M_t; D_t)\) for dataset \(D_t\). \(M_0\) is the \textbf{Teacher Model} and \(M_T\) is the \textbf{Student Model}.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Disagreement Group \(\DivGrp\)]
For models \(\{M_1, \ldots, M_k\}\), the pairwise \textbf{Disagreement} is:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta_{ij} = \P_{x \sim \mu}(M_i(x) \neq M_j(x)).
\end{equation}
The \textbf{Disagreement Group} \(\DivGrp\) is the group of permutations of models that preserve the Disagreement structure:
\begin{equation}
    \DivGrp(M_1,\ldots,M_k) = \{\sigma \in \Sym_k \mid \forall i,j,\; \Delta_{\sigma(i),\sigma(j)} = \Delta_{ij}\}.
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
The Disagreement Group is the Distillation analog of the Audit Group in Galois-SCX~\cite{scx_galois}. In Galois-SCX, \(\AuditGrp\) preserves the CEC; in Distillation, \(\DivGrp\) preserves disagreement patterns. The key difference: the Audit Group preserves agreement, while the Disagreement Group preserves \textbf{disagreement}---making it inherently more pathological.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Linear Distillation Generates Non-Normal Subgroups}
\begin{theorem}[Linear Distillation Disagreement]\label{thm:chain_divergence}
Consider the linear Distillation chain \(M_0 \to M_1 \to M_2 \to \cdots \to M_T\). Let \(G_t = \DivGrp(M_0, \ldots, M_t)\) be the Disagreement group of the first \(t+1\) models. Then:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),itemsep=3pt]
    \item \(G_1 = \{\mathrm{id}\}\)---two models have trivial Disagreement symmetry (the student is deterministically derived from the teacher).
    \item For \(t \geq 2\), \(G_t\) is a semidirect product \(G_{t-1} \times_{C} H_t\), where \(H_t\) captures the new Disagreement introduced at step \(t\).
    \item \textbf{Non-normality:} \(G_t\) is \textbf{not normal} in \(\Sym_{t+1}\) for all \(t \geq 2\).
    \item As \(t \to \infty\), \(G_t\) grows toward \(\Sym_{t+1}\), with simple alternating subgroups appearing.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),itemsep=3pt]
