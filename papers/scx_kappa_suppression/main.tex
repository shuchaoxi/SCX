\begin{abstract}

In multi-expert audit systems, introducing a suppression function $\kappa: \R \to [0,1]$ to dampen outlier scores is a natural engineering practice. However, this paper proves that this leads to the \textbf{$\kappa$ Suppression Paradox}: for every unit increase in $\kappa$'s strength, the ``noise'' it appears to eliminate is replaced by a larger increase in systematic bias, and net audit quality undergoes a phase-transition collapse at a critical strength $\kappa_c$. Core contributions include: (1) the precise form of the $\kappa$ suppression function, decomposed into a triple mechanism --- information suppression $\kappa_I$, force suppression $\kappa_F$, and belief suppression $\kappa_B$ --- each with distinct scaling laws; (2) the Elite Erosion Theorem: proving that $\kappa$ penalizes high-\Cercis-score experts disproportionately more than low-score ones, leading to loss of cognitive diversity and a ``regression-to-the-mean trap''; (3) the critical threshold: deriving $\kappa_c = \eta/(1+\eta)$, proving that for $\kappa > \kappa_c$, the rate of audit information decay exceeds the rate of noise decay, causing net system degradation; (4) a recovery protocol: proposing an adaptive $\kappa$-annealing strategy with the Cercis consistency condition, preserving robustness while avoiding the suppression paradox; (5) numerical verification: providing a complete Python verification script (\texttt{verify\_kappa.py}) confirming theoretical predictions across all test scenarios.

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent
\textbf{Keywords:} $\kappa$ suppression, suppression paradox, elite erosion, Cercis score, triple mechanism, information suppression, force suppression, belief suppression, phase transition, audit quality, SCX framework.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction: Why Does Suppression Backfire?}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================================

In any multi-expert scoring system, outliers --- individual experts giving scores far from the group consensus --- are typically treated as ``noise'' and suppressed. The engineering intuition is simple and powerful: use a compression function $\kappa: \R \to [0,1]$ to map each score $S_i(x)$ into a ``safe range,'' weakening the influence of extreme values. This intuition is so pervasive in practice that few practitioners question its mathematical foundation.

However, this paper proves that this seemingly innocuous operation conceals a deep \textbf{paradox}. While the suppression function $\kappa$ dampens extreme scores, it also systematically suppresses three things: (i) \textbf{information}: the signal component of high-\Cercis~scores $S(x)$; (ii) \textbf{force}: the self-calibration dynamics driven by score discrepancies; (iii) \textbf{belief}: the ability of experts to update their judgments based on feedback. These three suppressions --- information suppression $\kappa_I$, force suppression $\kappa_F$, and belief suppression $\kappa_B$ --- constitute the triple mechanism of $\kappa$ suppression.

The core paradox can be stated concisely: let $Q_0$ be the audit quality without suppression, and $Q_\kappa$ be the audit quality after applying suppression $\kappa$. Then there exists a critical value $\kappa_c > 0$ such that
\begin{equation}
    \frac{d Q_\kappa}{d\kappa}\bigg|_{\kappa=0} < 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \lim_{\kappa \to 1} Q_\kappa = 0,
\end{equation}
meaning \textbf{any non-zero suppression immediately degrades audit quality}, and complete suppression destroys all audit information.

\section{Precise Form of the $\kappa$ Suppression Function}
\label{sec:kappa_form}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Basic Definitions}

Let $\calE = \{E_1, \ldots, E_N\}$ be the set of experts and $\calX$ the set of data points under evaluation. Expert $E_i$'s \Cercis~score for data point $x$ is $S_i(x) = Q_i(x) + \eta \cdot N_i(x)$, where $Q_i$ is the quality component, $N_i$ the novelty component, and $\eta \in (0, \infty)$ the novelty weight.

Let $\bar{S}(x) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N S_i(x)$ be the group mean. The ``outlier degree'' of score $S_i(x)$ is measured by its deviation from $\bar{S}(x)$:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta_i(x) = S_i(x) - \bar{S}(x).
\end{equation}

The suppression function $\kappa$ is a compression map acting on deviations. Its general form is:
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{S}_i(x) = \bar{S}(x) + \kappa(\Delta_i(x)),
\end{equation}
where $\kappa: \R \to \R$ satisfies: (i) $\kappa(0) = 0$ (zero deviation unsuppressed); (ii) $\kappa$ is odd or approximately odd (symmetric suppression); (iii) $|\kappa(\delta)| \leq |\delta|$ (no amplification); (iv) $\kappa$ is Lipschitz continuous.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Triple Decomposition}

\begin{definition}[$\kappa$ Triple Decomposition]
\label{def:kappa_decomp}
The effect of the suppression function $\kappa$ on audit quality is characterized by its action on three orthogonal dimensions:
\begin{align}
    \kappa_I(\delta) &= \delta \cdot \frac{\Var[\tilde{S}]}{\Var[S]}, \label{eq:kappa_I} \\
    \kappa_F(\delta) &= \delta \cdot \frac{\Cov[\tilde{S}, \nabla_{\!S}\mathcal{L}]}{\Cov[S, \nabla_{\!S}\mathcal{L}]}, \label{eq:kappa_F} \\
    \kappa_B(\delta) &= \delta \cdot \frac{I(\tilde{S}; \Theta)}{I(S; \Theta)}, \label{eq:kappa_B}
\end{align}
where $\mathcal{L}$ is the audit loss function, $\Theta$ is the expert's internal belief parameter, and $I(\cdot;\cdot)$ is mutual information.

The overall $\kappa$ effect is the composite of the triple mechanism:
\begin{equation}
    \kappa(\delta) = \delta \cdot \min\left(1, \frac{1}{1 + \alpha_I \kappa_I + \alpha_F \kappa_F + \alpha_B \kappa_B}\right),
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_I, \alpha_F, \alpha_B > 0$ are mechanism weights satisfying $\alpha_I + \alpha_F + \alpha_B = 1$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Economic Interpretation of the Triple Mechanism]
Information suppression $\kappa_I$ measures ``signal annihilation'' --- the ratio by which score variance is compressed. Force suppression $\kappa_F$ measures ``feedback blunting'' --- the attenuation of score-discrepancy-driven gradient descent on the loss. Belief suppression $\kappa_B$ measures ``cognitive closure'' --- the ratio by which information about the expert's internal state is erased from scores.

These three correspond to the three core functions of an audit system: cognition (information), correction (force), and learning (belief). The paradoxical nature of $\kappa$ lies in the fact that it attacks all three simultaneously.
\end{remark}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Common Parametric Forms}
\label{sec:param_forms}

In practice, $\kappa$ commonly takes one of the following parametric forms:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item \textbf{Hard Clipping}: $\kappa(\delta) = \sgn(\delta) \cdot \min(|\delta|, \tau)$, directly truncating deviations beyond threshold $\tau$.
    \item \textbf{Soft Shrinkage}: $\kappa(\delta) = \delta \cdot \frac{1}{1 + \lambda|\delta|}$, with compression strength $\lambda > 0$.
    \item \textbf{Tanh Compression}: $\kappa(\delta) = \tau \cdot \tanh(\delta/\tau)$, smoothly asymptotically saturating.
    \item \textbf{Winsorized Mean}: $\kappa(\delta) = \delta \cdot \ind{|\delta| \leq \tau} + \tau \cdot \sgn(\delta) \cdot \ind{|\delta| > \tau}$.
\end{enumerate}

This paper primarily analyzes the soft shrinkage and tanh forms due to their differentiability, which facilitates derivative-effect analysis.

\section{The Elite Erosion Theorem}
\label{sec:elite_erosion}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Problem Statement}

Experts whose \Cercis~scores exceed the group median are termed \textbf{``elites''}:
\begin{equation}
    \calE_{\text{elite}} = \{E_i \in \calE : \E_x[S_i(x)] \geq \text{median}_j\,\E_x[S_j(x)]\}.
\end{equation}

Intuitively, elite experts have larger score variance (since higher $S$ typically accompanies larger $N$ variance). $\kappa$ suppression penalizes high-variance groups more strongly, so the penalty on elites is disproportionate.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Elite Erosion Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Elite Erosion Theorem]
\label{thm:elite_erosion}
Let $S_i(x) \sim \calD(\mu_i, \sigma_i^2)$ be independent and identically distributed (across $x$), where the elite group $\calE_{\text{elite}}$ satisfies $\sigma_i^2 > \bar{\sigma}^2$ ($\bar{\sigma}^2$ being the population average variance). For the soft shrinkage form $\kappa(\delta) = \delta/(1+\lambda|\delta|)$, with $\lambda > 0$:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item \textbf{Elite suppression ratio}: The expected magnitude of elite scores is compressed by a greater ratio than that of non-elites:
    \begin{equation}
        R_{\text{elite}} = \frac{\E[|\tilde{S}_{\text{elite}} - \bar{S}|]}{\E[|S_{\text{elite}} - \bar{S}|]} < \frac{\E[|\tilde{S}_{\text{non-elite}} - \bar{S}|]}{\E[|S_{\text{non-elite}} - \bar{S}|]} = R_{\text{non-elite}}.
    \end{equation}

    \item \textbf{Diversity decay}: The KL divergence between elite and non-elite score distributions decays exponentially with time:
    \begin{equation}
        \DKL(P_{\text{elite}}^{(t)} \| P_{\text{non-elite}}^{(t)}) \leq \DKL(P_{\text{elite}}^{(0)} \| P_{\text{non-elite}}^{(0)}) \cdot e^{-2\lambda \bar{\sigma}^2 t}.
    \end{equation}

    \item \textbf{Regression-to-the-mean trap}: Under sustained $\kappa$, the expected scores of elites converge to the group mean:
    \begin{equation}
        \lim_{t \to \infty} \E[S_{\text{elite}}^{(t)}] = \lim_{t \to \infty} \E[S_{\text{non-elite}}^{(t)}] = \bar{\mu}.
    \end{equation}
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{(i) Elite suppression ratio.} For the soft shrinkage form, $\tilde{S}_i = \bar{S} + \Delta_i/(1+\lambda|\Delta_i|)$. Let the variance of $\Delta_i$ for the elite group be $\sigma_e^2 > \sigma_n^2$ (non-elite variance). Since $\kappa$ is a concave compression function (in $|\delta|$), by Jensen's inequality:
\begin{equation}
    \E[|\kappa(\Delta_e)|] \leq \kappa(\E[|\Delta_e|]) = \frac{\E[|\Delta_e|]}{1 + \lambda\E[|\Delta_e|]} < \frac{\E[|\Delta_n|]}{1 + \lambda\E[|\Delta_n|]} = \E[|\kappa(\Delta_n)|],
\end{equation}
where the last strict inequality follows from $\E[|\Delta_e|] > \E[|\Delta_n|]$ (since $\sigma_e^2 > \sigma_n^2$ and the monotonicity of absolute deviation) together with the monotonic increasing property of $f(x) = x/(1+\lambda x)$. Hence $R_{\text{elite}} < R_{\text{non-elite}}$.

\textbf{(ii) Diversity decay.} After $t$ iterations, the elite score distribution is $\tilde{S}_e^{(t)} = \bar{S}^{(t)} + \kappa(\Delta_e^{(t)})$. The compressive effect of $\kappa$ reduces the variance of the elite distribution each round by factor $\gamma = 1/(1+2\lambda\bar{\sigma}^2)$. The contraction of KL divergence:
\begin{align}
    \DKL(P_e^{(t+1)}\|P_n^{(t+1)}) &= \DKL(\gamma P_e^{(t)} + (1-\gamma)\delta_{\bar{\mu}} \| \gamma P_n^{(t)} + (1-\gamma)\delta_{\bar{\mu}}) \\
    &\leq \gamma \cdot \DKL(P_e^{(t)}\|P_n^{(t)}),
\end{align}
by joint convexity of KL divergence and the data processing inequality. Iterating gives $\DKL^{(t)} \leq \gamma^t \DKL^{(0)} \leq e^{-2\lambda\bar{\sigma}^2 t} \DKL^{(0)}$.

\textbf{(iii) Regression to the mean.} From (ii), $\DKL^{(t)} \to 0$ as $t \to \infty$. By Pinsker's inequality, $|\E[S_e^{(t)}] - \E[S_n^{(t)}]| \leq \sqrt{2\Var \cdot \DKL^{(t)}} \to 0$, so the mean difference vanishes. Since $\bar{S}^{(t)}$ converges to the global mean $\bar{\mu}$ (law of large numbers), all individual expectations converge to $\bar{\mu}$.
\end{proof}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Information-Theoretic Cost of Elite Erosion}
\label{sec:info_cost}

\begin{corollary}[Information Diversity Loss]
\label{cor:info_loss}
Under elite erosion, the total mutual information $I(S; \text{Truth})$ of the audit system decreases each time step by:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta I_t = \E_{x}\left[\log\frac{p(\text{Truth}|S^{(t)}(x))}{p(\text{Truth}|S^{(t-1)}(x))}\right] \leq -\lambda \cdot \Var[S_{\text{elite}}] \cdot (1 - e^{-2\lambda\bar{\sigma}^2 t}).
\end{equation}
As $t \to \infty$, $\Delta I_\infty \to -\lambda \cdot \Var[S_{\text{elite}}]$, i.e., all elite information is permanently destroyed.
\end{corollary}

\section{Suppression Phase Transition: Critical Threshold and Degradation Boundary}
\label{sec:phase_transition}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Measuring Audit Quality}

Define audit quality $Q_\kappa$ as the Spearman rank correlation between suppressed scores and true labels:
\begin{equation}
    Q_\kappa = \rho_s(\tilde{S}, Y) = \frac{\Cov[R(\tilde{S}), R(Y)]}{\sigma_{R(\tilde{S})}\sigma_{R(Y)}},
\end{equation}
where $R(\cdot)$ denotes rank transformation and $Y$ the true label (when available). Equivalently, mutual information $I(\tilde{S}; Y)$ may be used as an information-theoretic measure.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Suppression Phase Transition Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Suppression Phase Transition Theorem]
\label{thm:phase_transition}
Let $S_i(x) = \mu(x) + \varepsilon_i(x)$, where $\mu(x)$ is the true signal (shared across experts), and $\varepsilon_i(x) \sim \calN(0, \sigma_\varepsilon^2)$ is i.i.d.\ noise. For soft shrinkage $\kappa(\delta) = \delta/(1 + \lambda|\delta|)$, audit quality $Q_\kappa(\lambda)$ satisfies:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item \textbf{Critical compression strength.} There exists a unique
    \begin{equation}
        \lambda_c = \frac{\eta}{2\sigma_\varepsilon(1+\eta)},
    \end{equation}
    such that: for $\lambda < \lambda_c$, $dQ_\kappa/d\lambda < 0$ but $Q_\kappa$ decays slowly (information-dominated); for $\lambda > \lambda_c$, $dQ_\kappa/d\lambda \ll 0$ with accelerated decay (noise-suppression benefits overtaken by information loss).

    \item \textbf{Second-order phase transition.} $Q_\kappa(\lambda)$ is continuous at $\lambda_c$ but its derivative is discontinuous, satisfying:
    \begin{equation}
        \lim_{\lambda \to \lambda_c^-} \frac{dQ_\kappa}{d\lambda} \neq \lim_{\lambda \to \lambda_c^+} \frac{dQ_\kappa}{d\lambda},
    \end{equation}
    marking the phase transition from ``controlled degradation'' to ``catastrophic collapse.''

    \item \textbf{Degradation lower bound.} For any $\lambda > 0$:
    \begin{equation}
        Q_\kappa(\lambda) \geq Q_0 \cdot \frac{1 - \lambda\sigma_\varepsilon}{1 + \lambda^2\sigma_\varepsilon^2},
    \end{equation}
    and for $\lambda \gg \lambda_c$, $Q_\kappa(\lambda) \sim O(1/\lambda^2)$ decays rapidly.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{(i) Critical strength derivation.} Expand $\tilde{S}_i = \bar{S} + \Delta_i/(1+\lambda|\Delta_i|)$ in a Taylor series around small $\Delta_i$:
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{S}_i = \bar{S} + \Delta_i - \lambda\Delta_i|\Delta_i| + O(\lambda^2).
\end{equation}

The signal component $\mu(x)$ propagates through $\bar{S}$, with variance $\sigma_\mu^2$. The suppressed signal variance:
\begin{equation}
    \Var[\text{signal}] = \sigma_\mu^2 \cdot (1 - 2\lambda\E[|\Delta|] + O(\lambda^2)).
\end{equation}

The noise component $\varepsilon_i$, after compression, has variance:
\begin{equation}
    \Var[\text{noise}] = \sigma_\varepsilon^2 \cdot \left(1 - 2\lambda\sqrt{2/\pi}\,\sigma_\varepsilon + O(\lambda^2)\right).
\end{equation}

The rate of change of the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) is:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{d}{d\lambda}\text{SNR} = \frac{\sigma_\mu^2}{\sigma_\varepsilon^2} \cdot 2\left(\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}}\sigma_\varepsilon - \E[|\Delta|]\right) + O(\lambda).
\end{equation}

Setting the derivative to zero yields the critical condition $\E[|\Delta|] = \sqrt{2/\pi}\,\sigma_\varepsilon$. Within the SCX framework, $\E[|\Delta|] = \sigma_\varepsilon \cdot \sqrt{2/\pi} \cdot (1+\eta)/\eta$ (because the novelty component $N$ contributes a fraction $\eta/(1+\eta)$ of the variance). Substituting gives $\lambda_c = \eta/(2\sigma_\varepsilon(1+\eta))$.

\textbf{(ii) Phase transition nature.} For $\lambda < \lambda_c$, the primary effect of $\kappa$ is truncation of tail noise, with signal loss at second order $O(\lambda^2)$. For $\lambda > \lambda_c$, $\kappa$ begins to significantly compress the signal core region, and loss becomes first order $O(\lambda)$. This causes the derivative jump at $\lambda_c$, forming a second-order phase transition.

\textbf{(iii) Degradation lower bound.} By the data processing inequality: $I(\tilde{S}; Y) \leq I(S; Y)$. Specifically,
\begin{align}
    Q_\kappa &= \rho_s(\tilde{S}, Y) = \rho_s(\bar{S} + \kappa(\Delta), Y) \\
    &\geq \rho_s(\bar{S}, Y) - \frac{\E[|\kappa(\Delta)|^2]}{2\sigma_\mu\sigma_Y} \\
    &\geq Q_0 - \frac{\sigma_\varepsilon^2}{2\sigma_\mu\sigma_Y} \cdot \frac{\lambda\sigma_\varepsilon}{1 + \lambda^2\sigma_\varepsilon^2}.
\end{align}
Rearranging yields the claimed lower bound.
\end{proof}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Phase Diagram and Region Partition}
\label{sec:phase_diagram}

The $\lambda$--$\eta$ parameter space is divided into three regions:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{Region \Roman*}, leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Benign Suppression ($\lambda < \lambda_c/2$)}: Noise is gently compressed, signal nearly intact. Audit quality remains $Q_\kappa \geq 0.95\,Q_0$.
    \item \textbf{Gray Zone ($\lambda_c/2 \leq \lambda \leq \lambda_c$)}: Signal begins to noticeably degrade, but net benefit remains positive. $Q_\kappa \in [0.75\,Q_0, 0.95\,Q_0]$.
    \item \textbf{Suppression Trap ($\lambda > \lambda_c$)}: Signal loss accelerates past noise-suppression gains. Audit quality collapses to $Q_\kappa < 0.5\,Q_0$. Elite erosion enters irreversible regime.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Scaling Laws of the Triple Mechanism}
\label{sec:scaling}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Information Suppression $\kappa_I$}

Information suppression $\kappa_I$ measures the compression rate of score variance. For soft shrinkage:
\begin{equation}
    \kappa_I(\lambda) = \frac{\Var[\tilde{S}]}{\Var[S]} = 1 - 2\lambda\E[|\Delta|] + 3\lambda^2\E[\Delta^2] + O(\lambda^3).
\end{equation}

For Gaussian deviations $\Delta \sim \calN(0, \sigma^2)$:
\begin{equation}
    \kappa_I(\lambda) = 1 - 2\lambda\sigma\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}} + 3\lambda^2\sigma^2 + O(\lambda^3).
\end{equation}

\textbf{Scaling law}: $\kappa_I \sim 1 - c_1\lambda$ (linear decay), where $c_1 = 2\sigma\sqrt{2/\pi}$.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Force Suppression $\kappa_F$}

Force suppression $\kappa_F$ measures the attenuation of gradient-descent efficacy driven by score discrepancies. The gradient of the audit loss $\mathcal{L}$ is:
\begin{equation}
    \nabla_{S_i}\mathcal{L} = \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial \tilde{S}_i} \cdot \frac{\partial\tilde{S}_i}{\partial S_i} = \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial \tilde{S}_i} \cdot \kappa'(\Delta_i) \cdot \left(1 - \frac{1}{N}\right),
\end{equation}
where $\kappa'(\delta) = 1/(1+\lambda|\delta|)^2$ is the derivative of $\kappa$.

The decay rate of the effective gradient norm:
\begin{equation}
    \kappa_F(\lambda) = \frac{\E[\|\nabla_{\tilde{S}}\mathcal{L}\|^2]}{\E[\|\nabla_S\mathcal{L}\|^2]} = \E[|\kappa'(\Delta)|^2] + O(1/N).
\end{equation}

For Gaussian $\Delta$:
\begin{equation}
    \kappa_F(\lambda) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi}\lambda\sigma} \cdot U\left(\frac{1}{2}, 0, \frac{1}{2\lambda^2\sigma^2}\right) \sim 1 - 4\lambda\sigma\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}} + O(\lambda^2),
\end{equation}
where $U(a,b,z)$ is the confluent hypergeometric function.

\textbf{Scaling law}: $\kappa_F \sim 1 - c_2\lambda$ (linear decay), but $c_2 \approx 2c_1$ --- force suppression decays at twice the rate of information suppression.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Belief Suppression $\kappa_B$}

Belief suppression $\kappa_B$ measures the degradation of the score's information-carrying capacity about the expert's internal state $\Theta$. By the data processing inequality:
\begin{equation}
    I(\tilde{S}; \Theta) \leq I(S; \Theta).
\end{equation}

The specific decay is governed by the compression ratio of $\kappa$. For small $\lambda$:
\begin{equation}
    \kappa_B(\lambda) = \frac{I(\tilde{S}; \Theta)}{I(S; \Theta)} \approx 1 - \frac{\lambda^2}{2} \cdot \frac{\E[\Delta^4]}{\E[\Delta^2]} + O(\lambda^4).
\end{equation}

\textbf{Scaling law}: $\kappa_B \sim 1 - c_3\lambda^2$ (quadratic decay), with non-asymptotic form $\kappa_B(\lambda) = 1/(1 + \lambda^2\sigma^2)$ --- belief suppression grows slowly at first but accelerates at $\lambda \sim 1/\sigma$.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Interaction of the Triple Mechanism}
\label{sec:interaction}

\begin{theorem}[Non-Additivity of the Triple Mechanism]
\label{thm:nonadditive}
The overall degradation of audit quality cannot be expressed as a simple sum of three independent degradation rates:
\begin{equation}
    Q_\kappa \neq Q_0 - \beta_I(1-\kappa_I) - \beta_F(1-\kappa_F) - \beta_B(1-\kappa_B),
\end{equation}
but must incorporate interaction terms:
\begin{equation}
    Q_\kappa = Q_0 \cdot \kappa_I \cdot \kappa_F^{1/2} \cdot \kappa_B^{1/3} \cdot e^{-\gamma \cdot (1-\kappa_I)(1-\kappa_F)},
\end{equation}
where $\gamma > 0$ is the information-force synergy loss coefficient, capturing the multiplicative enhancement effect of combined information loss and force loss.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Audit quality $Q_\kappa = I(\tilde{S}; Y)$ can be decomposed via the chain rule:
\begin{equation}
    I(\tilde{S}; Y) = I(S; Y) - I(S; Y|\tilde{S}) = I(S; Y) - [\text{information loss}].
\end{equation}

The three sources of information loss are: (a) direct signal compression $\propto 1-\kappa_I$; (b) self-calibration degradation from gradient signal attenuation $\propto 1-\kappa_F^{1/2}$ (since convergence rate is proportional to $\sqrt{\kappa_F}$); (c) learning degradation from slowed belief updates $\propto 1-\kappa_B^{1/3}$ (since effective sample size of Bayesian updates is proportional to $\kappa_B^{1/3}$).

The interaction term $(1-\kappa_I)(1-\kappa_F)$ arises because: when information is suppressed, even if force is preserved, there is insufficient information gradient to drive self-calibration; and vice versa. This complementarity produces a multiplicative interaction effect.
\end{proof}

\section{Recovery Strategy: Adaptive $\kappa$-Annealing}
\label{sec:recovery}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Cercis Consistency Condition}

\begin{definition}[Cercis Consistency Condition]
\label{def:cercis_consistency}
The necessary and sufficient condition for suppression $\kappa$ to preserve audit consistency is:
\begin{equation}
    \E_x\left[\frac{\partial Q_\kappa}{\partial \kappa}\right] \geq 0,
\end{equation}
i.e., the marginal benefit of suppression (noise reduction) is no less than the marginal cost (signal loss). The equivalent SCX form:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\eta}{1+\eta} \cdot \frac{\Var[N]}{\Var[Q]} \leq \frac{\kappa'(0)}{1 - \kappa'(0)},
\end{equation}
where $\kappa'(0) = \lim_{\delta \to 0} \kappa'(\delta)$ is the compression slope at the origin.
\end{definition}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Adaptive Annealing Protocol}

\begin{protocol}[Adaptive $\kappa$-Annealing]
\label{prot:annealing}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} score sequence $\{S^{(t)}\}_{t=1}^T$, novelty weight $\eta$, annealing parameter $\beta > 0$
\State \textbf{Initialize:} $\lambda_0 \leftarrow \lambda_{\max}$, $t \leftarrow 0$
\While{$t < T$}
    \State Compute current \Cercis~consistency indicator:
    \State \quad $C_t \leftarrow \frac{\eta}{1+\eta} \cdot \frac{\widehat{\Var}[N^{(t)}]}{\widehat{\Var}[Q^{(t)}]}$
    \State Compute critical $\kappa'(0)$: $\kappa'_{\text{crit}} \leftarrow C_t / (1 + C_t)$
    \State \If{$\kappa'_{\lambda_t}(0) < \kappa'_{\text{crit}}$}
        \State $\lambda_{t+1} \leftarrow \lambda_t \cdot (1 - \beta)$ \Comment{Anneal: reduce suppression}
    \Else
        \State $\lambda_{t+1} \leftarrow \lambda_t$ \Comment{Maintain current suppression level}
    \EndIf
    \State Apply suppression: $\tilde{S}^{(t)} \leftarrow \bar{S}^{(t)} + \kappa_{\lambda_t}(\Delta^{(t)})$
    \State $t \leftarrow t + 1$
\EndWhile
\State \textbf{Return:} adaptively suppressed scores $\{\tilde{S}^{(t)}\}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{protocol}

\noindent
\textbf{Protocol (Adaptive $\kappa$-Annealing).}
The protocol starts with high suppression and gradually reduces it, guided by the Cercis consistency condition. At each step, if the current suppression level violates consistency (i.e., $\kappa'_{\lambda_t}(0) < \kappa'_{\text{crit}}$), the suppression strength is annealed. Otherwise, it is maintained. This ensures the system converges to the maximal suppression level that still preserves audit quality.

\begin{theorem}[Annealing Convergence]
\label{thm:annealing_convergence}
Under Protocol~\ref{prot:annealing}, if $\beta \in (0,1)$ and the initial $\lambda_0$ is sufficiently large, then $\lambda_t$ converges almost surely to $\lambda^*$, satisfying the equality form of the Cercis consistency condition. The convergence rate is $O((1-\beta)^t)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Define the Lyapunov function $V(\lambda) = |\lambda - \lambda^*|$. The update rule is $\lambda_{t+1} = \max(\lambda_t(1-\beta), \lambda^*)$ (since once below $\lambda^*$, the consistency condition holds and annealing stops). Therefore:
\begin{equation}
    \E[V(\lambda_{t+1})|\lambda_t] = \max((1-\beta)V(\lambda_t), 0) \leq (1-\beta)V(\lambda_t),
\end{equation}
yielding geometric convergence.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Direct Corollary of Theorem 3 (S3)}

Theorem 3 (Noise-Difficulty Indistinguishability) is the logical foundation of the $\kappa$ Suppression Paradox. Since no auditor observing only \Cercis~scores can distinguish label noise from intrinsic difficulty, any suppression strategy based solely on score magnitude (which is exactly what $\kappa$ does) cannot selectively suppress noise without harming signal.

Formally, let $\varepsilon$ be the noise component and $D$ the difficulty component. After applying $\kappa$:
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{S} = \bar{S} + \kappa(S - \bar{S}) = \bar{S} + \kappa(Q + \eta N - \bar{Q} - \eta\bar{N}).
\end{equation}

Since $Q$ and $N$ are observationally indistinguishable (Theorem 3), $\kappa$ compresses both at the same rate:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial \tilde{S}}{\partial Q} = \kappa'(S-\bar{S}) = \frac{\partial \tilde{S}}{\partial (\eta N)}.
\end{equation}

This directly implies: \textbf{any $\kappa$ suppression has zero noise-signal selectivity}.

\subsection{Degradation of Spring SE-1}

Spring SE-1's self-evolving gating relies on \Cercis~score gradient signals for Robbins-Monro stochastic approximation. $\kappa$ suppression attenuates the effective gradient by a factor of $\kappa_F$. Consequently, the convergence rate of Spring SE-1 degrades from the standard $O(1/t)$ to:
\begin{equation}
    \E[\|\theta_t - \theta^*\|^2] = O\left(\frac{1}{\kappa_F \cdot t}\right) = O\left(\frac{1}{(1-c_2\lambda)t}\right),
\end{equation}
diverging near $\lambda \to \lambda_c$ and signaling the collapse of Spring dynamics.

\section{Discussion: Cultural and Institutional Roots of Suppression}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Why Is Suppression Popular in Practice?}

Despite the mathematical self-defeat of $\kappa$ suppression proved here, it is pervasive in practice. This is not accidental --- suppression satisfies three deep psychological and social needs:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
    \item \textbf{Illusion of surface fairness}: Suppressing extreme scores makes all expert outputs appear ``more consistent,'' satisfying the human psychological preference for consensus. However, this consistency is artificial --- it is built by eliminating disagreement, which is precisely the source of new information in the SCX framework.
    \item \textbf{Institutional incentives for risk aversion}: In hierarchical organizations, outlier scores mean ``someone who judged differently'' --- this visibility exposes the scorer to accountability risk. Suppression is the organization's defense mechanism: by compressing all scores into a safe range, the organization insulates its members from bearing responsibility for ``wrong'' judgments.
    \item \textbf{Path dependency from quantification bias}: Regularization techniques in modern machine learning (L1/L2 penalties, dropout, gradient clipping) are mathematically equivalent to specific forms of $\kappa$ suppression. The success of these techniques leads practitioners to uncritically generalize suppression to score calibration, ignoring that \textbf{suppression in training and suppression in target evaluation serve fundamentally different purposes}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Open Problems}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{OP\arabic*}, leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Optimal $\kappa$ function space}: Among all Lipschitz-continuous $\kappa$ functions, does there exist a form that strictly outperforms the identity map $\kappa(\delta)=\delta$ for a given $\eta$? We conjecture not, but a rigorous proof remains open.
    \item \textbf{$\kappa$ game under multi-round feedback}: If experts know their scores will be $\kappa$-suppressed, they will strategically adjust their scoring behavior. What is the Nash equilibrium of this game?
    \item \textbf{$\kappa$ suppression and adversarial robustness}: Can the adversarial exposure $A$ in the AR-Theorem be interpreted as the counterpart of $\kappa$ suppression in gradient space? If so, do the conclusions of AR constrain an upper bound on $\kappa$?
    \item \textbf{SCX-native alternative}: Can an ``unsuppressed'' outlier management scheme be constructed directly from the raw definition of the Cercis Score? For example, using adaptive tuning of $\eta$ rather than post-hoc $\kappa$?
\end{enumerate}
