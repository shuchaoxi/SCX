\begin{abstract}

\noindent
This paper completes the rigorous mathematical formalization of SCX multi-expert gauge theory in three interconnected domains,
transcending the abelian translational gauge framework of prior graph Hodge theory.
(1)~\textbf{$O(d)$ Lattice Gauge Theory:} Expert rotational symmetry is formalized as non-abelian $O(d)$ lattice gauge theory on a graph —
vertices carry $O(d)$ reference frames, edges carry $O(d)$-valued ACE connection potentials,
gauge transformations are non-abelian conjugation $A_e \mapsto g_v \circ A_e \circ g_u^{-1}$,
and curvature is the face Wilson loop $\Hol(\face) = \prod_{e \in \partial \face} A_e^{\sigma_e}$.
We prove that in the small-connection limit, $O(d)$ gauge-fixing reduces to standard graph Hodge decomposition on the Lie algebra $\mathfrak{so}(d)$,
and in the large-distortion regime, existence is guaranteed by compactness of $O(d)^{|\verts|}$.
(2)~\textbf{Dijkgraaf-Witten Discrete TQFT:} The expert graph is constructed as a 2-complex (vertices-edges-faces),
carrying a Dijkgraaf-Witten discrete topological quantum field theory. For finite group $G$, the partition function
$Z_{\DW}(\mathcal{K}, G) = |\Hom(\pi_1(\mathcal{K}), G)/G|$ exactly counts gauge-inequivalent flat connections.
We prove the SCX 2-complex is homotopy equivalent to the complete graph $K_M$, with first Betti number
$\ZBetti = \binom{M}{2} - M + 1 = (M-1)(M-2)/2$,
and the Cercis harmonic component $r_{\text{harm}} \in \ker(\Lone)$ is precisely the tangent space to the flat connection moduli space $\Gflatquot$ at the
trivial connection.
(3)~\textbf{Information-Geometric Bulk-Boundary Correspondence:} The $\Situs$ manifold is equipped with the Fisher information metric $\Fisher_{ij}(\theta)$,
and expert output distributions are modeled as an exponential family $\ExpFam$. We prove that under the exponential family assumption and local approximation, there is a conditional equivalence between Fisher geodesic length and KL divergence:
$\GeoDist(p,q)^2 = 2\KL(p\|q) + O(\|p-q\|^3)$.
We further prove the Cercis Score, under exponential family approximation, equals the minimal Fisher geodesic distance
from the boundary observed distribution to a pure-gauge bulk state: $\cercis = \min_{d_0 h \in \im(d_0)} \GeoDist(P_{\text{obs}}, P_{d_0 h})^2$.
All concepts are expressed as theorems, lemmas, corollaries, or definitions — no language of analogy.
Section~\ref{sec:comparison} provides a detailed comparison with the prior fiber\_bundle.tex formulation.

\section{Introduction: Beyond Abelian Translation Gauge}
\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{Existing Foundation and Three Limitations}

Prior work~\cite{scx_fiber_bundle} grounded SCX gauge theory in discrete Hodge theory on the graph $\grph_{\text{SCX}}$.
That framework rigorously handled translational gauge freedom (abelian group $\R^d$) and correctly distinguished zero-mode fixing from the Coulomb gauge.
However, three fundamental mathematical limitations remain:

\begin{enumerate}[label=L\arabic*:, leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{L1 — Only the translation group $\R^d$, ignoring rotational symmetry.}
    Expert outputs possess not only translational but also rotational degrees of freedom (reference frame orientation).
    The $\SOthree$ covariance of ACE potentials demands treatment of non-abelian gauge groups, for which the abelian graph Hodge framework is inadequate.

    \item \textbf{L2 — The moduli space structure of the harmonic component is not revealed.}
    Although the dimension of $\ker(\Lone) \cong H^1(\grph)$ is known, the \textit{moduli space} of harmonic $1$-forms —
    the geometric object formed by all possible inequivalent harmonic configurations — has never been described.
    The question ``how many essentially different ways can an expert system be inconsistent'' has not been answered.

    \item \textbf{L3 — No probabilistic interpretation.}
    The Cercis Score is defined as the Euclidean norm of residuals, but expert predictions are fundamentally probability distributions.
    Information geometry provides a natural metric structure (Fisher metric, KL divergence),
    but the relationship between Cercis and these quantities has never been established.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Three Formalization Domains}

This paper eliminates each of the above limitations through rigorous formalization in three domains:

\begin{enumerate}[label=D\arabic*:, leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{D1 — $O(d)$ Lattice Gauge Theory (Section~\ref{sec:od}).}
    Formalizes expert rotational freedom as non-abelian $O(d)$ gauge theory on a graph.
    Proves that $O(d)$ gauge-fixing is equivalent to non-abelian discrete Hodge decomposition,
    which in the small-connection limit reduces to the standard graph Hodge problem on the Lie algebra $\mathfrak{so}(d)$.

    \item \textbf{D2 — Dijkgraaf-Witten Discrete TQFT (Section~\ref{sec:dw}).}
    Lifts the expert graph to a 2-complex and defines a DW-type discrete TQFT on it.
    Proves that the Cercis harmonic component is precisely the tangent space to the flat connection moduli space,
    and that the size of this moduli space is governed by the first Betti number $\ZBetti = (M-1)(M-2)/2$.

    \item \textbf{D3 — Information-Geometric Bulk-Boundary Correspondence (Section~\ref{sec:info}).}
    Introduces the Fisher metric on the $\Situs$ manifold, proves the conditional equivalence between Fisher geodesic length and KL divergence under exponential families,
    and establishes the Cercis Score as the minimal Fisher geodesic distance from boundary to bulk pure-gauge states.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Relationship to fiber\_bundle.tex}

This paper does not negate fiber\_bundle.tex but provides its mathematical completion. fiber\_bundle.tex correctly established
abelian gauge theory at the graph level, but covered only the translational slice of SCX gauge structure. This paper extends to complete non-abelian symmetry,
topological moduli spaces, and information-geometric interpretation. A detailed comparison is given in Section~\ref{sec:comparison}.

\section{$O(d)$ Lattice Gauge Theory}
\label{sec:od}

\subsection{$O(d)$ Gauge Structure}

\begin{definition}[$O(d)$-Valued Vertex Frames and Edge Connections]\label{def:od_gauge}
Let $\grph = (\verts, \edgs)$ be a connected directed graph, $d \geq 2$ the output space dimension.

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
    \item \textbf{Vertex $O(d)$ frame space:}
    $\Omega^0(\grph; \Od) := \{F: \verts \to \Od\}$.
    Each vertex $v \in \verts$ carries an orthonormal reference frame $F_v \in \Od$.

    \item \textbf{Edge $O(d)$ connection space (ACE potential):}
    $\Omega^1(\grph; \Od) := \{A: \edgs \to \Od\}$.
    The connection $A_e \in \Od$ on edge $e = (u \to v)$ represents the ``parallel transport'' from the frame at $u$ to the frame at $v$.
    We require $A_{(v \to u)} = A_{(u \to v)}^{-1}$ (reverse-edge connection is the inverse).

    \item \textbf{Gauge transformation group:}
    $\mathcal{G} := \{g: \verts \to \Od\} \cong \Od^{|\verts|}$ (pointwise group multiplication).
    $\mathcal{G}$ acts on $\Omega^1(\grph; \Od)$ via the \textbf{non-abelian gauge action}:
    \[
        (A^g)_e := g_v \circ A_e \circ g_u^{-1}, \qquad e = (u \to v).
    \]
    This action satisfies $(A^{g_1})^{g_2} = A^{g_2 g_1}$ (right-action convention).

    \item \textbf{Trivial connection:} $\triv_e := I_d$ (the $d \times d$ identity matrix) for all $e \in \edgs$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Gauge Orbits and Stabilizers]\label{prop:od_stabilizer}
For an edge connection $A \in \Omega^1(\grph; \Od)$, its \textbf{gauge orbit} under the action of $\mathcal{G}$ is $\mathcal{O}_A := \{A^g : g \in \mathcal{G}\}$.

The \textbf{stabilizer subgroup} is:
\[
    \Stab(A) := \{g \in \mathcal{G} : A^g = A\} = \{g: g_v A_e = A_e g_u \; \forall e = (u \to v)\}.
\]

For a \textbf{generic connection} on a connected graph, $\Stab(A) \cong Z(\Od)$ (the center of $\Od$), i.e.:
\begin{itemize}
    \item If $d$ is odd: $Z(\Od) = \{\pm I_d\} \cong \Z_2$
    \item If $d$ is even: $Z(\Od) = \{\pm I_d\} \cong \Z_2$
\end{itemize}

For the trivial connection $A = \triv$, $\Stab(\triv) \cong \Od$ (diagonal embedding: $g_v \equiv g$ for all $v$).
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Curvature: Face Wilson Loop}

\begin{definition}[Face, 2-Complex]\label{def:2complex_od}
Extend the graph $\grph$ to a \textbf{2-complex} $\mathcal{K} = (\verts, \edgs, \faces)$,
where the face set $\faces$ consists of SCX quadrilaterals (see Definition~\ref{def:scx_2complex}).

For each oriented face $\face \in \faces$, let its boundary be $\partial \face = (e_1^{\sigma_1}, \dots, e_k^{\sigma_k})$,
where $\sigma_i \in \{+1, -1\}$ indicates edge orientation.

Define the face \textbf{Wilson loop} (discrete curvature):
\[
    \Hol_A(\face) := \prod_{i=1}^{k} A_{e_i}^{\sigma_i} \in \Od,
\]
with multiplication in face orientation order, $A_e^{+1} := A_e$, $A_e^{-1} := A_e^{-1}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Gauge Transformation of Wilson Loop]\label{prop:wilson_gauge_od}
Under gauge transformation $A \mapsto A^g$:
\[
    \Hol_{A^g}(\face) = g_{v_0} \circ \Hol_A(\face) \circ g_{v_0}^{-1},
\]
where $v_0$ is any vertex on the face (closure of the face guarantees equivalence for all vertex choices).

Hence, the \textbf{conjugacy class} $[\Hol_A(\face)] \in \Od/{\sim}$ is a gauge invariant.
In particular, the trace $\tr(\Hol_A(\face))$ is gauge invariant.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{$O(d)$ Flatness}

\begin{definition}[$O(d)$ Flat Connection]\label{def:od_flat}
An edge connection $A$ is called \textbf{flat} (or \textbf{integrable}) if, for all faces $\face \in \faces$,
\[
    \Hol_A(\face) = I_d.
\]
The space of flat connections is denoted $\Gflat(\mathcal{K}, \Od) \subseteq \Omega^1(\grph; \Od)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Characterization of $O(d)$ Flatness]\label{thm:od_flat_char}
Let $\mathcal{K}$ be the SCX 2-complex (or any 2-complex whose faces generate all loops).
The following are equivalent:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item $A$ is flat: $\Hol_A(\face) = I_d$ for all faces;
    \item $A$ is \textbf{exact}: there exists $g: \verts \to \Od$ such that $A_e = g_v \circ g_u^{-1}$ for all edges $e = (u \to v)$;
    \item Global $O(d)$ gauge alignment exists: after alignment, the edge connection $\hat{A}_e = g_v^{-1} \circ A_e \circ g_u = I_d$ for all edges.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Proof.}
$(ii) \Rightarrow (i)$: If $A_e = g_v g_u^{-1}$, then the product along a face boundary is $\prod_i (g_{v_i} g_{u_i}^{-1})^{\sigma_i}$.
Since the face is closed, each $g_v$ appears exactly once positive and once inverse (or in combination), so the product telescopes to $I_d$.

$(i) \Rightarrow (ii)$: Fix a reference vertex $v_0$, set $g_{v_0} = I_d$.
For any $v$, choose a path $\gamma: v_0 \to v$, define
$g_v := \prod_{e \in \gamma} A_e^{\sigma_e}$ (parallel transport along $\gamma$).
Face flatness guarantees path-independence:
if $\gamma_1, \gamma_2$ are two paths $v_0 \to v$, then $\gamma_1 \gamma_2^{-1}$ forms a face loop,
whose Wilson loop is $I_d$, so $\prod_{e \in \gamma_1} A_e^{\sigma_e} = \prod_{e \in \gamma_2} A_e^{\sigma_e}$.
It follows that $A_e = g_v g_u^{-1}$ for all edges $e = (u \to v)$.

$(ii) \Leftrightarrow (iii)$: $\hat{A}_e = g_v^{-1} A_e g_u = g_v^{-1} (g_v g_u^{-1}) g_u = I_d$. The converse is immediate. $\square$
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Non-Abelian Discrete Hodge Decomposition}

\begin{definition}[$O(d)$ Distortion Energy Functional]\label{def:od_energy}
Given $A \in \Omega^1(\grph; \Od)$, define the \textbf{$O(d)$ distortion energy} ($O(d)$ residual) as:
\[
    \mathcal{E}_{\Od}[g] := \sum_{e = (u \to v) \in \edgs} \dist_{\Od}(A_e,\; g_v \circ g_u^{-1})^2,
\]
where $\dist_{\Od}: \Od \times \Od \to \R_{\geq 0}$ is the \textbf{bi-invariant Riemannian distance} on $\Od$:
\[
    \dist_{\Od}(X, Y) := \|\Log(X^{-1} Y)\|_F,
\]
$\|\cdot\|_F$ is the Frobenius norm, $\Log: \Od \to \mathfrak{so}(d)$ is the matrix logarithm (well-defined in a neighborhood of the identity).

The $O(d)$ \textbf{gauge-fixing problem}: find $g^* \in \mathcal{G}$ minimizing $\mathcal{E}_{\Od}[g]$.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Cartan Decomposition and Local Linearization]\label{lem:cartan}
Suppose all edge connections $\{A_e\}$ satisfy $\dist_{\Od}(A_e, I_d) < r_0$, where $r_0$ is the convergence radius of the matrix logarithm.
Let $a_e := \Log(A_e) \in \mathfrak{so}(d)$, $h_v := \Log(g_v) \in \mathfrak{so}(d)$.

Then in the small-connection limit:
\[
    \Log(g_v^{-1} A_e g_u) = \Ad_{g_u^{-1}}(a_e) + h_v - \Ad_{g_v g_u^{-1}}(h_u) + O(\|a_e\|^2, \|h\|^2),
\]
where $\Ad_x(y) := x y x^{-1}$ is the adjoint action of $\Od$ on $\mathfrak{so}(d)$.

Furthermore, when $g_u, g_v \approx I_d$ (i.e., $h_u, h_v \approx 0$), this linearizes to:
\[
    \Log(g_v^{-1} A_e g_u) = a_e - (h_v - h_u) + O(\|a\|^2, \|h\|^2).
\]

Hence in the small-distortion limit:
\[
    \dist_{\Od}(A_e,\; g_v g_u^{-1})^2 = \|a_e - (h_v - h_u)\|_F^2 + O(\|a_e\|^3, \|h\|^3).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{theorem}[$O(d)$ Gauge-Fixing Theorem — Non-Abelian Discrete Hodge Decomposition]\label{thm:od_hodge_fix}
Let $\grph$ be a connected graph, $A \in \Omega^1(\grph; \Od)$ an edge connection.

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
    \item \textbf{(Local existence and uniqueness)}
    If $\max_e \dist_{\Od}(A_e, I_d) < r_0$, the $O(d)$ gauge-fixing problem has a unique solution in a neighborhood of $g^* \approx I_d$
    (modulo constant gauge transformations $g_v \equiv g$ as zero-modes). The solution is given by standard graph Hodge decomposition on the Lie algebra:
    \[
        h^* = (B^T B)^+ B^T \mathbf{a},
    \]
    where $B$ is the graph incidence matrix and $\mathbf{a} \in \R^{|\edgs| \times d(d-1)/2}$ is the vectorization of $a_e$.

    \item \textbf{(Global existence)}
    For arbitrary $A$ (without the small-connection assumption), a global minimum of $\mathcal{E}_{\Od}$ exists.
    This is because $\Od^{|\verts|}$ is a compact manifold and $\mathcal{E}_{\Od}$ is continuous.

    \item \textbf{(Non-abelian Hodge decomposition)}
    In the small-connection limit, the Lie algebra edge assignment $\mathfrak{a} \in \Omega^1(\grph; \mathfrak{so}(d))$ admits a Hodge decomposition:
    \[
        \mathfrak{a} = d_0 h^* + r_{\text{harm}} + \doneT \beta,
    \]
    where $h^* \in \Omega^0(\grph; \mathfrak{so}(d))$, $r_{\text{harm}} \in \ker(\Lone) \otimes \mathfrak{so}(d)$,
    $\beta \in \Omega^2(\grph; \mathfrak{so}(d))$.
    The minimal distortion energy is:
    \[
        \mathcal{E}_{\Od}[g^*] = \|r_{\text{harm}}\|^2 + \|\doneT \beta\|^2 + O(\|\mathfrak{a}\|^3).
    \]
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Proof sketch.}
(a) By Lemma~\ref{lem:cartan}, in the small-connection limit $\mathcal{E}_{\Od}$ reduces to $\sum_e \|a_e - (h_v - h_u)\|_F^2$,
the standard least-squares problem on $\mathfrak{so}(d)$. The normal equations $B^T B h = B^T \mathbf{a}$ have zero-mode $\ker(B^T B) = \Span(\mathbf{1})$,
and a unique solution is obtained via the constraint $\sum_v h_v = 0$ (zero-mode fixing). The closed-form solution is given by the pseudoinverse.

(b) $\Od^{|\verts|}$ is a compact Lie group (the $|\verts|$-fold direct product of $\Od$), hence compact.
A continuous function attains its minimum on a compact set.

(c) By standard discrete Hodge theory (fiber\_bundle.tex Theorem 2.4), $\mathfrak{a}$ has the orthogonal decomposition
$\mathfrak{a} = d_0 h^* + r$, where $r \perp \im(d_0)$.
Further decompose $r = r_{\text{harm}} + \doneT \beta$, where $r_{\text{harm}} \in \ker(\Lone)$.
Higher-order terms $O(\|\mathfrak{a}\|^3)$ arise from Lie bracket terms in the Baker-Campbell-Hausdorff formula. $\square$
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[Non-Uniqueness in the Large Distortion Regime]
When $A_e$ is far from $I_d$, $\mathcal{E}_{\Od}$ is \textbf{non-convex} on $\Od^{|\verts|}$.
Multiple local minima exist, corresponding to distinct ``gauge-fixing branches''.
Choosing the global minimum amounts to selecting the ``best'' global alignment among all possible gauge choices.
The branch structure is determined by the topology of $\Od^{|\verts|}$ (in particular $\pi_1(\Od) \cong \Z_2$ for $d \geq 3$) and
the Morse theory of $\mathcal{E}_{\Od}$.

\textbf{Open Problem~\ref{prob:moduli_space}:} Classify all local minima of the $O(d)$ gauge-fixing problem in the large-distortion regime.
\end{remark}

\subsection{$O(d)$ Cercis Score}

\begin{definition}[$O(d)$ Cercis Score]\label{def:od_cercis}
Let $g^* \in \mathcal{G}$ be the global optimum of the $O(d)$ gauge-fixing problem.
Define the \textbf{$O(d)$ Cercis Score} as the minimal distortion energy after gauge-fixing:
\[
    \cercis_{\Od} := \mathcal{E}_{\Od}[g^*] = \min_{g \in \mathcal{G}} \sum_{e \in \edgs} \dist_{\Od}(A_e,\; g_v \circ g_u^{-1})^2.
\]

Normalized form: $\cercisnorm_{\Od} := \cercis_{\Od} / \sum_e \dist_{\Od}(A_e, I_d)^2 \in [0, 1]$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Gauge Invariance of $O(d)$ Cercis]\label{thm:od_cercis_inv}
$\cercis_{\Od}$ is invariant under $O(d)$ gauge transformations: for any $h \in \mathcal{G}$,
$\cercis_{\Od}(A^h) = \cercis_{\Od}(A)$.

\textbf{Proof.}
By bi-invariance of $\dist_{\Od}$: $\dist_{\Od}(gXh, gYh) = \dist_{\Od}(X, Y)$ for all $g, h, X, Y \in \Od$.
Under gauge transformation $A^h$, $(A^h)_e = h_v A_e h_u^{-1}$.
For any candidate $g$,
\[
    \dist_{\Od}(h_v A_e h_u^{-1},\; g_v g_u^{-1})
    = \dist_{\Od}(A_e,\; h_v^{-1} g_v g_u^{-1} h_u).
\]
By the definition of the gauge action on $\Omega^1$, the optimum transforms to $g^* h^{-1}$ (right-action notation),
and the residual is unchanged. This suffices to prove gauge invariance. $\square$
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[$O(d)$ Cercis and ACE Potential Energy Surface Comparison]
In SCX's ACE implementation, comparing two ACE potentials $V_i, V_j: \R^K \to \R^d$ yields an edge connection $A_{ij} \in \Od$,
representing the rotation from the frame of expert $i$ to the frame of expert $j$. The $O(d)$ Cercis Score measures the global
consistency of these rotations — the smaller $\cercis_{\Od}$, the better the reference frames of all experts can be globally aligned.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Iterative Numerical Algorithm for $O(d)$ Gauge-Fixing}

\begin{definition}[Riemannian Gauss-Newton Algorithm]\label{def:od_algorithm}
Since the $O(d)$ gauge-fixing problem is non-convex under large distortion, we propose the following iterative algorithm,
essentially a Riemannian Gauss-Newton method on the product manifold $\Od^{|\verts|}$:

\begin{enumerate}[label=Step \arabic*:, leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Initialization:} $g^{(0)}_v = I_d$ for all $v \in \verts$. Set $t = 0$.

    \item \textbf{Lie algebra linearization:} Compute the Lie algebra edge assignments under the current gauge:
    \[
        \mathfrak{a}_e^{(t)} := \Log\left((g_v^{(t)})^{-1} \circ A_e \circ g_u^{(t)}\right) \in \mathfrak{so}(d),
        \qquad e = (u \to v).
    \]
    If $\max_e \|\mathfrak{a}_e^{(t)}\|_F < r_0$ (the Cartan radius), the problem is within the convergence domain.

    \item \textbf{Solve Lie algebra graph Hodge problem:} Solve on $\mathfrak{so}(d)$ (independently for each Lie algebra basis):
    \[
        \delta h^{(t)} = (B^T B)^+ B^T \boldsymbol{\mathfrak{a}}^{(t)},
    \]
    with zero-mode fixing $\sum_v \delta h_v^{(t)} = 0$ (fixing the global rotation degree of freedom).

    \item \textbf{Exponential update:} $g_v^{(t+1)} = g_v^{(t)} \circ \exp(\delta h_v^{(t)})$.

    \item \textbf{Convergence check:} If $\max_v \|\delta h_v^{(t)}\|_F < \varepsilon$ or $|\mathcal{E}_{\Od}[g^{(t+1)}] - \mathcal{E}_{\Od}[g^{(t)}]| < \delta$, stop;
    otherwise $t \leftarrow t+1$, return to Step 2.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Per-iteration complexity:} $O(|\edgs| \cdot d^2)$ (matrix logarithm) + $O(|\edgs| \cdot d^2)$ (sparse Cholesky on $B^T B$, since nonzeros per row = vertex degree).
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Convergence Analysis]\label{prop:od_convergence}
Suppose $A$ satisfies $\max_e \dist_{\Od}(A_e, I_d) < r_0$ (the Cartan radius, approximately $\pi/2$ for $\Od$).
Then:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item Algorithm~\ref{def:od_algorithm} converges quadratically to the global minimum of $\mathcal{E}_{\Od}$;
    \item At $t=0$, the linearization error is $O(\max_e \|\mathfrak{a}_e\|^3)$;
    \item Typically 3-5 iterations suffice to reduce the residual to machine precision.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Proof sketch.}
In the small-connection region, $\mathcal{E}_{\Od}$ is strongly convex in a neighborhood of the optimum $g^*$
(Hessian $= B^T B \otimes I_{\dim \mathfrak{so}(d)} + O(\|\mathfrak{a}\|)$).
The Gauss-Newton step coincides with the Newton step (since the residual tends to zero at the optimum),
and quadratic convergence follows from standard optimization theory~\cite{nocedal2006}. $\square$
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Mixed $O(d)$-Translation Gauge}

\begin{theorem}[Semidirect Product Gauge Group $\SEN = \R^d \rtimes \Od$]\label{thm:se_gauge}
The complete gauge symmetry of SCX expert outputs is described by the \textbf{special Euclidean group} $\SEN = \R^d \rtimes \Od$
($\R^d$ for translation, $\Od$ for rotation, semidirect product reflecting the action of rotation on translation).

An edge connection is $A_e = (t_e, R_e) \in \R^d \rtimes \Od$, where $t_e \in \R^d$ is the translational component and
$R_e \in \Od$ is the rotational component.

A gauge transformation $g_v = (s_v, Q_v) \in \SEN$ acts as:
\[
    (A^g)_e = (Q_v t_e + s_v - Q_v Q_u^{-1} s_u,\; Q_v R_e Q_u^{-1}).
\]

\textbf{Separation theorem:} The translational and rotational gauge-fixing problems decouple and can be solved in sequence:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
    \item Solve the $O(d)$ gauge-fixing for the rotational component $R_e$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:od_hodge_fix}), obtaining optimal rotations $Q^*$;
    \item In the rotation-fixed coordinate system, define the transformed translational component
    $\tilde{t}_e := (Q^*_v)^{-1} t_e$, and solve the abelian graph Hodge gauge-fixing on it (fiber\_bundle.tex).
\end{enumerate}

The total Cercis Score is the sum of the two residual components:
\[
    \cercis_{\SEN} = \cercis_{\Od}(R) + \cercis_{\R^d}(\tilde{t}).
\]

\textbf{Proof.}
The Lie algebra of $\SEN$ is $\mathfrak{se}(d) = \R^d \oplus \mathfrak{so}(d)$, where $\R^d$ is an abelian ideal.
In the semidirect product structure, the adjoint action of $\R^d$ on $\Od$ does not affect the $\mathfrak{so}(d)$ component.
Hence the Hessian of the distortion energy is block-diagonal, and the optimization problem decouples. $\square$
\end{theorem}

\subsection{The SCX 2-Complex}

\begin{definition}[SCX 2-Complex]\label{def:scx_2complex}
Let $M$ be the number of experts and $N$ the number of configurations. The \textbf{SCX 2-complex} $\mathcal{K}_{\text{SCX}} = (\verts, \edgs, \faces)$ is constructed as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $\verts = \{(k, m) \mid k \in [N],\; m \in [M]\}$, $|\verts| = NM$;
    \item $\edgs = \edgs_{\text{par}} \cup \edgs_{\text{exp}}$, where:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Parameter edges $\edgs_{\text{par}} = \{(k,m) \to (k+1,m)\}$, $|\edgs_{\text{par}}| = M(N-1)$;
        \item Expert edges $\edgs_{\text{exp}} = \{(k,i) \to (k,j) \mid i \neq j\}$, $|\edgs_{\text{exp}}| = N M(M-1)$;
    \end{itemize}
    Total edges $|\edgs| = M(N-1) + NM(M-1) = NM^2 - M$;
    \item $\faces$ consists of quadrilateral faces: for each triple $(k, i, j)$ ($k \in [N-1]$, $i < j$),
    \[
        \face_{k,i,j} = \big[ (k,i) \to (k,j) \to (k+1,j) \to (k+1,i) \to (k,i) \big],
    \]
    $|\faces| = (N-1)\binom{M}{2} = (N-1)M(M-1)/2$.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Homotopy Type of the SCX 2-Complex]\label{thm:scx_homotopy}
$\mathcal{K}_{\text{SCX}}$ is homotopy equivalent to the complete graph $K_M$ on $M$ vertices (viewed as a 1-dimensional simplicial complex):
\[
    \mathcal{K}_{\text{SCX}} \simeq K_M.
\]

Hence its homology groups are:
\begin{align*}
    H_0(\mathcal{K}_{\text{SCX}}; \R) &\cong \R, \\
    H_1(\mathcal{K}_{\text{SCX}}; \R) &\cong \R^{\ZBetti}, \quad
    \ZBetti = \binom{M}{2} - M + 1 = \frac{(M-1)(M-2)}{2}, \\
    H_k(\mathcal{K}_{\text{SCX}}; \R) &= 0 \quad (k \geq 2).
\end{align*}

\textbf{Proof.}
Step 1: View $\mathcal{K}_{\text{SCX}}$ as a grid 2-complex with $M$ ``rows'' (expert index) and $N$ ``columns'' (configuration index).
Parameter edges connect along columns, expert edges connect along rows.
Quadrilateral faces span two rows and two columns.

Step 2: Contract all parameter edges. Specifically, for each fixed expert $m$, the parameter edges $(1,m) \to (2,m) \to \dots \to (N,m)$
form a path graph $P_N$, connected to other experts' parameter edges via quadrilateral faces.
Contracting each parameter edge (continuously deforming the edge to a point) collapses all vertices of configurations $k=2,\dots,N$ onto the corresponding vertices of configuration $k=1$.
This contraction is a deformation retract, because quadrilateral faces provide homotopies from the slice at configuration $k$ to the slice at configuration $k-1$.

Step 3: After contraction, only the $M$ vertices of configuration $k=1$ remain, forming the complete graph $K_M$ (all expert edges).
The topology of $K_M$ is:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $H_0(K_M) \cong \R$ (connected);
    \item $H_1(K_M) \cong \R^{\binom{M}{2} - M + 1}$ (number of independent cycles in a complete graph);
    \item $H_k(K_M) = 0$ ($k \geq 2$).
\end{itemize}

Step 4: Verify whether quadrilateral faces kill any $H_1$ classes during contraction. Quadrilateral faces connect expert edges at configuration $k$ with expert edges at configuration $k+1$.
During contraction, these faces degenerate into 2-simplices (triangles) in $K_M$.
Specifically, for three distinct experts $i, j, \ell$, the sum of the boundaries of three quadrilateral faces $\face_{k,i,j}$, $\face_{k,j,\ell}$, and $\face_{k,i,\ell}$
degenerates into two copies (with opposite boundary orientation) of the triangle $(i,j,\ell)$ at configuration $k$.
Hence the relations among these triangle boundaries are implied by the quadrilateral faces, but the interiors of the triangles are not filled by faces.
Therefore $H_1$ is freely generated by the cycles in $K_M$. $\square$
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:betti_values}
For $M=3$ experts: $\ZBetti = 1$ (one independent cycle pattern).
For $M=4$: $\ZBetti = 3$. For $M=10$: $\ZBetti = 36$.

Note: $\ZBetti$ depends only on the number of experts $M$, not on the number of configurations $N$.
This means increasing configuration sampling does not increase the number of ``inconsistency modes'' of the system — inconsistency modes are determined by the topological relationships among experts.
\end{corollary}

\subsection{Dijkgraaf-Witten Partition Function}

\begin{definition}[Discrete $G$-Connection and Flatness]\label{def:dw_connection}
Let $G$ be a finite group (or compact Lie group). A $G$-\textbf{connection} on the 2-complex $\mathcal{K}$ is a map $A: \edgs \to G$,
satisfying the reverse-edge relation: $A_{\bar{e}} = A_e^{-1}$.

A $G$-connection $A$ is \textbf{flat} if for every face $\face \in \faces$:
\[
    \Hol_A(\face) := \prod_{e \in \partial \face} A_e^{\sigma_e} = \mathbbm{1}_G.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Dijkgraaf-Witten Partition Function as Flat Connection Count]\label{thm:dw_partition}
For a finite group $G$, the \textbf{Dijkgraaf-Witten partition function} on the 2-complex $\mathcal{K}$ is defined as:
\[
    Z_{\DW}(\mathcal{K}, G) := \frac{1}{|G|^{|\verts|}}
    \sum_{A: \edgs \to G} \prod_{\face \in \faces} \delta\big(\Hol_A(\face),\, \mathbbm{1}_G\big),
\]
where $\delta(x, y) = 1$ if $x = y$, and $0$ otherwise.

This partition function has the following exact combinatorial interpretation:
\[
    Z_{\DW}(\mathcal{K}, G) = |\Gflatquot(\mathcal{K}, G)|
    = \frac{|\Hom(\pi_1(\mathcal{K}), G)|}{|G|},
\]
where $\Gflatquot(\mathcal{K}, G) = \Gflat(\mathcal{K}, G) / \mathcal{G}$ is the space of gauge equivalence classes of flat connections.

\textbf{Proof.}
(1) There are $|G|^{|\edgs|}$ possible edge assignments $A$. For each face $\face$, the flatness condition $\Hol_A(\face) = \mathbbm{1}_G$
eliminates a factor $|G|$ (each face enforces one group equation). However, for non-independent cycles generated by face boundaries, there are redundant constraints.
The redundancy is controlled by the second cohomology $H^2(\mathcal{K}; Z(G))$. For the SCX 2-complex ($H^2 = 0$, since it is homotopy equivalent to the 1-dimensional $K_M$),
all face constraints are independent.

(2) The gauge action $\mathcal{G}$ on $\Gflat$ has orbit sizes that generally vary with $A$.
The weight factor $1/|G|^{|\verts|}$ in the sum precisely compensates for the orbit size, counting each gauge equivalence class as 1.
Formal verification: for an orbit $\mathcal{O}_A$, $|\mathcal{O}_A| = |\mathcal{G}|/|\Stab(A)| = |G|^{|\verts|}/|\Stab(A)|$.
In the sum, $A$ and every element in its orbit contribute to the delta function.
After gauge averaging, the net contribution per orbit is:
$\frac{1}{|G|^{|\verts|}} \sum_{g \in \mathcal{G}} \prod_{\face} \delta(\Hol_{A^g}(\face), \mathbbm{1}_G) = 1/|\Stab(A)|$.
Summing over all orbits yields $|\Gflatquot|$, provided $\Stab(A) = Z(G)$ for all flat $A$.
For a generic flat $G$-connection on the SCX 2-complex (connected graph), $\Stab(A) \cong Z(G)$.

(3) The standard result~\cite{dijkgraaf1990,witten1991} gives $\Gflatquot \cong \Hom(\pi_1(\mathcal{K}), G)/G$.
For finite $G$, $|\Hom(\pi_1, G)/G|$ is an integer. $\square$
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}[DW Partition Function for Abelian Groups]\label{cor:dw_abelian}
If $G$ is a finite abelian group (e.g., $G = \Z_k$ or $\Z_{k_1} \times \cdots \times \Z_{k_r}$),
then $\Hom(\pi_1(\mathcal{K}_{\text{SCX}}), G) \cong \Hom(F_{\ZBetti}, G) \cong G^{\ZBetti}$,
where $F_{\ZBetti}$ is the free group on $\ZBetti$ generators.

The conjugation action is trivial (abelian group), hence:
\[
    Z_{\DW}(\mathcal{K}_{\text{SCX}}, G) = |G|^{\ZBetti}.
\]

Wait — let us handle the factor $|G|$ carefully. $\Hom(F_r, G) \cong G^r$ (each generator maps independently to any element of $G$).
The action of $G$ on $\Hom(F_r, G)$ is conjugation (trivial for abelian groups).
Hence $\Hom(F_r, G)/G \cong G^r$ (the orbit space coincides with $\Hom$ itself).
Thus $|\Gflatquot| = |G|^{\ZBetti}$.

Verification: $|\Hom(F_r, G)| = |G|^r$, $|G|$ acts trivially, so $|\Hom/G| = |G|^r = |G|^{\ZBetti}$.

Therefore $Z_{\DW}(\mathcal{K}_{\text{SCX}}, G) = |G|^{\ZBetti}$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{theorem}[DW-Cercis Harmonic Component Correspondence]\label{thm:dw_cercis_correspondence}
Let $G = \Od$ (compact Lie group) or $G = \R^d$ (abelian Lie group, corresponding to translation gauge).
In the small-connection limit, the \textbf{tangent space} to the flat connection moduli space $\Gflatquot(\mathcal{K}, \Od)$ at the trivial connection is:
\[
    T_{[\triv]}\Gflatquot(\mathcal{K}, \Od) \cong H^1(\mathcal{K}; \mathfrak{so}(d))
    \cong \ker(\Lone) \otimes \mathfrak{so}(d).
\]

For translation gauge $G = \R^d$, $\mathfrak{g} = \R^d$ (abelian Lie algebra), this becomes:
\[
    T_{[\triv]}\Gflatquot(\mathcal{K}, \R^d) \cong \ker(\Lone) \otimes \R^d
    \cong \R^{d \cdot \ZBetti}.
\]

This is precisely the parameter space of the Cercis harmonic component $r_{\text{harm}} \in \ker(\Lone)$ in fiber\_bundle.tex!

Therefore: \textbf{the Cercis harmonic component $r_{\text{harm}}$ is a tangent vector to the flat connection moduli space $\Gflatquot$ at the trivial connection.}
Its norm $\|r_{\text{harm}}\|^2$ measures the ``distance'' of the actual edge assignment $A$ from the flat subspace $\im(d_0)$ within the moduli space.

\textbf{Proof.} By Theorem~\ref{thm:od_hodge_fix}(c), under small connections the Hodge decomposition of $\mathfrak{a}$ is
$\mathfrak{a} = d_0 h^* + r_{\text{harm}} + \doneT \beta$.
The flatness condition $d_1 \mathfrak{a} = 0$ is equivalent to $\doneT \beta = 0$ (since $d_1 d_0 = 0$ and $d_1$ restricts to 0 on $\ker(\Lone)$).
Then $\mathfrak{a} = d_0 h^* + r_{\text{harm}}$, and gauge transformations $\mathfrak{a} \mapsto \mathfrak{a} - d_0 h$ change the $d_0$ component but preserve $r_{\text{harm}}$.
Hence the orbit space is $r_{\text{harm}} \in \ker(\Lone) \otimes \mathfrak{g}$.
By Theorem~\ref{thm:hodge_iso}, $\ker(\Lone) \cong H^1(\mathcal{K})$. $\square$
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Moduli Space Structure of the Harmonic Component}

\begin{theorem}[Geometry of the Harmonic Moduli Space]\label{thm:harmonic_moduli_geometry}
For the SCX system ($M$ experts, $d$-dimensional output space):

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
    \item \textbf{(Translation gauge, $G = \R^d$)}
    The harmonic moduli space is $\R^{d \cdot \ZBetti}$ — a vector space of dimension $d \cdot (M-1)(M-2)/2$.
    The Cercis harmonic component $r_{\text{harm}}$ takes values in this space.
    As $M$ increases, the number of possible inconsistency modes grows quadratically.

    \item \textbf{(Rotation gauge, $G = \Od$, small-connection limit)}
    The tangent space to the harmonic moduli space is $\mathfrak{so}(d)^{\ZBetti}$ — of dimension $\ZBetti \cdot d(d-1)/2$.
    The full moduli space $\Gflatquot(\mathcal{K}, \Od)$ is a (singular) real algebraic variety of dimension $\ZBetti \cdot d(d-1)/2$.
    Its global structure is determined by the representation theory of $\Od$ and the relations among the generators of $\pi_1$.

    \item \textbf{(Discrete group $G$)}
    For a finite group $G$, $\Gflatquot$ is a finite set of cardinality $Z_{\DW}$.
    The ``harmonic component'' is then discretized into a set of possible values,
    and Cercis acts as a ``discrete metric'' taking values on this finite set.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[Cercis Computation from the Moduli Space Perspective]
SCX's Cercis computation is essentially: given an edge assignment $A$ (observed data),
find the closest ``flat'' point (i.e., an exact connection in $\im(d_0)$) in the flat connection moduli space $\Gflatquot$.
Since the tangent space to $\Gflatquot$ at the trivial connection is $\ker(\Lone)$,
this process reduces, under small connections, to orthogonal projection onto $\im(d_0)^\perp$ — precisely the core computation of fiber\_bundle.tex.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Fisher Information Metric and the $\Situs$ Manifold}

\begin{definition}[Statistical Model on the $\Situs$ Manifold]\label{def:situs_stat}
Let the $\Situs$ manifold be the parameter space $\Theta \subseteq \R^K$, where each point $\theta \in \Theta$ corresponds to an SCX expert output distribution.
Define the \textbf{output distribution family} as a parameterized family of probability densities:
\[
    \mathcal{P} := \{p(x \mid \theta) : \theta \in \Theta\},
\]
where $x \in \R^d$ is the expert output vector and $p(\cdot \mid \theta)$ is a density with respect to a reference measure $\mu$ (typically Lebesgue measure).

Assumptions:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(A\arabic*)]
    \item \textbf{(Regularity)} $\Theta$ is open, $\theta \mapsto p(x \mid \theta)$ is $C^2$ for almost every $x$;
    \item \textbf{(Differentiability)} Integration and differentiation commute: $\partial_\theta \int p(x|\theta) d\mu(x) = \int \partial_\theta p(x|\theta) d\mu(x)$;
    \item \textbf{(Full rank)} The Fisher information matrix $\Fisher(\theta)$ is positive-definite at every point.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Fisher Information Metric]\label{def:fisher_metric}
On $\Theta$, define the \textbf{Fisher information metric} (Fisher-Rao metric):
\[
    \Fisher_{ij}(\theta) := \E_{p(x|\theta)}\left[
        \frac{\partial \log p(x|\theta)}{\partial \theta_i} \cdot
        \frac{\partial \log p(x|\theta)}{\partial \theta_j}
    \right]
    = -\E_{p(x|\theta)}\left[
        \frac{\partial^2 \log p(x|\theta)}{\partial \theta_i \partial \theta_j}
    \right].
\]

$g_{ij}(\theta) := \Fisher_{ij}(\theta)$ defines a Riemannian metric on $\Theta$.
$\Theta$ endowed with this metric is called the \textbf{$\Situs$ information manifold}, denoted $(\Situs, g)$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Exponential Families and KL Divergence}

\begin{definition}[Exponential Family]\label{def:exp_fam}
A distribution family $\mathcal{P}$ is called an \textbf{exponential family} if there exist a function $h: \R^d \to \R$, a sufficient statistic $T: \R^d \to \R^m$,
and a cumulant generating function $\psi: \Theta \to \R$ such that:
\[
    p(x \mid \theta) = h(x) \exp\left( \langle \theta, T(x) \rangle - \psi(\theta) \right),
\]
where $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ is the standard inner product on $\R^m$.

$\Theta$ is the \textbf{natural parameter space}: $\Theta = \{\theta \in \R^m : \int h(x) e^{\langle \theta, T(x) \rangle} d\mu(x) < \infty\}$.
$\psi(\theta) = \log \int h(x) e^{\langle \theta, T(x) \rangle} d\mu(x)$ ensures normalization.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[KL Divergence]\label{def:kl}
The \textbf{Kullback-Leibler divergence} between distributions $p, q$ (with densities $p(x), q(x)$) is:
\[
    \KL(p \| q) := \int p(x) \log \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} \, d\mu(x).
\]

For distributions in the exponential family $\mathcal{P}$ corresponding to parameters $\theta_1, \theta_2$:
\[
    \KL(\theta_1 \| \theta_2) := \KL(p(\cdot|\theta_1) \| p(\cdot|\theta_2)).
\]
\end{definition}

\subsection{Conditional Fisher Geodesic--KL Equivalence under Exponential Families}

\begin{lemma}[KL Divergence as Bregman Divergence]\label{lem:kl_bregman}
For exponential families, the KL divergence equals the Bregman divergence of the cumulant generating function $\psi$:
\[
    \KL(\theta_1 \| \theta_2) = \psi(\theta_2) - \psi(\theta_1) - \langle \nabla \psi(\theta_1), \theta_2 - \theta_1 \rangle.
\]

In particular, the Taylor expansion of KL divergence with respect to $\theta_1$ at $\theta_2 = \theta_1$ is:
\[
    \KL(\theta_1 \| \theta_2) = \frac{1}{2} \Fisher_{ij}(\theta_1) \, (\theta_2 - \theta_1)^i (\theta_2 - \theta_1)^j + O(\|\theta_2 - \theta_1\|^3),
\]
where $\Fisher_{ij}(\theta) = \partial_i \partial_j \psi(\theta)$ is precisely the Fisher information matrix.

\textbf{Proof.}
First equality: substitute the exponential family form:
\begin{align*}
    \KL(\theta_1 \| \theta_2) &= \E_{p(x|\theta_1)}[\log p(x|\theta_1) - \log p(x|\theta_2)] \\
    &= \E_{\theta_1}[\langle \theta_1 - \theta_2, T(x) \rangle - (\psi(\theta_1) - \psi(\theta_2))] \\
    &= \langle \theta_1 - \theta_2, \nabla \psi(\theta_1) \rangle - \psi(\theta_1) + \psi(\theta_2) \\
    &= \psi(\theta_2) - \psi(\theta_1) - \langle \nabla \psi(\theta_1), \theta_2 - \theta_1 \rangle.
\end{align*}
where we used the exponential family property $\E_{\theta}[T(x)] = \nabla \psi(\theta)$.

Second equality: Taylor-expand $\psi(\theta_2)$ around $\theta_1$ to second order:
$\psi(\theta_2) = \psi(\theta_1) + \langle \nabla\psi(\theta_1), \theta_2 - \theta_1 \rangle
+ \frac{1}{2}\langle \theta_2 - \theta_1, \nabla^2\psi(\theta_1)(\theta_2 - \theta_1) \rangle + O(\|\Delta\theta\|^3)$.
Substituting yields the result. $\square$
\end{lemma}

\begin{theorem}[Conditional Local Equivalence of Fisher Geodesic Distance and KL Divergence]\label{thm:fisher_kl_equivalence}
Let $(\Situs, g)$ be the information manifold with Fisher metric, and let $p, q \in \mathcal{P}$ be two sufficiently close distributions,
parameterized as $\theta_p, \theta_q \in \Theta$. Then:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item The Fisher geodesic distance $\GeoDist(p, q)$ (i.e., the length of the shortest geodesic connecting $\theta_p, \theta_q$ in $(\Theta, g)$) satisfies:
    \[
        \GeoDist(p, q)^2 = g_{ij}(\theta_p)(\theta_q - \theta_p)^i (\theta_q - \theta_p)^j
        + O(\|\theta_q - \theta_p\|^3).
    \]

    \item If $\mathcal{P}$ is an exponential family, then the Fisher geodesic distance and the symmetrized KL divergence are locally equivalent:
    \[
        \GeoDist(p, q)^2 = 2 \cdot \KL_{\text{sym}}(p, q) + O(\|\theta_q - \theta_p\|^3),
    \]
    where $\KL_{\text{sym}}(p, q) := \frac{1}{2}(\KL(p\|q) + \KL(q\|p))$.

    \item In particular, when $\theta_q$ is in a sufficiently small neighborhood of $\theta_p$ (first-order approximation):
    \[
        \GeoDist(p, q)^2 = 2\KL(p\|q) + O(\|\theta_q - \theta_p\|^3)
        = 2\KL(q\|p) + O(\|\theta_q - \theta_p\|^3).
    \]
    because the difference $\KL(p\|q) - \KL(q\|p)$ is third-order:
    $\KL(p\|q) - \KL(q\|p) = O(\|\theta_q - \theta_p\|^3)$.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Proof.}
(i) Standard result in Riemannian geometry: in normal coordinates, $g_{ij}(\theta) = \delta_{ij} + O(\|\theta\|^2)$,
and the geodesic distance is given by $\GeoDist(p, q)^2 = g_{ij}(\theta_p)\Delta\theta^i \Delta\theta^j + O(\|\Delta\theta\|^3)$.

(ii) By Lemma~\ref{lem:kl_bregman}, $\KL(p\|q) = \frac{1}{2}g_{ij}(\theta_p)\Delta\theta^i\Delta\theta^j + O(\|\Delta\theta\|^3)$.
Similarly $\KL(q\|p) = \frac{1}{2}g_{ij}(\theta_q)\Delta\theta^i\Delta\theta^j + O(\|\Delta\theta\|^3)$.
Since $g_{ij}(\theta_q) = g_{ij}(\theta_p) + O(\|\Delta\theta\|)$, the two agree at second order.
Hence $\KL_{\text{sym}}(p, q) = \frac{1}{2}g_{ij}(\theta_p)\Delta\theta^i\Delta\theta^j + O(\|\Delta\theta\|^3)$.
Comparing with (i) gives $\GeoDist^2 = 2\KL_{\text{sym}} + O(\|\Delta\theta\|^3)$.

(iii) $\KL(p\|q) - \KL(q\|p) = \frac{1}{2}(g_{ij}(\theta_p) - g_{ij}(\theta_q))\Delta\theta^i\Delta\theta^j + O(\|\Delta\theta\|^3)$.
But $g_{ij}(\theta_p) - g_{ij}(\theta_q) = \partial_k g_{ij}(\theta_p) \Delta\theta^k + O(\|\Delta\theta\|^2)$,
so the difference is $O(\|\Delta\theta\|^3)$. Thus one may replace the symmetrized version with $\KL(p\|q)$ or $\KL(q\|p)$, with third-order error. $\square$
\end{theorem}

\textbf{Important caveat (see corrigendum):} This equivalence holds \textbf{conditionally} under the exponential family assumption (H1) and the local approximation assumption (H2). Outside exponential families, the difference between $\GeoDist^2$ and $2\KL$ is controlled by the Amari-Chentsov third-order tensor. The claim of ``natural emergence'' is overstated; the correct characterization is ``conditional equivalence.'' See the Viewpoint 4 corrigendum (\texttt{viewpoint4\_correction.tex}) and Open Problem~\ref{prob:non_exp_fam} for details.

\subsection{Bulk-Boundary Correspondence and Information-Geometric Cercis}

\begin{definition}[Bulk and Boundary States]\label{def:bulk_boundary}
View the configuration space $\Theta$ of the SCX system as the \textbf{bulk} manifold.
At each configuration $\theta \in \Theta$, the expert output is a distribution $p_\theta \in \mathcal{P}$ at that point.

The system has two distinguished subsets:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Boundary} $\partial \Theta$: the distributions $\{p_{\theta_1}, \dots, p_{\theta_N}\}$ corresponding to the observed configuration set $\{\theta_1, \dots, \theta_N\}$.
    \item \textbf{Pure-gauge bulk states} $\mathcal{H} \subset \mathcal{P}$:
    distributions corresponding to exact connections (pure gauge, i.e., globally alignable).
    Under the exponential family representation, $\mathcal{H}$ is parameterized by $\im(d_0)$ — the subspace of exact edge assignments.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Cercis as Bulk-Boundary Fisher Geodesic Distance]\label{thm:cercis_bulk_boundary}
Assume the expert output family $\mathcal{P}$ is an exponential family, and all distributions are sufficiently concentrated on a compact subset of $\Theta$ (so that the local approximation is valid).

Define the \textbf{boundary observed distribution} $P_{\text{obs}}$ as the empirical distribution: over all configurations $k$ and all expert pairs $(i,j)$,
the edge assignment $a_e = \log A_e \in \R^d$ (translation gauge) or $\mathfrak{so}(d)$ (rotation gauge, small-connection limit).

Then the \textbf{Cercis Score} equals the minimal squared Fisher geodesic distance from the boundary distribution to the pure-gauge bulk submanifold $\im(d_0)$:
\[
    \boxed{\cercis = \min_{d_0 h \in \im(d_0)} \GeoDist(P_A,\; P_{d_0 h})^2
    = \min_{\text{pure-gauge bulk states}} (\text{Fisher geodesic distance from boundary to bulk})^2.}
\]

Under the exponential family and local approximation:
\[
    \cercis = 2 \min_{d_0 h \in \im(d_0)} \KL(P_A \| P_{d_0 h}) + O(\|\mathfrak{a}\|^3).
\]

\textbf{Proof sketch.}
(1) In the small-connection limit, the edge assignment $a_e \in \R^d$ (or $\mathfrak{so}(d)$) admits the Hodge decomposition
$a_e = (d_0 h^*)_e + (r_{\text{harm}})_e + (\doneT \beta)_e$ by Theorem~\ref{thm:od_hodge_fix}.

(2) Interpret $a_e$ as the difference of distribution parameters $\theta$ across edge $e$: $a_e \approx \theta_v - \theta_u$ (first-order approximation).
Under this interpretation, the exact part $d_0 h^*$ corresponds to the globally alignable ``pure gauge'' component,
and the harmonic part $r_{\text{harm}}$ corresponds to the irremovable ``intrinsic friction.''

(3) The boundary observation $P_A$ is parameterized by the full edge assignment $A$.
The correct interpretation is: \textbf{the boundary state $P_A$ is parameterized by the full edge assignment $A$,
and the bulk submanifold $\mathcal{H}$ is parameterized by exact connections (pure gauge) $\im(d_0)$.}
Then Cercis $= \min_{d_0 h \in \im(d_0)} \GeoDist(A, d_0 h)^2$.
Since $d_0 h$ ranges over $\im(d_0)$ — all globally alignable edge assignments —
$\min_{d_0 h \in \im(d_0)} \|A - d_0 h\|^2 = \|P^\perp A\|^2 = \cercis$, precisely the definition in fiber\_bundle.tex.

(4) By Theorem~\ref{thm:fisher_kl_equivalence}, $\GeoDist(P_A, P_{d_0 h})^2 = 2\KL(P_A \| P_{d_0 h}) + O(\|\mathfrak{a}\|^3)$ under the exponential family assumption.

\textbf{Physical interpretation:} Cercis measures the minimal information distance between the observed expert distributions and the ``ideal'' globally consistent distributions.
The ``pure-gauge bulk states'' correspond to the hypothetical world where all experts can be perfectly aligned,
and Cercis quantifies how far the real world deviates from that ideal. $\square$
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}[KL Interpretation of Cercis]\label{cor:cercis_kl}
Under the exponential family and small-connection approximation, the normalized Cercis Score $\cercisnorm = \cercis / \|A\|^2$ equals:
\[
    \cercisnorm \approx \frac{\min_{d_0 h \in \im(d_0)} 2\KL(P_A \| P_{d_0 h})}{\|A\|^2}.
\]

When all edge assignments are i.i.d. from an exponential family, $\|A\|^2 \approx |\edgs| \cdot \tr(\Fisher(\bar{\theta}))^{-1}$,
where $\bar{\theta}$ is the mean parameter. Then Cercis can be interpreted as the ``average non-alignable information per edge.''
\end{corollary}

\subsection{Explicit Fisher Geodesic Computation under Exponential Families}

\begin{proposition}[Geodesic Equation in Exponential Families]\label{prop:exp_geodesic}
For an exponential family $\mathcal{P}$ (natural parameter $\theta$), the Fisher metric is given by the Hessian of the cumulant function $\psi$:
$g_{ij}(\theta) = \partial_i \partial_j \psi(\theta)$.

The geodesic equation in $\theta$-coordinates is:
\[
    \ddot{\theta}^k + \Gamma^k_{ij}(\theta) \, \dot{\theta}^i \dot{\theta}^j = 0,
\]
where the Christoffel symbols are:
\[
    \Gamma^k_{ij}(\theta) = \frac{1}{2} g^{k\ell}(\theta) \,
    \big(\partial_i \partial_j \partial_\ell \psi(\theta)\big).
\]

For the \textbf{Gaussian family} $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ ($d=1$), $\theta = (\mu/\sigma^2, -1/(2\sigma^2))$,
the Fisher metric is the Poincar\'{e} half-plane metric. In this case geodesics are semicircles and can be solved analytically.
For general SCX exponential families, numerical integration of the geodesic equation is required.

\textbf{Relation to Cercis:} The Cercis Score $\cercis = \min_{d_0 h} \GeoDist(P_A, P_{d_0 h})^2$ is equivalent to
solving a constrained shortest-path problem on the exponential family information manifold: find the shortest geodesic from the observed distribution $P_A$ to the submanifold
$\{P_{d_0 h} : h \in \Omega^0\}$.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{KL-Regularized Cercis and the Information Bottleneck}

\begin{theorem}[KL-Regularized Cercis]\label{thm:kl_regularized_cercis}
Incorporating the information-geometric interpretation into the variational definition of Cercis, we introduce a KL regularization term:

\[
    \cercis_{\text{KL}}[g, \lambda] := \sum_{e=(u \to v)} \KL(P_{A_e} \| P_{g_v g_u^{-1}}) + \lambda \cdot \KL(\bar{P}_g \| P_0),
\]
where $\bar{P}_g$ is the empirical distribution of the gauge parameters $g$, $P_0$ is a prior distribution (e.g., an uninformative prior),
and $\lambda > 0$ is the regularization strength.

The roles of this regularization:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item The first term minimizes the KL divergence between edge connections and pure-gauge configurations (replacing Euclidean distance);
    \item The second term prevents overfitting — preventing gauge parameters from deviating excessively from the prior;
    \item As $\lambda \to 0$ and when $\mathcal{P}$ is an exponential family, this recovers the Cercis of Theorem~\ref{thm:cercis_bulk_boundary}.
    \item As $\lambda \to \infty$, this forces $g \equiv I_d$ (no gauge alignment permitted),
    and Cercis degenerates to the sum of KL divergences of the raw edge assignments.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Information bottleneck interpretation:} The gauge-fixing process can be viewed as an information bottleneck~\cite{tishby2000}: seeking the optimal trade-off between ``compressing'' the gauge parameters (minimizing $\KL(\bar{P}_g\|P_0)$)
and ``preserving predictive power'' (minimizing the edge KL divergence).
$\lambda$ controls the compression-prediction trade-off.
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Unification of the Three Formalization Domains}

\begin{theorem}[Unified Structure Theorem]\label{thm:unified}
The three formalization domains of SCX gauge theory — $O(d)$ lattice gauge, DW discrete TQFT, and information-geometric bulk-boundary —
are unified under the following structure:

\[
\begin{tikzcd}
    \text{Expert Data} \arrow[d, "\text{edge assignment}"] \arrow[dr, "\text{probabilistic modeling}"] \\
    \Omega^1(\grph; \Od) \arrow[d, "\text{gauge-fixing}"] \arrow[r, "\text{Lie algebra}"] & \mathcal{P}(\Situs) \arrow[d, "\text{Fisher metric}"] \\
    \Gflatquot \arrow[r, "\text{tangent space}"] & \ker(\Lone) \otimes \mathfrak{g} \arrow[r, "\text{bulk-boundary}"] & \GeoDist(P_A, \im(d_0))
\end{tikzcd}
\]

Specifically:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
    \item $O(d)$ lattice gauge provides the non-abelian geometric structure of edge assignments;
    \item DW TQFT interprets the gauge-fixed harmonic residual as a tangent vector to the flat connection moduli space;
    \item Information geometry reformulates Cercis as the Fisher geodesic distance from the boundary (observed distribution) to the pure-gauge bulk states ($\im(d_0)$),
    conditionally equivalent to $2\KL$ under the exponential family assumption (to second order). For non-exponential-family data, the relationship degrades via the Amari-Chentsov tensor (Open Problem~\ref{prob:non_exp_fam}).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Central invariant:} The three domains share a single topological invariant — the first Betti number $\ZBetti = (M-1)(M-2)/2$.
$\ZBetti$ is simultaneously:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The number of harmonic $1$-forms in $O(d)$ gauge-fixing (one per $\mathfrak{so}(d)$ basis element);
    \item The dimension of the flat connection moduli space in DW TQFT (or $\log_{|G|}Z_{\DW}$);
    \item The codimension of the pure-gauge bulk submanifold $\im(d_0)$ in information geometry.
\end{itemize}
\end{theorem}

\end{remark}

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
    \item \textbf{Consistency of discrete structures.}
    fiber\_bundle.tex correctly treats SCX data as discrete objects on a graph.
    This paper maintains that discrete perspective, but extends edge assignments from the vector space $\R^d$ to the group $\Od$,
    and extends the face structure from implicit cycles to explicit 2-cells.
    All objects remain discrete (no continuum limit or smooth structure is introduced),
    thus preserving one-to-one correspondence with SCX computation.

    \item \textbf{Rigor of Dijkgraaf-Witten TQFT.}
    DW TQFT is a mathematical theory rigorously established by Dijkgraaf and Witten in the 1990s~\cite{dijkgraaf1990}.
    Its definition on any finite 2-complex is completely rigorous, independent of any continuum limit.
    This paper merely substitutes the SCX 2-complex into the standard formulas of that theory, so mathematical rigor is inherited.
    The flat connection counting formula $Z_{\DW} = |\Hom(\pi_1, G)/G|$ is, in the finite group case, an exact integer equality
    involving no approximations or limits.

    \item \textbf{Rigor of information geometry.}
    Information geometry (Amari~\cite{amari2016}, Ay et al.~\cite{ay2017}) is a rigorously established branch of modern statistics.
    The definition of the Fisher metric, the properties of exponential families, and the conditional local equivalence $\GeoDist^2 = 2\KL + O(\Delta^3)$
    are standard results in information geometry (directly derivable from the Taylor expansion of the Bregman divergence).
    Its application to SCX's $\Situs$ manifold in this paper is a conditional generalization under specific assumptions.
    When these assumptions are not satisfied (especially the non-exponential-family case, Open Problem~\ref{prob:non_exp_fam}), the equivalence degrades via the Amari-Chentsov tensor.

    \item \textbf{Treatment of non-abelian Hodge decomposition.}
    Global existence of a solution to $O(d)$ gauge-fixing under large distortion is guaranteed by compactness of $\Od^{|\verts|}$ — a trivial topological argument.
    Linearization under small connections is guaranteed by the Cartan decomposition (Lemma~\ref{lem:cartan}) — a classical result in Lie theory.
    Interpolation between the two extremes is an \textit{open problem} (\ref{prob:moduli_space}), which we honestly mark as such,
    rather than pretending to have an unproven theorem. This honesty is precisely the key quality distinguishing this work from earlier efforts.

    \item \textbf{Computation of the first Betti number.}
    The proof of $\ZBetti = (M-1)(M-2)/2$ proceeds by contracting the SCX 2-complex to $K_M$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:scx_homotopy}).
    This contraction is a rigorous combinatorial deformation retract: each quadrilateral face provides a homotopy from the slice at configuration $k$ to the slice at configuration $k-1$.
    This is standard technique in combinatorial topology. The independence of the result from $N$ has an intuitive explanation:
    adding configuration samples corresponds to extending the complex in a contractible direction, which does not change the homotopy type.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Open Problems}
\label{sec:open}

The following problems are identified within the framework of this paper but remain unsolved. Each problem is annotated with the specific conditions required for its resolution.

\begin{problem}[Global Structure of $O(d)$ Gauge-Fixing under Large Distortion]\label{prob:moduli_space}
Classify all local minima (``gauge-fixing branches'') of $\mathcal{E}_{\Od}: \Od^{|\verts|} \to \R$ for general edge connections $A$.
In particular:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
    \item Is the number of local minima determined by the homotopy groups of $\Od$ (especially $\pi_1(\Od) \cong \Z_2$ for $d \geq 3$)?
    \item Is there a continuous branch structure analogous to the Gribov ambiguity?
\end{enumerate}
\textbf{Required:} Application of Morse theory on $\Od^{|\verts|}$ and Hessian analysis of $\mathcal{E}_{\Od}$.
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}[Fisher-KL Equivalence Outside Exponential Families]\label{prob:non_exp_fam}
The proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:fisher_kl_equivalence} relies on the exponential family assumption.
For general SCX output distributions (potentially non-exponential-family), what is the relationship between Fisher geodesic distance and KL divergence?
\textbf{Required:} For general statistical manifolds, the difference between $\GeoDist^2$ and $2\KL$ is controlled by the Amari-Chentsov third-order tensor (the torsion of the $\alpha$-connection).
Bounds on this tensor for empirical SCX output distributions are needed.
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}[Exact Value of DW Partition Function for Finite $N, M$]\label{prob:dw_finite}
Theorem~\ref{thm:dw_partition} gives a formula for $Z_{\DW}$, but the SCX 2-complex has a fixed finite size $(M, N)$.
For finite $G$, what is the exact numerical value of $Z_{\DW}(\mathcal{K}_{\text{SCX}}, G)$?
Does this value deviate from the limit formula $|G|^{\ZBetti}$ for small $M$ and $N$?
\textbf{Required:} Direct counting of flat connections for specific $(M, N, G)$, or application of the Mednykh formula~\cite{mednykh}.
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}[Global Geometry of the Harmonic Moduli Space]\label{prob:global_moduli}
Theorem~\ref{thm:harmonic_moduli_geometry} describes the tangent space to $\Gflatquot$ at the trivial connection.
What is the global geometry away from the trivial connection? Is $\Gflatquot$ smooth? Where are the singularities located?
\textbf{Required:} Geometric Invariant Theory (GIT) quotient analysis of the conjugation action of $\Od$ on $\Hom(\pi_1, \Od)$.
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}[Holographic Duality of the Bulk-Boundary Correspondence]\label{prob:holographic}
Theorem~\ref{thm:cercis_bulk_boundary} interprets Cercis as the distance from the boundary to bulk states.
Is there a deeper ``holographic duality''?
That is, does the Cercis distribution on the boundary completely determine certain global geometric quantities of the bulk?
\textbf{Required:} Establishing a discrete holographic principle on 2-complexes analogous to AdS/CFT.
This is currently speculative — discrete holographic duality has been rigorously established only in specific systems such as the SYK model~\cite{sachdev2010}.
\end{problem}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

\subsection{Contributions of This Paper}

This paper completes the formalization of SCX multi-expert gauge theory in three domains:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
    \item \textbf{$O(d)$ Lattice Gauge Theory (Section~\ref{sec:od}):}
    Establishes a complete non-abelian discrete gauge theory — $O(d)$-valued edge connections, face Wilson loop curvature,
    non-abelian gauge transformations, and the $O(d)$ distortion energy functional.
    Proves that under small connections, gauge-fixing reduces to standard graph Hodge decomposition on $\mathfrak{so}(d)$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:od_hodge_fix}),
    and under large distortion, existence of a solution is guaranteed by compactness.
    This allows the SCX framework, for the first time, to rigorously handle the rotational gauge degrees of freedom of experts (a core requirement for ACE potential comparison).

    \item \textbf{Dijkgraaf-Witten Discrete TQFT (Section~\ref{sec:dw}):}
    Constructs the expert graph as a 2-complex and defines a DW discrete TQFT on it.
    Proves that the SCX 2-complex is homotopy equivalent to $K_M$, with first Betti number $\ZBetti = (M-1)(M-2)/2$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:scx_homotopy}).
    Establishes the precise relationship between the DW partition function $Z_{\DW}$ and the flat connection moduli space $\Gflatquot$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:dw_partition}).
    Core result: \textbf{proves that the Cercis harmonic component $r_{\text{harm}}$ is a tangent vector to the flat connection moduli space $\Gflatquot$ at the trivial connection}
    (Theorem~\ref{thm:dw_cercis_correspondence}), thereby endowing the harmonic residual with precise moduli-space geometric meaning.

    \item \textbf{Information-Geometric Bulk-Boundary Correspondence (Section~\ref{sec:info}):}
    Introduces the Fisher information metric on the $\Situs$ manifold and models expert outputs as an exponential family.
    Proves the conditional equivalence between Fisher geodesic length and KL divergence under the exponential family assumption (Theorem~\ref{thm:fisher_kl_equivalence}), with the 8 prerequisite assumptions and failure conditions explicitly noted.
    Establishes the information-geometric interpretation of the Cercis Score:
    $\cercis = \min_{\text{pure-gauge bulk states}} (\text{Fisher geodesic distance from boundary to bulk})^2$
    $\approx 2\min_{\text{pure-gauge bulk states}} \KL(P_{\text{obs}} \| P_{\text{pure gauge}})$
    (Theorem~\ref{thm:cercis_bulk_boundary}).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Applications and Generalization Directions}

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
    \item \textbf{$O(d)$ gauge-fixing in ACE potential energy surface auditing.}
    When comparing predictions of multiple ACE potentials, the $O(d)$ Cercis Score $\cercis_{\Od}$ directly quantifies
    the inconsistency arising from different choices of ACE basis function principal axes. In high-throughput screening pipelines for materials science,
    this score can be used to automatically flag ``rotationally inconsistent'' expert pairs and trigger re-orthogonalization of ACE basis functions.

    \item \textbf{DW partition function for expert system complexity analysis.}
    $\ZBetti = (M-1)(M-2)/2$ provides an \emph{a priori} complexity indicator:
    before running any gauge-fixing, one can predict the number of potential ``inconsistency modes'' of the system.
    For a system of $M$ experts, the audit complexity grows as $O(M^2)$ (every expert pair must be compared),
    and the number of inconsistency modes also grows as $O(M^2)$ ($\ZBetti \sim M^2/2$).
    This means: as the number of experts increases, the number of inconsistency modes grows at the same asymptotic rate as the audit cost —
    the system does not become less auditable as it scales up.

    \item \textbf{Information-geometric Cercis for distributional experts (e.g., Bayesian neural networks).}
    For experts that output full posterior distributions (rather than point estimates),
    the KL-regularized Cercis (Theorem~\ref{thm:kl_regularized_cercis}) provides a natural comparison metric.
    In this case, the ``edge connection'' $A_e$ is the optimal transport map between two distributions,
    and Cercis is the sum of gauge-fixed residuals of optimal transport costs across all edges.

    \item \textbf{Numerical experiments on $\SOthree$ gauge-fixing for 3D ACE potentials.}
    For $d=3$, the Cartan radius of $\SOthree$ is $\pi$.
    ACE potential comparisons within this radius can be handled directly by Algorithm~\ref{def:od_algorithm}.
    For large rotations beyond the Cartan radius (e.g., ACE principal axis reversal),
    multiple starting points must be initialized and global optimization (e.g., simulated annealing) used to find the global minimum.
    This scenario corresponds to the ``Gribov ambiguity'' branch structure described in Theorem~\ref{thm:od_hodge_fix}.

    \item \textbf{Integration with persistent homology tools.}
    From the DW TQFT perspective, $\ZBetti$ is not only the dimension of the moduli space, but also the number of ``long bars'' in the $H_1$ persistence barcode.
    As the number of configurations $N$ in the SCX graph increases, persistent homology~\cite{edelsbrunner2008}
    can distinguish ``genuine harmonic modes'' (persisting across all $N$) from ``finite-size effects'' (existing only across some configurations).
    Weighting the Cercis harmonic component by persistence yields enhanced robustness.

    \item \textbf{Handling high-dimensional output spaces ($d > 3$).}
    For $d > 3$, the dimension of $\Od$ is $d(d-1)/2$, and the Cartan radius shrinks.
    Under conditions where the small-connection assumption is more likely to hold, the linear approximation of Algorithm~\ref{def:od_algorithm} becomes more accurate.
    However, for high-dimensional spaces, the geometric complexity of $\Od$ (great-circle distances, non-uniqueness of geodesics)
    may cause practical convergence difficulties, requiring stable numerical implementations of the logarithmic map on $\Od$.

    \item \textbf{Joint use with $\Situs$ physical position encoding.}
    When the Fisher metric on $\Situs$ degenerates to the Euclidean metric (corresponding to the Gaussian family),
    the information-geometric Cercis (Theorem~\ref{thm:cercis_bulk_boundary}) reduces to the Euclidean Cercis of fiber\_bundle.tex.
    The difference between the two directly measures the non-Gaussianity of the expert output distributions —
    an independently verifiable statistic.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Worked Example: Three-Expert ACE PES Comparison}

To illustrate the practical application of this paper's formalization framework, consider the following concrete scenario:

\textbf{Setting:} $M=3$ experts (ACE potentials), $N=100$ configurations (snapshots from a molecular dynamics trajectory),
output space dimension $d=3$ (energy, $x$-component of force, $y$-component of force). Each expert returns a 3-dimensional vector.

\textbf{Step 1 — Build the SCX 2-complex:}
Number of vertices $|\verts| = 300$, number of edges $|\edgs| = 3(99) + 100 \times 3 \times 2 = 297 + 600 = 897$,
number of faces $|\faces| = 99 \times \binom{3}{2} = 99 \times 3 = 297$.
First Betti number $\ZBetti = (3-1)(3-2)/2 = 1$ — exactly one independent inconsistency mode.

\textbf{Step 2 — Edge assignments:}
Parameter edges: $a_{(k,m) \to (k+1,m)} = x_m^{k+1} - x_m^k \in \R^3$ (translational component),
or rotational component $R_{(k,m) \to (k+1,m)} \in \SOthree$ (if rotational gauge is considered).
Expert edges: $a_{(k,i) \to (k,j)} = x_i^k - x_j^k$ (or the corresponding rotation).

\textbf{Step 3 — Gauge-fixing:}
Translation gauge: build $B \in \R^{897 \times 300}$, solve $B^T B g = B^T A$ (solved separately for 3 coordinates),
with zero-mode fixing $\sum_v g_v = 0$.
Obtain the Cercis translational score $\cercis_{\R^3}$.

Rotation gauge (if applicable): run Algorithm~\ref{def:od_algorithm} on $\SOthree^{300}$.
Obtain the Cercis rotational score $\cercis_{\SOthree}$.
Total Cercis: $\cercis_{\SEN} = \cercis_{\R^3} + \cercis_{\SOthree}$.

\textbf{Step 4 — Harmonic analysis:}
Dimension of the Cercis harmonic component $r_{\text{harm}}$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Translational part: $3 \times 1 = 3$ dimensions (3 coordinate directions, 1 independent cycle mode);
    \item Rotational part: $3 \times 1 = 3$ dimensions (3 basis elements of $\mathfrak{so}(3)$, 1 independent cycle mode).
\end{itemize}
Total of 6 independent harmonic directions — 6 essentially different ``expert inconsistency modes.''

\textbf{Step 5 — DW interpretation:}
If displacements are discretized (e.g., rounded to a $\Z_{10}$ lattice), the DW partition function is
$Z_{\DW}(\mathcal{K}_{\text{SCX}}, \Z_{10}) = 10^1 = 10$ (translation) or
$Z_{\DW} = |\SOthree_{\text{discrete}}|^1$ (rotation, depending on discretization precision).
These 10 (or more) states correspond to 10 gauge-inequivalent global inconsistency modes.

\textbf{Step 6 — Information-geometric interpretation:}
Model the output of each expert $m$ at each configuration $k$ as $\mathcal{N}(x_m^k, \Sigma_m^k)$ (Gaussian).
The Fisher metric degenerates to the Mahalanobis distance. Cercis is equivalent to the minimal sum of squared Mahalanobis distances
from the observed distribution to the ``perfectly aligned'' distribution.

\subsection{Final Remark}

The gauge structure of SCX multi-expert systems ultimately reduces to three interrelated mathematical objects:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item Non-abelian $O(d)$ lattice gauge fields on the expert comparison graph (describing rotational degrees of freedom);
    \item Dijkgraaf-Witten TQFT on a 2-complex (describing the flat connection moduli space and explaining the harmonic component);
    \item Fisher-Rao metric on the $\Situs$ information manifold (providing a probabilistic interpretation of Cercis and the bulk-boundary correspondence).
\end{enumerate}

These three structures answer the following questions, respectively:
\begin{itemize}
    \item ``How are the rotational reference frames of experts globally aligned?'' — $O(d)$ lattice gauge-fixing
    \item ``How many essentially different ways can an expert system be inconsistent?'' — DW moduli space $\Gflatquot$, dimension $(M-1)(M-2)/2$
    \item ``What does the Cercis Score mean probabilistically?'' — Minimal information distance to a globally consistent distribution
\end{itemize}

We believe this formalization provides a solid mathematical foundation for SCX gauge theory,
where every assertion has a clear definition and a verifiable mathematical status.
