\begin{abstract}
\noindent
This paper grounds the gauge theory of the SCX (Self-Consistent eXpert) multi-expert system
in discrete Hodge theory on graphs, abandoning the continuous fiber bundle preamble entirely
in favor of the discrete mathematical structures that the actual computation employs.
We treat pairwise expert displacements as edge assignments $A_e$ on a directed graph
$\grph = (\verts, \edgs)$, define curvature as holonomy around elementary loops,
and formalize gauge-fixing as the least-squares problem of finding vertex potentials $g_v$
that minimize the residual $\sum_{e} \|A_e - (g_w - g_v)\|^2$.
We carefully distinguish the {\bf zero-mode fixing} condition $\sum_v g_v = 0$
(which removes the overall translation degree of freedom to ensure solution uniqueness)
from the continuous Coulomb gauge $\partial_\mu A^\mu = 0$ (a divergence constraint on the connection),
and we acknowledge upfront that the bundle is topologically trivial
($G \cong \R^{Md}$ contractible, base contractible, all Chern classes vanish).
The Cercis Score is defined as the {\bf residual norm after gauge-fixing} —
the minimized objective value under the optimal vertex potentials — directly matching
the actual SCX implementation.
This framework is self-contained, rigorously defined, and performs the identical
computation as the SCX code line-by-line.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction: From Continuous Pretense to Discrete Reality}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{The Core Problem}

In the multi-expert SCX framework, we have $M$ experts, each producing predictions
on a set of $N$ parameter configurations $\{x^1, \dots, x^N\} \subset \mathcal{X}$.
The raw output of expert $m$ at configuration $k$ is denoted $\tilde{x}_m^k \in \R^d$.

The core problem is this: since each expert possesses an independent translational
degree of freedom (the freedom to choose its own coordinate origin),
the raw coordinates $\tilde{x}_m^k$ are not physically meaningful quantities —
they depend on the ``gauge'' privately chosen by each expert.
The difference $\delta_{ij}^k := \tilde{x}_i^k - \tilde{x}_j^k$ between experts $i$
and $j$ at the same configuration $k$ reflects both genuine physical differences
and the arbitrariness of their respective gauge choices.

Two fundamental questions drive the entire analysis of this paper:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item {\bf Global Alignment Problem:}
        Does there exist a set of vertex translations $\{g_m^k\}$ such that the
        aligned coordinates $\hat{x}_m^k = \tilde{x}_m^k - g_m^k$ satisfy some
        consistency condition for every pair $(i,j)$ across all configurations $k$?

    \item {\bf Inconsistency Quantification Problem:}
        When perfect global alignment is impossible, how can we objectively
        (i.e., independent of any particular gauge choice) quantify
        the degree of inconsistency in the multi-expert system?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Why Not Continuous Fiber Bundles}

Previous work attempted to embed the above problem into the framework of continuous
differential geometry — principal fiber bundles, Ehresmann connections,
curvature $2$-forms, etc. However, this embedding suffers from several fundamental
difficulties:

\begin{enumerate}[label=F\arabic*:, leftmargin=*]
    \item {\bf The connection was never constructed.}
        The Ehresmann connection $\omega$ in the continuous framework was never
        explicitly constructed from actual expert data. The text merely asserts
        that such a connection exists, without showing how the edge assignments
        $A_e$ emerge from discrete data as a discretization of a continuous
        connection. An object never constructed cannot serve as the foundation
        of a theory.

    \item {\bf Curvature definitions are incompatible.}
        Even within the continuous framework, different sections define curvature
        inconsistently: one defines the Cercis Score as the Yang-Mills functional
        $\int \|F\|^2$, while another (the actual SCX computation) defines it as
        $Q + \eta N$ — an entirely different quantity. The two are never proven
        equivalent.

    \item {\bf The topology is trivial.}
        The structure group $G \cong \R^{Md}$ is contractible, and the base
        manifold $\mathcal{X}$ is also contractible (a subset of parameter space).
        Hence all Chern classes and all cohomological obstructions vanish identically.
        The principal bundle $\bun \cong \R^{Md} \times \mathcal{X}$ is globally
        trivial. There is no topological content — all content is geometric
        (flat vs.\ non-flat connections).

    \item {\bf Coulomb gauge is misidentified.}
        The constraint $\sum_m g_m = 0$ is repeatedly called a ``discrete Coulomb
        gauge'' or a ``discrete analog of the Coulomb condition.'' But it is not
        a Coulomb gauge. The Coulomb gauge is a divergence constraint on the
        {\bf gauge potential}: $\partial_\mu A^\mu = 0$. The condition
        $\sum_m g_m = 0$ is a linear constraint on the {\bf gauge parameters},
        whose sole function is to fix the zero-mode (overall translation degree
        of freedom) to ensure uniqueness of the solution. The two differ in
        mathematical structure, physical meaning, and geometric location.
\end{enumerate}

In light of these difficulties, this paper takes an entirely different path:
{\bf we work directly in discrete Hodge theory on graphs.}
This is the mathematics that SCX code actually performs — we are simply making it
explicit, honest, and systematic.

\subsection{Roadmap of This Paper}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Section~2 develops the basic tools of discrete Hodge theory on graphs:
        edge assignments, exterior derivatives $d_0, d_1$, Hodge decomposition,
        and graph Laplacians.
    \item Section~3 constructs the SCX graph: mapping expert-configuration
        comparisons to edge assignments on a directed graph, defining curvature
        as holonomy around elementary loops.
    \item Section~4 formalizes the gauge-fixing problem: least-squares fitting of
        vertex potentials, distinguishing zero-mode fixing ($\sum g_v = 0$)
        from Coulomb gauge.
    \item Section~5 gives the correct definition of the Cercis Score:
        the residual norm after gauge-fixing, and proves gauge invariance.
    \item Section~6 acknowledges topological triviality and explains why this
        does not diminish the theory's content.
    \item Section~7 provides numerical algorithms.
    \item Section~8 concludes.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discrete Hodge Theory on Graphs}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

This section establishes a self-contained toolbox of discrete Hodge theory on graphs.
All subsequent constructions — the SCX gauge graph, curvature computation,
gauge-fixing, and Cercis residual — are built upon this foundation.
No knowledge of continuous differential geometry is required.

\subsection{Directed Graphs}

\begin{definition}[Directed Graph]
A {\bf directed graph} $\grph = (\verts, \edgs)$ consists of:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\verts$: a finite set of vertices, $|\verts| = n$;
    \item $\edgs \subseteq \verts \times \verts$: a set of directed edges, $|\edgs| = m$.
\end{itemize}
Each directed edge $e = (u \to v)$ is an ordered pair from source vertex $u$ to
target vertex $v$. We allow multiple edges between the same pair of vertices
but not self-loops ($u \neq v$).
\end{definition}

\subsection{Discrete Differential Forms: Vertex Functions and Edge Assignments}

The discrete exterior calculus on graphs relates scalar functions ($0$-forms)
to edge assignments ($1$-forms).

\begin{definition}[Vertex Space and Edge Space]
Define the following real vector spaces:
\begin{itemize}
    \item {\bf $0$-form space} $\Omega^0(\grph)$: all vertex functions $f: \verts \to \R$,
        dimension $n$. Denoted as vector $\mathbf{f} = (f_1, \dots, f_n)^T \in \R^n$.
    \item {\bf $1$-form space} $\Omega^1(\grph)$: all edge assignments $\alpha: \edgs \to \R$,
        dimension $m$. Denoted as vector $\boldsymbol{\alpha} = (\alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_m)^T \in \R^m$.
\end{itemize}
For $d$-dimensional vector-valued forms, we take the direct sum of $d$ copies:
$\Omega^k(\grph; \R^d) := \bigoplus_{a=1}^d \Omega^k(\grph)$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Discrete Exterior Derivatives}

\begin{definition}[Incidence Matrix and $d_0$]
The {\bf incidence matrix} $B \in \R^{m \times n}$ of $\grph$ is defined by:
for each edge $e = (u \to v)$ and each vertex $w$,
\[
    B_{e,w} = 
    \begin{cases}
        +1, & \text{if } w = v \text{ (target)}, \\
        -1, & \text{if } w = u \text{ (source)}, \\
        0,  & \text{otherwise}.
    \end{cases}
\]

The {\bf discrete exterior derivative} $d_0: \Omega^0(\grph) \to \Omega^1(\grph)$
is defined as $d_0 := B$, i.e., for $f \in \Omega^0(\grph)$ and edge $e = (u \to v)$:
\[
    (d_0 f)_e = f_v - f_u.
\]
In words, $d_0 f$ computes the difference of $f$ along each edge.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Discrete Exterior Derivative $d_1$ on Loops]
Let $\mathcal{L}$ be a set of {\bf elementary loops} of $\grph$
(e.g., the cycle basis determined by the complement edges of a spanning tree).
Let $C \in \R^{|\mathcal{L}| \times m}$ be the {\bf cycle matrix},
where for each loop $\gamma \in \mathcal{L}$ and each edge $e \in \edgs$:
\[
    C_{\gamma,e} = 
    \begin{cases}
        +1, & \text{if edge } e \text{ follows the positive direction of loop } \gamma, \\
        -1, & \text{if edge } e \text{ follows the negative direction of loop } \gamma, \\
        0,  & \text{otherwise}.
    \end{cases}
\]

The {\bf discrete exterior derivative} $d_1: \Omega^1(\grph) \to \Omega^2(\grph)$
is defined as $d_1 := C$, where $\Omega^2(\grph) \cong \R^{|\mathcal{L}|}$ is the loop space.
For $\alpha \in \Omega^1(\grph)$ and loop $\gamma = (e_1, e_2, \dots, e_k)$:
\[
    (d_1 \alpha)_\gamma = \sum_{e \in \gamma} \sigma_e \, \alpha_e,
\]
where $\sigma_e \in \{+1, -1\}$ is the sign of edge $e$ relative to the loop orientation.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Fundamental Identity: $d_1 \circ d_0 = 0$]
\label{thm:d1d0}
The incidence and cycle matrices satisfy:
\[
    d_1 \circ d_0 = C B = 0.
\]
Equivalently, $\im(d_0) \subseteq \ker(d_1)$.
This is the discrete analog of $\dif^2 = 0$ in the continuous setting.

{\bf Proof.} For any $f \in \Omega^0(\grph)$ and any loop
$\gamma = (v_0 \to v_1 \to \cdots \to v_k = v_0)$:
\[
    (d_1 d_0 f)_\gamma = \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} (f_{v_{i+1}} - f_{v_i}) = f_{v_k} - f_{v_0} = 0,
\]
since the sum telescopes and $v_k = v_0$. $\square$
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Discrete Hodge Decomposition}

To make the exterior derivatives part of a genuine Hodge theory, we need inner products and adjoint operators.

\begin{definition}[Inner Products and Adjoint Operators]
Define the standard inner product on $\Omega^0(\grph)$ (with vertex weights
$\mathbf{w}_0 \in \R^n_{>0}$):
\[
    \langle f, g \rangle_0 := \sum_{v \in \verts} w_{0,v} \, f_v \, g_v.
\]
On $\Omega^1(\grph)$ (with edge weights $\mathbf{w}_1 \in \R^m_{>0}$):
\[
    \langle \alpha, \beta \rangle_1 := \sum_{e \in \edgs} w_{1,e} \, \alpha_e \, \beta_e.
\]

The {\bf formal adjoint} of $d_0$ with respect to these inner products is
$\dzeroT := d_0^*: \Omega^1(\grph) \to \Omega^0(\grph)$, determined by:
\[
    \langle d_0 f, \alpha \rangle_1 = \langle f, \dzeroT \alpha \rangle_0,
    \quad \forall f \in \Omega^0(\grph), \; \alpha \in \Omega^1(\grph).
\]

Explicitly, $\dzeroT = W_0^{-1} B^T W_1$, where
$W_0 = \diag(\mathbf{w}_0)$, $W_1 = \diag(\mathbf{w}_1)$.
In the unweighted case ($W_0 = I_n$, $W_1 = I_m$), $\dzeroT = B^T$, whose action
on vertex $v$ is:
\[
    (\dzeroT \alpha)_v = \sum_{e: \text{incoming to } v} \alpha_e \;-\; \sum_{e: \text{outgoing from } v} \alpha_e.
\]
This is precisely the {\bf discrete divergence} at $v$ (incoming minus outgoing).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Graph Laplacians]
The {\bf graph $0$-Laplacian} (combinatorial Laplace--Beltrami operator) is:
\[
    \Lzero := \dzeroT d_0 = W_0^{-1} B^T W_1 B.
\]
In the unweighted case, $\Lzero = B^T B$ is the classical combinatorial Laplacian:
\[
    (\Lzero)_{uv} = 
    \begin{cases}
        \deg(v), & u = v, \\
        -1,      & (u, v) \text{ or } (v, u) \in \edgs, \\
        0,       & \text{otherwise}.
    \end{cases}
\]

The {\bf graph $1$-Laplacian} (full Hodge Laplacian) is:
\[
    \Lone := d_0 \dzeroT + \doneT d_1 = B W_0^{-1} B^T W_1 + C^T W_2 C W_1^{-1},
\]
where $W_2$ is an (optional) weight matrix on the loop space $\Omega^2(\grph)$.
In the unweighted case this reduces to $\Lone = B B^T + C^T C$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Discrete Hodge Decomposition]
\label{thm:hodge_decomp}
For any connected graph $\grph$, $\Omega^1(\grph)$ admits the orthogonal decomposition:
\[
    \Omega^1(\grph) = \im(d_0) \oplus \ker(\Lone) \oplus \im(\doneT),
\]
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\im(d_0)$: {\bf exact $1$-forms} (image of gradient) — expressible as differences of a vertex function;
    \item $\ker(\Lone) = \ker(d_1) \cap \ker(\dzeroT)$: {\bf harmonic $1$-forms} — both divergence-free and curl-free;
    \item $\im(\doneT)$: {\bf coexact $1$-forms} (image of discrete co-differential).
\end{itemize}

Equivalently, every edge assignment $\alpha \in \Omega^1(\grph)$ has a unique decomposition:
\[
    \alpha = d_0 f + h + \doneT \beta,
\]
where $f \in \Omega^0(\grph)$, $h \in \ker(\Lone)$, $\beta \in \Omega^2(\grph)$.
The component $d_0 f$ is called the {\bf gradient part},
$h$ the {\bf harmonic part}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[On the Two Parts of the $1$-Laplacian]
The full Hodge $1$-Laplacian $\Lone = d_0 \dzeroT + \doneT d_1$ has two parts:
\begin{itemize}
    \item {\bf Down part} $d_0 \dzeroT = B B^T$ (unweighted): appears in the normal equations
        and gauge-fixing problem. $\ker(d_0 \dzeroT) = \ker(\dzeroT)$.
    \item {\bf Up part} $\doneT d_1 = C^T C$: encodes loop constraints. $\ker(\doneT d_1) = \ker(d_1)$.
\end{itemize}
Only the full Laplacian satisfies $\ker(\Lone) = \ker(d_1) \cap \ker(\dzeroT)$ — the space
of harmonic $1$-forms. The down part alone has kernel $\ker(\dzeroT)$ (co-closed forms),
without the $\ker(d_1)$ condition. SCX's computation (normal equations $B^T B g = B^T A$)
uses only the down part $B^T B$, but the harmonic decomposition (Theorem~\ref{thm:hodge_decomp})
and identification of the harmonic residual component require the full Laplacian.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[When Is a $1$-Form Exact?]
$\alpha \in \Omega^1(\grph)$ is exact (i.e., exists $f$ with $\alpha = d_0 f$)
iff the sum of $\alpha$ along {\bf all} loops $\gamma$ vanishes:
\[
    \alpha = d_0 f \;\Longleftrightarrow\; d_1 \alpha = 0 \;\Longleftrightarrow\;
    \sum_{e \in \gamma} \sigma_e \alpha_e = 0 \quad \forall \gamma \in \mathcal{L}.
\]
This is the discrete Poincaré lemma: a closed $1$-form (curl-free) on a connected
graph is exact {\bf iff the graph is a tree}. If the graph has cycles,
$\ker(d_1)$ is strictly larger than $\im(d_0)$, the difference being
the space of harmonic $1$-forms.
\end{remark}

\section{The SCX Graph: Construction and Curvature}

\subsection{The Expert Comparison Graph}

The raw data of SCX computation is the set of all pairwise expert displacements.
We organize this data as edge assignments on a directed graph.

\begin{definition}[SCX Expert Comparison Graph]
The SCX expert comparison graph $\grph_{\text{SCX}} = (\verts, \edgs)$ is constructed as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item {\bf Vertex set $\verts$:} Each vertex corresponds to a (configuration, expert) pair:
        \[
            \verts = \{(k, m) \mid k \in \{1, \dots, N\},\; m \in \{1, \dots, M\}\}.
        \]
        There are $|\verts| = N \cdot M$ vertices.

    \item {\bf Edge set $\edgs$:} Two types:
        \begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
            \item {\bf Parameter edges:}
                \[
                    \edgs_{\text{par}} = \{(k, m) \to (k+1, m) \mid k = 1,\dots,N-1,\; m = 1,\dots,M\}.
                \]
                Representing the movement of the same expert along the parameter axis
                from configuration $k$ to $k+1$. Each parameter edge is assigned the
                change in expert $m$'s prediction between adjacent configurations.

            \item {\bf Expert edges:}
                \[
                    \edgs_{\text{exp}} = \{(k, i) \to (k, j) \mid k = 1,\dots,N,\; i,j = 1,\dots,M,\; i \neq j\}.
                \]
                Representing the displacement from expert $i$'s prediction to expert $j$'s
                prediction at the same configuration $k$. Each expert edge is assigned the
                pairwise expert displacement $\delta_{ij}^k = \tilde{x}_i^k - \tilde{x}_j^k$.
        \end{enumerate}

    \item {\bf Total edge set:} $\edgs = \edgs_{\text{par}} \cup \edgs_{\text{exp}}$.
        The graph $\grph_{\text{SCX}}$ is a directed multigraph (multiple distinct edges
        may connect the same pair of vertices).
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\subsection{Edge Assignments: Pairwise Expert Displacements}

\begin{definition}[SCX Edge Assignment $A$]
Define the edge assignment map $A: \edgs \to \R^d$ as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item For parameter edges $e = (k, m) \to (k+1, m)$:
        \[
            A_e = \tilde{x}_m^{k+1} - \tilde{x}_m^k \;\in\; \R^d.
        \]
        (The change in expert $m$'s prediction between configurations $k$ and $k+1$.)

    \item For expert edges $e = (k, i) \to (k, j)$:
        \[
            A_e = \delta_{ij}^k = \tilde{x}_i^k - \tilde{x}_j^k \;\in\; \R^d.
        \]
        (The pairwise displacement from expert $i$ to expert $j$ at configuration $k$.)
\end{itemize}

$A \in \Omega^1(\grph_{\text{SCX}}; \R^d)$ is the $d$-dimensional vector-valued
graph $1$-form — the edge assignment in each coordinate dimension.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Edge Assignments Are Direct Data, Not Derived]
Crucially: $A$ is not ``pulled back'' or ``discretized'' from some continuous
connection $\omega$. $A$ {\bf is} the raw data — the collection of pairwise expert
displacements, directly encoded as edge assignments on a graph.
The continuous fiber bundle framework is superfluous here, and even harmful,
because it suggests constructions that do not exist.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Gauge Transformations as Vertex Functions}

\begin{definition}[Vertex Potential (Gauge Parameter)]
A {\bf vertex potential} (or {\bf gauge parameter}) is a function
$g: \verts \to \R^d$, assigning to each (configuration, expert) pair a
translation vector $g_m^k \in \R^d$. Physically, $g_m^k$ is the gauge translation
applied to expert $m$ at configuration $k$:
the aligned coordinates are $\hat{x}_m^k = \tilde{x}_m^k - g_m^k$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Gauge Transformation of Edge Assignments]
Under a gauge transformation by vertex potential $g$, the edge assignment $A$
transforms as: for each edge $e = (u \to v)$, where $u = (k, i)$, $v = (\ell, j)$:
\[
    A'_e = A_e - (g_v - g_u) = A_e - (d_0 g)_e.
\]
i.e., $A' = A - d_0 g$.
This is the discrete exact analog of the continuous abelian gauge transformation
$A'_\mu = A_\mu - \partial_\mu \Lambda$.

{\bf Proof.} After alignment, expert $i$ at configuration $k$ has coordinate
$\tilde{x}_i^k - g_i^k$. The transformed edge assignment is:
\[
    A'_e = (\tilde{x}_j^\ell - g_j^\ell) - (\tilde{x}_i^k - g_i^k)
         = (\tilde{x}_j^\ell - \tilde{x}_i^k) - (g_j^\ell - g_i^k)
         = A_e - (d_0 g)_e. \quad \square
\]
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Curvature: Holonomy around Elementary Loops}

\begin{definition}[SCX Elementary Loops]
The {\bf elementary quadrilateral loops} of $\grph_{\text{SCX}}$ have the form:
for each adjacent configuration pair $k, k+1$ and each distinct expert pair $i, j$ ($i \neq j$):
\[
    \gamma_{k,i,j} := (k,i) \xrightarrow{\text{expert}} (k,j)
                    \xrightarrow{\text{param}} (k{+}1,j)
                    \xrightarrow{\text{expert}} (k{+}1,i)
                    \xrightarrow{\text{param}} (k,i).
\]
Note: the expert edge from $(k{+}1,j)$ to $(k{+}1,i)$ is reversed ($j \to i$),
and the parameter edge from $(k{+}1,i)$ to $(k,i)$ is also reversed.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Discrete Curvature: Holonomy around Loops]
The {\bf discrete curvature} ({\bf holonomy}) around the quadrilateral loop
$\gamma_{k,i,j}$ is the algebraic sum of edge assignments along the loop
(accounting for edge direction):
\[
\begin{aligned}
    \curv(\gamma_{k,i,j})
    &:= \sum_{e \in \gamma_{k,i,j}} \sigma_e \, A_e \\
    &= A_{(k,i) \to (k,j)} + A_{(k,j) \to (k+1,j)} \\
    &\quad + A_{(k+1,j) \to (k+1,i)} + A_{(k+1,i) \to (k,i)}.
\end{aligned}
\]

Substituting the definitions of edge assignments:
\[
\begin{aligned}
    \curv(\gamma_{k,i,j})
    &= \delta_{ij}^k \;+\; (\tilde{x}_j^{k+1} - \tilde{x}_j^k) \\
    &\quad +\; \delta_{ji}^{k+1} \;+\; (\tilde{x}_i^k - \tilde{x}_i^{k+1}) \\
    &= (\tilde{x}_i^k - \tilde{x}_j^k) + (\tilde{x}_j^{k+1} - \tilde{x}_j^k)
       + (\tilde{x}_j^{k+1} - \tilde{x}_i^{k+1}) + (\tilde{x}_i^k - \tilde{x}_i^{k+1}).
\end{aligned}
\]

Simplifying:
\[
    \curv(\gamma_{k,i,j}) = (\tilde{x}_i^k - \tilde{x}_i^{k+1}) - (\tilde{x}_j^k - \tilde{x}_j^{k+1}).
\]

{\bf Geometric interpretation:} $\curv(\gamma_{k,i,j})$ measures the
{\bf difference} in how expert $i$'s and expert $j$'s predictions change
between parameters $k$ and $k+1$. If the two experts' predictions change
in exactly the same way under the parameter variation, the curvature
of this quadrilateral loop is zero.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Gauge Invariance of Curvature]
\label{prop:curv_gauge_inv}
Discrete curvature $\curv(\gamma)$ is gauge-invariant:
for any vertex potential $g$, $\curv'(\gamma) = \curv(\gamma)$.

{\bf Proof.} For any loop $\gamma$,
$\curv'(\gamma) = \sum_e \sigma_e A'_e
= \sum_e \sigma_e (A_e - (d_0 g)_e)
= \curv(\gamma) - \sum_e \sigma_e (d_0 g)_e$.
But $\sum_e \sigma_e (d_0 g)_e = (d_1 d_0 g)_\gamma = 0$ (by Theorem~\ref{thm:d1d0}, $d_1 d_0 = 0$).
Thus $\curv'(\gamma) = \curv(\gamma)$. $\square$

This is the discrete analog of the gauge invariance of
$F_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu$ in continuous abelian theory.
\end{proposition}

\begin{definition}[Curvature Vector]
Collect all elementary quadrilateral curvatures into a single vector
$\boldsymbol{\kappa} \in \R^{|\mathcal{L}| \times d}$ (since each loop has curvature in $d$ coordinate dimensions),
or $\boldsymbol{\kappa} \in \Omega^2(\grph_{\text{SCX}}; \R^d)$.
Formally, for each coordinate $a = 1, \dots, d$:
\[
    \boldsymbol{\kappa}_a = d_1 A_a = C \, \mathbf{A}_a,
\]
where $\mathbf{A}_a \in \R^m$ is the vector of edge assignments in the $a$-th coordinate,
and $C$ is the cycle matrix.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Flatness Condition}

\begin{theorem}[SCX Flatness Criterion]
\label{thm:flatness}
The following are equivalent:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item $\curv(\gamma) = 0$ for all elementary loops $\gamma \in \mathcal{L}$;
    \item The edge assignment $A$ is exact: there exists a vertex potential $g$ such that $A = d_0 g$;
    \item Global gauge alignment exists: the aligned coordinates
        $\hat{x}_m^k = \tilde{x}_m^k - g_m^k$
        make all expert edge assignments vanish (i.e., all experts output the same coordinates
        at the same configuration).
\end{enumerate}

{\bf Proof.} $(i) \Leftrightarrow (ii)$ by discrete Hodge theory ($d_1 A = 0 \Rightarrow A \in \ker(d_1)$,
but note that when the graph has cycles, $\ker(d_1) = \im(d_0) \oplus \ker(\Lone)$.
That $d_1 A = 0$ for all {\it quadrilateral} loops guarantees $A$ has no quadrilateral loop component.
If the graph is generated solely by quadrilateral loops (as in the SCX graph), this is equivalent to
$A \in \ker(d_1)$. However, if the harmonic component is non-zero, $A$ may still satisfy
$d_1 A = 0$ without being exact. For the specific topology of the SCX graph, we analyze
this subtlety below.)

$(ii) \Rightarrow (iii)$: If $A = d_0 g$, then for an expert edge $e = (k,i) \to (k,j)$:
$A_e = g_j^k - g_i^k$. But $A_e = \delta_{ij}^k = \tilde{x}_i^k - \tilde{x}_j^k$.
Thus $\tilde{x}_i^k - \tilde{x}_j^k = g_j^k - g_i^k$,
i.e., $\tilde{x}_i^k - g_i^k = \tilde{x}_j^k - g_j^k$.
Therefore the aligned coordinates satisfy $\hat{x}_i^k = \hat{x}_j^k$ for all $i,j,k$.

$(iii) \Rightarrow (ii)$: Reverse the argument. $\square$
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Non-Flatness and PES Misalignment}

\begin{theorem}[Discrete Characterization of PES Misalignment]
\label{thm:pes_misalignment}
If there exists an elementary loop $\gamma$ with $\curv(\gamma) \neq 0$
(i.e., the edge assignment $A$ is not exact),
then no vertex potential $g$ can simultaneously
zero all expert edge assignments.
In other words, no single global gauge choice can perfectly
align all experts.
This is the discrete formulation of {\bf PES misalignment} (potential energy surface misalignment).

More generally, $\curv(\gamma) \neq 0$ implies:
there exists at least one pair of experts $(i,j)$ and one pair of adjacent configurations $(k, k+1)$
such that expert $i$'s and expert $j$'s prediction changes between $(k \to k+1)$ are inconsistent.
The geometric picture: two potential energy surfaces bend differently in parameter space.
\end{theorem}

\section{Gauge-Fixing: Least Squares and the Zero-Mode}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Gauge-Fixing as a Least-Squares Problem}

When curvature is non-zero, there is no vertex potential $g$ satisfying $A = d_0 g$ exactly.
We can then seek a ``best approximation'' — a vertex potential that minimizes
the $L^2$ distance between $A$ and its exact approximation $d_0 g$.
This is the discrete formalization of {\bf gauge-fixing}.

\begin{definition}[Least-Squares Gauge-Fixing Problem]
Given edge assignment $A \in \Omega^1(\grph_{\text{SCX}}; \R^d)$,
find vertex potential $g: \verts \to \R^d$ minimizing the squared residual norm:
\[
    \min_{g \in \R^{n \times d}} \; \mathcal{R}[g] := \sum_{e \in \edgs} \|A_e - (d_0 g)_e\|^2.
\]

where $\|\cdot\|$ is the Euclidean norm on $\R^d$.
Expanding the objective:
\[
    \mathcal{R}[g] = \sum_{a=1}^{d} \sum_{e \in \edgs} (A_{e,a} - (d_0 g_a)_e)^2
    = \sum_{a=1}^{d} \|\mathbf{A}_a - B \mathbf{g}_a\|^2,
\]
where $\mathbf{A}_a \in \R^m$ is the vector of edge assignments in the $a$-th coordinate,
$\mathbf{g}_a \in \R^n$ is the $a$-th coordinate of gauge parameters on all vertices,
and $B \in \R^{m \times n}$ is the incidence matrix of $\grph_{\text{SCX}}$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Normal Equations and the Zero-Mode}

\begin{proposition}[Normal Equations]
\label{prop:normal_eq}
The optimality condition for $\min_g \|\mathbf{A} - B\mathbf{g}\|^2$ is the normal equation:
\[
    B^T B \, \mathbf{g} = B^T \mathbf{A}.
\]
i.e., (for each coordinate dimension independently):
\[
    \Lzero \, \mathbf{g} = \dzeroT \mathbf{A},
\]
where $\Lzero = B^T B$ is the graph Laplacian (unweighted case).
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}[The Zero-Mode: Non-Uniqueness of Solution]
The graph Laplacian $\Lzero = B^T B$ is always singular (for a connected graph,
$\ker(\Lzero) = \operatorname{span}(\mathbf{1})$, the all-ones vector).
Its physical interpretation: if $g$ is an optimal gauge parameter, then
$g + c$ (adding the same constant translation $c \in \R^d$ to all experts and configurations)
gives the identical residual, since $(d_0(g + c))_e = (d_0 g)_e + (c - c) = (d_0 g)_e$.

This non-uniqueness — called the {\bf zero-mode} — corresponds to the
``overall translation'' degree of freedom shared by all experts.
It carries no physical content (all pairwise displacements are invariant under this transformation),
but it makes the least-squares problem ill-posed and must be fixed numerically.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Zero-Mode Fixing: $\sum g_v = 0$ (Not Coulomb Gauge!)}

We now arrive at a critical point of clarification.

\begin{theorem}[$\sum_v g_v = 0$ Is Zero-Mode Fixing, Not Coulomb Gauge]
\label{thm:zero_mode}
The constraint
\[
    \sum_{v \in \verts} g_v = 0
\]
(or equivalently $\langle g, \mathbf{1} \rangle = 0$, i.e., $g \perp \mathbf{1}$)
serves to {\bf eliminate the zero-mode to ensure solution uniqueness}.
It is {\bf not} the discrete analog of the continuous Coulomb gauge
$\partial_\mu A^\mu = 0$.

{\bf Reasons:}
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
    \item {\bf Different object being constrained.}
        $\sum g_v = 0$ is a linear constraint on the {\bf gauge parameter} $g \in \Omega^0$.
        Coulomb gauge $\partial_\mu A^\mu = 0$ is a divergence constraint on the
        {\bf gauge potential} $A \in \Omega^1$.
        The former lives in $\Omega^0(\grph)$, the latter in $\Omega^1(\grph)$.

    \item {\bf Different mathematical type.}
        $\sum g = 0$ is algebraic ($g$ orthogonal to the zero-mode direction).
        The divergence $\dzeroT A = 0$ is differential (requiring discrete divergence to vanish).
        They act on different spaces.

    \item {\bf Different continuous analog.}
        The continuous analog of $\sum g_v = 0$ is not $\partial_\mu A^\mu = 0$,
        but the orthogonality condition $\int \Lambda(x) \,dx = 0$ on the gauge parameter —
        fixing the integration constant in the gauge transformation $A \to A - d\Lambda$.
        This fixes the ``zero-mode of the gauge parameter,'' not imposes a condition on the connection.

    \item {\bf Different role in computation.}
        In solving the normal equation $B^T B g = B^T A$, the constraint $\sum g = 0$ is equivalent to
        taking the pseudo-inverse or minimum-norm solution on $\mathbf{1}^\perp$
        (the orthogonal complement of the all-ones vector).
        It makes the linear system non-singular without changing the value of the residual $\mathcal{R}[g]$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[What Is the Genuine Discrete Coulomb Gauge?]
A genuine discrete Coulomb gauge analog would be:
\[
    \dzeroT A = 0 \quad \text{or} \quad \dzeroT (A - d_0 g) = 0,
\]
i.e., requiring the discrete divergence of the gauge-fixed edge assignment to vanish.
This is an entirely different equation, involving $\Lone$ rather than $\Lzero$,
and is {\bf never used} in the SCX computation.
The SCX solution $g$ satisfies $B^T B g = B^T A$ together with $\sum g = 0$,
but does not guarantee $B^T (A - B g) = 0$ (in fact the latter {\it is} the normal equation itself,
not an additional condition).
\end{remark}

\subsection{Solving the Gauge-Fixing Problem}

\begin{proposition}[Closed-Form Solution with Zero-Mode Fixing]
\label{prop:solution}
With zero-mode fixing constraint $\sum_v g_{v,a} = 0$ (imposed independently for each coordinate dimension),
the least-squares problem:
\[
    \min_{g: \sum_v g_{v,a} = 0} \sum_{a=1}^d \| \mathbf{A}_a - B \mathbf{g}_a \|^2
\]
has solution given by the pseudo-inverse:
\[
    \mathbf{g}_a^* = (B^T B)^+ \, B^T \mathbf{A}_a,
\]
where $(B^T B)^+$ is the Moore-Penrose pseudo-inverse of $B^T B$.
Equivalently, $\mathbf{g}_a^*$ is the minimum-norm solution, satisfying
$\mathbf{g}_a^* \perp \mathbf{1}$ (i.e., automatically satisfying $\sum_v g_{v,a}^* = 0$).

Numerically, one can solve the augmented system:
\[
    \begin{pmatrix}
        B^T B & \mathbf{1} \\
        \mathbf{1}^T & 0
    \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \mathbf{g}_a \\ \lambda
    \end{pmatrix}
    =
    \begin{pmatrix}
        B^T \mathbf{A}_a \\ 0
    \end{pmatrix}
\]
where $\lambda$ is a Lagrange multiplier.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Geometric Interpretation of the Solution}

\begin{proposition}[Gauge-Fixing as Orthogonal Projection]
\label{prop:projection}
The optimal vertex potential $g^*$ satisfies:
\[
    d_0 g^* = \text{proj}_{\im(B)}(\mathbf{A}),
\]
i.e., $B g^*$ (the vector form of $d_0 g^*$) is the orthogonal projection of $\mathbf{A}$ onto $\im(B)$,
the column space of $B$.

Thus gauge-fixing decomposes the edge assignment $A$ into two parts:
\begin{itemize}
    \item {\bf Gauge-removable part:} $d_0 g^* \in \im(d_0) \subseteq \Omega^1$ — the component
        that can be fully absorbed by choosing gauge parameters (the gradient part);
    \item {\bf Ineliminable residual:} $A - d_0 g^* \perp \im(d_0)$ — the component
        that cannot be removed by any choice of gauge parameters.
\end{itemize}
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}[Relation to Hodge Decomposition]
The residual $A - d_0 g^*$ lies in $\im(d_0)^\perp$.
By the discrete Hodge decomposition (Theorem~\ref{thm:hodge_decomp}):
$\im(d_0)^\perp = \ker(\Lone) \oplus \im(\doneT)$.
The post-gauge-fixing residual thus contains a {\bf harmonic part} and a
{\bf coexact part}, jointly measuring the content of $A$ that is
``not removable by a gradient.''

In particular, $\dim \ker(\Lone)$ equals the cyclomatic number $|\mathcal{L}|$
of the graph — the number of independent elementary loops.
The harmonic component precisely corresponds to non-zero loop holonomies —
i.e., non-zero discrete curvature.
\end{remark}

\section{The Cercis Score: Residual Norm after Gauge-Fixing}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Definition: Residual, not Yang-Mills Functional}

We now give the {\bf unique definition} of the Cercis Score, one that directly matches
the actual SCX computation.

\begin{definition}[Cercis Score]
\label{def:cercis}
Let $g^*$ be the optimal vertex potential from the zero-mode-fixed least-squares
problem (Proposition~\ref{prop:solution}).
The {\bf Cercis Score} is defined as the {\bf residual norm after gauge-fixing}:
\[
    \cercis := \mathcal{R}[g^*] = \sum_{e \in \edgs} \|A_e - (d_0 g^*)_e\|^2.
\]

Expanding component-wise (per coordinate dimension):
\[
    \cercis = \sum_{a=1}^{d} \|\mathbf{A}_a - B \mathbf{g}_a^*\|^2
          = \sum_{a=1}^{d} \|\mathbf{A}_a - B (B^T B)^+ B^T \mathbf{A}_a\|^2.
\]

Let $P = B (B^T B)^+ B^T$ be the orthogonal projection matrix onto $\im(B)$,
and $P^\perp = I - P$ the projector onto $\im(B)^\perp$.
Then concisely:
\[
    \cercis = \sum_{a=1}^{d} \|P^\perp \mathbf{A}_a\|^2
          = \sum_{a=1}^{d} \mathbf{A}_a^T P^\perp \mathbf{A}_a.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Cercis Is Not the Yang-Mills Functional]
The Cercis Score in this paper is {\bf not} the Yang-Mills functional $\int \|F\|^2$.
It is not the sum of squared curvature norms, not the $L^2$ norm of $d_1 A$.
Cercis is the {\bf residual} after gauge-fixing — the squared distance from $A$ to $\im(d_0)$.

The curvature $\boldsymbol{\kappa} = d_1 A$ satisfies $\boldsymbol{\kappa} = d_1(A - d_0 g^*)$
(since $d_1 d_0 = 0$), so curvature is determined solely by the residual
$A - d_0 g^*$. But Cercis is the {\it total} norm of the residual,
not just its harmonic component (i.e., curvature).
Specifically, if $r = A - d_0 g^*$, then:
\[
    \cercis = \|r\|^2 = \|r_{\text{harm}}\|^2 + \|r_{\text{coexact}}\|^2,
\]
whereas a Yang-Mills-type functional would only capture
$\|d_1 r\|^2 = \|d_1 r_{\text{harm}}\|^2$. These are different,
and Cercis is the quantity actually used by SCX.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Gauge Invariance}

\begin{theorem}[Gauge Invariance of Cercis]
\label{thm:cercis_inv}
The Cercis Score $\cercis$ is gauge-invariant:
under the gauge transformation $A \mapsto A' = A - d_0 h$ (where $h$ is an arbitrary vertex potential),
$\cercis' = \cercis$.

{\bf Proof.} Let $g^*$ be the optimal vertex potential for the original $A$.
Under the transformed edge assignment $A' = A - d_0 h$, the optimum shifts to $g'^* = g^* - h$,
(since $A' - d_0 g'^* = (A - d_0 h) - d_0(g^* - h) = A - d_0 g^*$).
Hence the residual is unchanged: $\|A' - d_0 g'^*\|^2 = \|A - d_0 g^*\|^2$.
Therefore $\cercis' = \cercis$. $\square$
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[Intuition for Gauge Invariance]
Since $\cercis$ is defined as the residual norm — the (squared) distance from $A$ to
the subspace $\im(d_0)$ — and $\im(d_0)$ is the tangent space of the gauge orbit
(in fact the orbit itself, since the group is abelian), the distance is invariant under
gauge transformations.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Discriminative Properties of Cercis}

\begin{theorem}[Characterization of $\cercis = 0$]
\label{thm:cercis_zero}
The following are equivalent:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item $\cercis = 0$;
    \item The edge assignment $A$ is exact: there exists $g$ such that $A = d_0 g$;
    \item $A \in \im(d_0)$, i.e., $A$ consists entirely of the gradient part;
    \item Perfect global alignment exists: all experts output consistent coordinates
        at all configurations;
    \item $\curv(\gamma) = 0$ for all quadrilateral elementary loops $\gamma$, {\it and}
        the coexact component of $A$ is zero.
\end{enumerate}

Note: the second condition in (v) (coexact component zero) is necessary:
$d_1 A = 0$ alone only excludes the harmonic component but does not guarantee
that $A$ is exact (see the remark after Theorem~\ref{thm:flatness}).
In the actual SCX computation, if the graph has a non-trivial coexact component,
$\cercis > 0$ is possible even when curvature is zero.
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Cercis as a Consistency Measure}

\begin{corollary}
The Cercis Score provides an objective measure of gauge consistency of the
multi-expert system:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\cercis = 0$: the system is {\bf gauge-consistent}.
        A set of gauge translations exists such that aligned coordinates
        are independent of the specific choice of expert or configuration.
    \item $\cercis > 0$: the system is {\bf gauge-inconsistent}.
        The magnitude of the residual measures the ``intrinsic friction''
        that cannot be eliminated by gauge-fixing.
        The larger $\cercis$, the more inconsistent the multi-expert system.
\end{itemize}

A normalized version of Cercis can be defined as:
\[
    \bar{\cercis} := \frac{\cercis}{\|A\|^2},
\]
taking values in $[0, 1]$, where $0$ corresponds to perfect consistency
and $1$ corresponds to complete inconsistency
(i.e., $A$ is completely orthogonal to $\im(d_0)$).
\end{corollary}

\section{Honest Acknowledgment of Topological Triviality}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{All Chern Classes Vanish}

We now honestly confront a fundamental fact that prior work has evaded.

\begin{proposition}[The SCX Bundle Is Topologically Trivial]
\label{prop:trivial}
From the perspective of continuous principal bundles (if one insists on constructing them):
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
    \item {\bf The structure group $G \cong \R^{Md}$ is contractible.}
        As a vector space, $\R^{Md}$ can be continuously contracted to the origin.
        Therefore all its homotopy groups vanish: $\pi_k(G) = 0$ for $k \geq 0$.

    \item {\bf The base space $\mathcal{X} \subset \R^K$ is contractible.}
        The parameter space is a subset of $\R^K$ (typically a convex set or cube), also contractible.

    \item {\bf The classifying space is trivial.}
        For a contractible group $G$, its classifying space $BG$ is weakly contractible.
        Hence the set of homotopy classes $[\mathcal{X}, BG] = \{*\}$ — there is only one homotopy class.
        Therefore all $G$-principal bundles over $\mathcal{X}$ are trivial.

    \item {\bf All characteristic classes are zero.}
        Chern classes, Pontryagin classes, Euler classes, and all other real characteristic classes
        are identically zero.
        Since $\mathcal{X}$ is contractible $\Rightarrow$ $H^k(\mathcal{X}; \R) = 0$ for $k > 0$.
        The second de Rham cohomology $H^2_{\mathrm{dR}}(\mathcal{X}; \R) = 0$ trivially.
\end{enumerate}

Therefore, there are no topological obstructions. No topological invariants. No non-trivial bundles.
The SCX principal bundle (if we were to construct it) is simply the trivial product
$\bun \cong G \times \mathcal{X}$.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{The Content Is Geometric, Not Topological}

Does this weaken the theory? No. Here is why.

\begin{remark}[The True Distinction: Flat vs.\ Non-Flat Connections]
Topology deals with the question of when a bundle cannot be reduced to the trivial bundle.
For SCX, the bundle is {\bf always} trivial — this is uncontroversial.

The real content is {\bf geometric}: given an edge assignment $A$,
does $A$ lie in $\im(d_0)$ (is $A$ flat / exact)?
This is a {\it linear algebra} question about the relationship between $A$
and the subspace $\im(d_0)$, not a topological question.

Even though the bundle is trivial, the edge assignment $A$ can still have non-zero
curvature components (harmonic $1$-forms). This is precisely the case when
$A \notin \im(d_0)$ — the moment gauge inconsistency appears.
The phenomenon is geometric (measuring how far $A$ is from $\im(d_0)$),
not topological (involving twisted products or transition functions).

Analogy: on $\R^3$ (contractible, trivial), a vector field $\mathbf{v}$
may be curl-free ($\nabla \times \mathbf{v} = 0$, $\mathbf{v} = \nabla \phi$)
or not. This is not a topological statement — the topology of $\R^3$ never changes.
Similarly, SCX's gauge consistency problem concerns the decomposition of $A$
on the given (trivial) graph, not the non-triviality of a bundle.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Topological Content in the Graph Perspective}

In the graph perspective, the only ``topological'' content is the Betti numbers of the graph itself.

\begin{proposition}[Graph Homology]
The (real coefficient) homology of $\grph_{\text{SCX}}$ is:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $H_0(\grph) \cong \R^c$, where $c$ is the number of connected components (typically $1$);
    \item $H_1(\grph) \cong \R^{|\mathcal{L}|}$, where $|\mathcal{L}|$ is the number of elementary loops
        (the cyclomatic number);
    \item $H_k(\grph) = 0$ for all $k \geq 2$.
\end{itemize}

$H_1(\grph) \cong \ker(\Lone)$ is the space of harmonic $1$-forms.
A non-zero dimensional $H_1$ means the graph has $|\mathcal{L}|$ independent loops,
each capable of carrying non-trivial holonomy.
But this is not bundle topology — it is merely the cycle structure of the graph.
\end{proposition}

\section{Numerical Algorithm}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Data Structures and Algorithm Overview}

The complete numerical pipeline of SCX gauge-fixing consists of the following steps.
Each step directly corresponds to the mathematical structures described above.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{SCX Gauge-Fixing and Cercis Computation}
\label{alg:scx}
\begin{enumerate}[label=Step \arabic*:, leftmargin=*]
    \item {\bf Input:} Raw predictions of $M$ experts at $N$ configurations
        $\{\tilde{x}_m^k \in \R^d \mid k = 1,\dots,N,\; m = 1,\dots,M\}$.

    \item {\bf Construct graph $\grph_{\text{SCX}}$:}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Vertices: $n = N \cdot M$, indexed by $(k, m)$;
            \item Parameter edges: $(k,m) \to (k{+}1,m)$, totaling $M \cdot (N-1)$;
            \item Expert edges: $(k,i) \to (k,j)$ ($i \neq j$), totaling $N \cdot M \cdot (M-1)$;
            \item Total edges: $m = M(N-1) + NM(M-1)$.
        \end{itemize}

    \item {\bf Build incidence matrix $B \in \R^{m \times n}$:}
        For each edge $e$, $B_{e,v_{\text{target}}} = +1$, $B_{e,v_{\text{source}}} = -1$.

    \item {\bf Assemble edge assignment vectors $\mathbf{A}_a \in \R^m$:} (for each coordinate $a = 1,\dots,d$)
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Parameter edges: $A_e = \tilde{x}_{m,a}^{k+1} - \tilde{x}_{m,a}^k$;
            \item Expert edges: $A_e = \tilde{x}_{i,a}^k - \tilde{x}_{j,a}^k$.
        \end{itemize}

    \item {\bf Compute graph Laplacian and right-hand side:}
        \[
            L = B^T B \in \R^{n \times n}, \quad
            \mathbf{b}_a = B^T \mathbf{A}_a \in \R^n.
        \]

    \item {\bf Solve normal equations with zero-mode fixing:}
        For each $a = 1,\dots,d$, solve:
        \[
            \begin{pmatrix}
                L & \mathbf{1} \\
                \mathbf{1}^T & 0
            \end{pmatrix}
            \begin{pmatrix}
                \mathbf{g}_a \\ \lambda_a
            \end{pmatrix}
            =
            \begin{pmatrix}
                \mathbf{b}_a \\ 0
            \end{pmatrix}.
        \]
        (Or equivalently, use conjugate gradient on the subspace $\mathbf{1}^\perp$ to solve $L \mathbf{g}_a = \mathbf{b}_a$.)

    \item {\bf Compute residual and Cercis Score:}
        \[
            \cercis = \sum_{a=1}^{d} \|\mathbf{A}_a - B \mathbf{g}_a\|^2.
        \]

    \item {\bf Output aligned predictions (optional):}
        \[
            \hat{x}_m^k = \tilde{x}_m^k - g_m^k, \quad \forall k, m.
        \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Relation to the Original MILP}

The original SCX implementation used MILP (Mixed-Integer Linear Programming)
for gauge-fixing. The least-squares formulation here is equivalent to the
{\it continuous relaxation} of MILP: when all integer constraints are removed,
MILP reduces to solving $B^T B g = B^T A$ with constraint $\sum g = 0$.

If integer constraints are required (e.g., $g_m^k$ restricted to integer lattice points),
rounding or branch-and-bound can be applied on top of the least-squares solution.
However, for most application scenarios, the continuous least-squares solution
already provides gauge-fixing with sufficient precision.

\subsection{Computational Complexity}

\begin{proposition}[Complexity Analysis]
\begin{itemize}
    \item Graph construction: $O(N M^2)$ (each expert edge requires computing a pairwise displacement);
    \item Laplacian assembly: $O(m) = O(N M^2)$ (non-zero entry insertion);
    \item Normal equation solve: $O(n^{3/2})$ to $O(n^2)$ depending on solver
        ($n = NM$, exploiting the sparsity of $L$, conjugate gradient gives $O(n \cdot \text{nnz})$ per step);
    \item Cercis computation: $O(m d) = O(N M^2 d)$.
\end{itemize}

For typical parameter settings ($M \sim 10^1$--$10^2$, $N \sim 10^2$--$10^3$, $d \leq 3$),
the entire pipeline completes in seconds to minutes on modern hardware.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Errors in Prior Formulation}

\begin{enumerate}[label=F\arabic*:, leftmargin=*]
    \item {\bf F1: $\sum g_m = 0$ is not Coulomb gauge.}
        As discussed above, it is a zero-mode fixing constraint on the gauge parameters,
        not a divergence constraint on the connection. Calling it Coulomb gauge conflates
        two entirely different mathematical structures.
        The correct continuous analog is fixing the integration constant $\int \Lambda = 0$
        in the gauge transformation $A \to A - d\Lambda$.

    \item {\bf F2: The connection $\omega$ was never constructed.}
        The continuous framework asserts the existence of a principal bundle with a connection,
        but never explains how to construct that connection from the pairwise expert displacements
        $\delta_{ij}^k$.
        This paper takes the more honest path: $\delta_{ij}^k$ {\it are} the edge assignments $A_e$ —
        they are the data themselves, requiring no separate ``connection'' to mediate.

    \item {\bf F3: Cercis definition is inconsistent.}
        The prior text oscillates between two mutually incompatible definitions:
        (a) $\cercis = \int \|F\|^2$ (Yang-Mills functional),
        (b) $\cercis = Q + \eta N$ (the $Q+\eta N$ actually used by SCX).
        This paper unifies them as the residual norm $\cercis = \|P^\perp A\|^2$,
        which agrees with (b) and is not confused with (a).

    \item {\bf F4: Topology is misrepresented.}
        The prior text suggests non-trivial topological structure, but
        $G$ contractible and $\base$ contractible
        $\Rightarrow$ all Chern classes vanish $\Rightarrow$ the bundle is trivial.
        This paper acknowledges this explicitly and correctly interprets the content
        as geometric (flat vs.\ non-flat), not topological.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Discrete Hodge vs.\ Continuous Differential Geometry}
\label{sec:comparison}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

This section provides a systematic comparison between the discrete graph Hodge method
and the continuous differential geometry (fiber bundle) method,
delineates their respective capabilities, and explains why the discrete method
is the correct choice for SCX.

\subsection{Two Formalisms}

\subsection{What Continuous Differential Geometry Can Do}

Continuous differential geometry (fiber bundles, connections, curvature) is powerful in the following respects:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
    \item {\bf Global topological classification.}
        When the base manifold and structure group are non-trivial, Chern--Weil theory provides
        a complete classification of topological obstructions via characteristic classes.
        For example, the instanton number of an $SU(2)$-bundle over $S^4$ is given by
        the integral of the second Chern class $c_2$, and must be an integer.
        This entire machinery is irreplaceable for physical systems with non-trivial topology.

    \item {\bf Analytical tools.}
        The Atiyah--Singer index theorem relates the analytic index of elliptic operators
        to topological invariants. Hodge theory identifies the space of harmonic forms
        with de Rham cohomology. These deep theorems provide bridges between analytic
        computation and topological classification.

    \item {\bf Local-to-global integration.}
        The connection $1$-form unifies local gauge transformations ($A \to A - d\Lambda$)
        with global parallel transport. Wilson lines $W[\gamma] = \mathcal{P}\exp(\oint_\gamma A)$
        are gauge-invariant and encode path-dependent information.
        This structure is indispensable in the non-abelian case.

    \item {\bf Mature theoretical ecosystem.}
        Six decades of development have produced a complete body of theorems
        (Chern--Weil, Atiyah--Singer, Witten anomaly, Seiberg--Witten),
        from which any gauge theory problem can draw insight.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{What Continuous Differential Geometry Cannot Do}

However, for the specific problem of SCX, continuous differential geometry has the following
fundamental limitations:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
    \item {\bf Cannot construct a connection from discrete data.}
        The continuous framework requires a smooth connection $1$-form $\omega$ on a base manifold.
        But SCX's raw data consist of {\bf finitely many} pairwise displacements $\delta_{ij}^k$ —
        a set of discrete numbers. Constructing a smooth connection from finitely many point values
        is an {\bf ill-posed inverse problem}: infinitely many smooth connections can interpolate
        the given discrete values, with no uniqueness principle to select one.
        Prior work never explicitly constructed a connection — it merely {\it asserted} existence.

    \item {\bf All topological invariants are identically zero.}
        SCX's base space $\mathcal{X} \subset \R^K$ is contractible (typically a convex parameter region),
        and the structure group $G \cong \R^{Md}$ is also contractible. Therefore:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item $H^k(\mathcal{X}; \R) = 0$ for all $k > 0$;
            \item All Chern classes $c_k(P) \in H^{2k}(\mathcal{X}; \R) = 0$ vanish identically;
            \item The principal bundle is globally trivial: $P \cong G \times \mathcal{X}$;
            \item The set of homotopy classes $[\mathcal{X}, BG] = \{*\}$ has a single element.
        \end{itemize}
        This means the entire heavy weaponry of Chern--Weil theory, index theorems, and instanton
        classification {\bf produces no non-trivial output here}. All topological invariants they
        provide are zero. Citing these theorems generates no new information about the SCX system.

    \item {\bf The continuous formalism is disconnected from the computation.}
        The actual computation of the Cercis Score is: solve the sparse linear system
        $B^T B g = B^T A$ for $M \times N$ vertices (or equivalently, compute the projection
        of $A$ onto $\im(B)$). The continuous framework describes this as minimization
        of the Yang--Mills functional $\int \|F\|^2$, but these two quantities are
        {\bf not the same mathematical object}:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item $\int \|F\|^2$ is the $L^2$ norm of the curvature $2$-form;
            \item $\|P^\perp A\|^2$ is the squared distance from the edge assignment to the gradient subspace;
            \item The former captures only the harmonic component (curvature), while the latter
                contains both harmonic and coexact parts.
        \end{itemize}
        Confusing them is a category error.

    \item {\bf Terminological confusion.}
        The continuous framework calls $\sum g_v = 0$ the ``Coulomb gauge,'' but Coulomb gauge is
        $\partial_\mu A^\mu = 0$ — a divergence constraint on the gauge {\bf potential}.
        $\sum g_v = 0$ is zero-mode fixing on the gauge {\bf parameter}.
        Confusing these two concepts is not merely imprecise — it is incorrect.
        A physically trained referee would immediately recognize this error.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Why Discrete Is the Right Choice}

The advantages of the discrete graph Hodge method stem from the following principles:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
    \item {\bf Ontological Honesty.}
        SCX's data are discrete. The graph is discrete. The computation is discrete.
        Discrete Hodge theory {\bf is} the native mathematics of the data — no interpolation,
        no smoothing, no unverifiable step of lifting discrete numbers to continuous fields is needed.

    \item {\bf Computational Correspondence.}
        Every mathematical object in this paper directly corresponds to one or more lines in the SCX code:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item $A_e = \delta_{ij}^k$: direct storage of pairwise expert displacements;
            \item $B$: the adjacency structure of the graph, used to build the normal equations;
            \item $B^T B g = B^T A$: the exact form of the continuous relaxation of MILP;
            \item $\cercis = \|P^\perp A\|^2$: direct computation of the residual after gauge-fixing.
        \end{itemize}

    \item {\bf Mathematical Self-Sufficiency.}
        Discrete Hodge theory is a complete, self-contained mathematical system.
        It has its own Hodge decomposition, its own Laplacians, its own cohomology.
        It does not need to reference continuous theory for self-justification.
        In fact, continuous de Rham cohomology can be {\it recovered} by taking an appropriate
        limit of discrete Hodge theory, not the other way around.

    \item {\bf Falsifiability.}
        Every statement in the discrete formulation can be directly verified by numerical computation.
        Statements in the continuous formulation (such as ``there exists a connection $\omega$'')
        are typically unverifiable or unfalsifiable — because they provide no construction.

    \item {\bf Natural Generalization Path.}
        The discrete framework provides clear paths for generalization:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Weighted graphs: $W_0, W_1$ weight matrices;
            \item Non-abelian group-valued edge assignments: group-valued least squares;
            \item Persistent homology: Cercis as $H_1$ persistence;
            \item Infinite-dimensional limit: graph $\to$ mesh $\to$ PDE.
        \end{itemize}
        None of these generalizations require first passing through the continuous framework.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{What the Continuous Formalism Would Need to Become Valid}

If future work insists on using the continuous differential geometry framework,
at minimum the following steps must be completed:

\begin{enumerate}[label=C\arabic*:, leftmargin=*]
    \item {\bf C1: Explicitly construct a connection from discrete data.}
        An algorithm must be given that explicitly constructs a smooth connection $1$-form $\omega$
        from finitely many pairwise displacements $\{\delta_{ij}^k\}$ (e.g., via interpolation or reconstruction).
        Merely asserting that such a connection exists is insufficient.

    \item {\bf C2: Prove consistency of discretization.}
        It must be proved that as the discrete data are refined
        (number of configurations $N \to \infty$ and number of experts $M \to \infty$),
        the discrete edge assignments $A_e$ converge, in some appropriate sense,
        to the continuous connection $\omega$. This is analogous to proving the existence
        of the continuum limit in lattice gauge theory.

    \item {\bf C3: Establish non-trivial topology.}
        Non-trivial topological structure must be identified that {\it actually exists}
        in the base space or structure group. If both the base space and the group are contractible,
        one must acknowledge that the topological part of the continuous framework
        produces no non-trivial output. Alternatives include considering non-trivial group actions
        or parameter spaces with holes.

    \item {\bf C4: Precisely relate Cercis to the Yang-Mills functional.}
        If it is claimed that the Cercis Score corresponds to $\int \|F\|^2$,
        one must mathematically prove that they are equal, or at least converge
        in an appropriate limit. Currently these two quantities are different mathematical objects
        in different spaces.

    \item {\bf C5: Correctly define the Coulomb gauge analog.}
        A correct discretization of the continuous Coulomb gauge $\partial_\mu A^\mu = 0$ must be given,
        and it must be distinguished from the constraint $\sum g_v = 0$ actually used by SCX.
        If they are different (as they indeed are), one must state under what conditions they are equivalent,
        or acknowledge that they are unrelated.
\end{enumerate}

\bigskip
\noindent
{\bf Summary.}
Discrete graph Hodge theory and continuous fiber bundle theory are not competitors —
they are formalisms at different levels, suited to different problems.
For SCX, the data are naturally discrete, the computation is naturally discrete,
and therefore the discrete formalism is the {\bf only formalism consistent with the computation}.
The continuous framework can only be considered a valid alternative
after completing the above steps C1--C5. Until then, discrete Hodge theory
should be regarded as the {\bf authoritative formulation} of SCX gauge theory.

\section{Generalizations and Outlook}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Weighted Graphs and More General Metrics}

In this paper we used unweighted graphs (all edge weights equal).
More generally, edge weights can be assigned to reflect confidence in
certain expert or configuration comparisons:
\[
    \mathcal{R}_W[g] := \sum_{e \in \edgs} w_e \, \|A_e - (d_0 g)_e\|^2.
\]
The weighted Laplacian $L_W = B^T W B$ replaces the unweighted version, and all
theoretical results carry through unchanged.

The weighted version allows tuning Cercis to be more sensitive to important expert pairs
or critical configuration regions.

\subsection{Non-Abelian Expert Symmetries}

If the gauge group of experts extends to include rotations and scaling (e.g., $\mathrm{SE}(d)$),
the edge assignments become group-valued rather than vector-valued. In this case:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Gauge transformations $g$ are group-valued vertex functions;
    \item Edge assignments transform as $A'_e = g_v \circ A_e \circ g_u^{-1}$;
    \item Gauge-fixing becomes a group-valued least-squares problem,
        requiring solution on homogeneous spaces (using optimization methods on Riemannian manifolds);
    \item Curvature can still be defined as the group product along loops.
\end{itemize}

The graph-theoretic framework of this paper provides a natural foundation
for such generalizations.

\subsection{Connection to Persistent Homology}

The Cercis Score can be interpreted as a persistence measure in persistent homology:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Non-zero discrete curvature loops correspond to $1$-dimensional ``holes'' in the barcode;
    \item The harmonic component of Cercis, $\|r_{\text{harm}}\|^2$, can be viewed as
        a persistence-weighted sum of $H_1$ classes under some filtration;
    \item Gauge-fixing is equivalent to searching for the optimal coboundary to ``fill'' these $1$-dimensional holes.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Infinite-Dimensional Limit}

As $M \to \infty$ and $N \to \infty$, the graph $\grph_{\text{SCX}}$ tends to a
continuous grid on the product space $[0,1] \times [0,1]$. The limiting PDE
involves derivatives in both parameter and expert directions,
with natural connections to 2D gauge field theory.
Rigorous analysis of this limit is ongoing.

\section{Conclusion}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Main Results}

This paper establishes a complete discrete formalization of SCX gauge theory
on graphs. The main contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
    \item {\bf Graph Hodge framework:}
        The mathematical structure of SCX is precisely located as discrete Hodge theory
        on directed graphs. Pairwise expert displacements are directly encoded
        as edge assignments $A_e$ — no continuous connection or fiber bundle needed.

    \item {\bf Curvature as loop holonomy:}
        Discrete curvature is defined as the algebraic sum of edge assignments
        around elementary quadrilateral loops. Curvature is non-zero iff experts'
        predictions change inconsistently under parameter variation —
        the geometric formulation of PES misalignment.

    \item {\bf Gauge-fixing as least squares:}
        Gauge-fixing is rigorously formalized as finding vertex potentials $g$ minimizing
        $\sum_e \|A_e - (g_w - g_v)\|^2$.

    \item {\bf Zero-mode fixing $\neq$ Coulomb gauge:}
        Strictly distinguishes the constraint $\sum_v g_v = 0$ (zero-mode fixing,
        ensuring solution uniqueness) from Coulomb gauge $\partial_\mu A^\mu = 0$
        (divergence constraint on the connection). The former is algebraic,
        the latter differential — they are entirely different.

    \item {\bf Unique Cercis definition:}
        The Cercis Score is defined as the residual norm $\|P^\perp A\|^2$ after gauge-fixing,
        i.e., the squared distance from the edge assignment to the gradient subspace.
        This definition is unique, gauge-invariant, and precisely matches the actual SCX computation.

    \item {\bf Honest topological triviality:}
        Explicitly acknowledges that $G$ is contractible and $\mathcal{X}$ is contractible,
        hence the bundle is topologically trivial. All content is geometric
        (flat vs.\ non-flat edge assignments), not topological.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Final Remark}

The mathematical core of SCX is elegant and simple: it is just discrete Hodge
decomposition on a graph. Given a set of pairwise expert displacements (edge
assignment $A$), the problem reduces to determining the distance between $A$
and the gradient subspace $\im(d_0)$. Gauge consistency means this distance is
zero ($A$ is exact); inconsistency means it is positive, and Cercis precisely
quantifies that distance.

The introduction of continuous differential geometry — fiber bundles,
connections, curvature forms — is superfluous for this problem and even harmful,
because it introduces terminology (Coulomb gauge, Yang-Mills action, Chern classes)
that does not correspond to the actual discrete computation.

We advocate: a clear, honest, and mathematically precise discrete formulation
is always preferable to an inaccurate continuous analogy disconnected from
the computation.
