\begin{abstract}
\noindent
{\bf Abstract.} Medical diagnosis is one of the highest-risk domains of human decision-making: the misdiagnosis rate in clinical practice
is reported as high as 5--15\%, and missed diagnosis of rare diseases is even more common due to low prior probability.
This paper formalizes medical diagnosis as a {\bf multi-expert consensus audit problem}, placed under the \SCX{} framework.
In this formalization, each physician is an expert $f_m$, producing a diagnostic opinion $\diagnosis_m$;
imaging, laboratory testing, and pathology constitute {\bf multi-modal experts},
each observing the patient's status from different information channels;
the second opinion corresponds to $M=2$;
the multidisciplinary team / tumor board is the institutional embodiment of the \Yajie{} consensus mechanism.

We prove three core theorems, each with complete proof (\rigorFull).\\
{\bf Theorem~1 (Multi-Physician Misdiagnosis Detection Theorem):}
For $M$ independent physicians with effective multiplicity $M_{\eff} = M/(1+(M-1)\bar{\rho})$,
where $\bar{\rho}$ is the average inter-physician error correlation, the probability that all $M$ physicians collectively miss a diagnosis is bounded by
$\exp(-2M_{\eff}\,\Delta^2)$, where $\Delta$ is the diagnostic detection margin.\\
{\bf Theorem~2 (Multi-Modal Expert Consensus Theorem):}
When $K$ diagnostic modalities (imaging, laboratory, pathology, genomics) provide conditionally independent diagnostic signals,
the \Yajie{} weighted consensus reduces the misdiagnosis probability exponentially in $K_{\eff}$.
We derive the optimal modality weighting scheme: when modality $k$ has diagnostic odds ratio
$\text{DOR}_k$, the weight $\omega_k \propto \ln(\text{DOR}_k)$ is optimal in the sense of minimizing consensus error risk.\\
{\bf Theorem~3 (False Negative and Rare Disease Unidentifiability Theorem):}
When the disease prior probability $\pi_0 \to 0$ (rare disease regime), a negative result from a single diagnostic modality cannot distinguish
``the test is correct---the disease is truly absent'' from ``the test fails---the rare disease is missed.''
We prove the identifiability gap: $\abs{\Pbb(\disease=1 \mid \diagnosis=0) - \pi_0}
\leq \frac{1 - \Sens}{\Sens + \Spec - 1}$, and $M > M_{\min} =
\frac{\ln(1/\varepsilon)}{2\Delta^2}$ independent experts are required to shrink this gap below $\varepsilon$.

This paper is not a survey and does not claim to replace physicians. It provides theorems and proofs.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ======================================================================

Medical diagnosis is a decision under uncertainty with life-or-death consequences.
Despite centuries of clinical experience and decades of evidence-based medicine,
the fundamental structure of diagnostic error remains poorly quantified.
Published estimates of misdiagnosis rates range from 5\% in radiology \cite{berlin2007accuracy}
to 10--15\% in general internal medicine \cite{graber2005diagnostic}, and up
to 20--30\% in emergency department settings \cite{newman2013diagnostic}.
Autopsy studies reveal that 8--24\% of causes of death were missed during life
\cite{shojania2003changes}. These are not failures of individual physicians --- they
are structural properties of single-expert decision-making.

The clinical community has long intuited the remedy: get a second opinion.
The second-opinion practice ($M=2$) reduces diagnostic error rates by 15--30\%
in radiology \cite{bender2019second} and changes management in 10--62\% of cases
across specialties \cite{payne2014impact}. Tumor boards (multidisciplinary team meetings) --- formal
meetings where surgeons, medical oncologists, radiation oncologists, radiologists,
and pathologists jointly review cases --- have been shown to alter diagnosis or
management in 25--52\% of cases \cite{pillay2016impact}. These practices are
instantiations of a mathematical principle that the clinical literature has
never explicitly recognized: \textbf{multi-expert consensus with independent
information channels produces exponentially decreasing error probability}.

This paper provides the missing mathematical foundation. We formalize medical
diagnosis as an \SCX{} audit problem where each physician is an expert, each
diagnostic modality (imaging, laboratory, pathology, genomics) is an independent
information channel, and the clinical decision is a \Yajie{} consensus over
multiple expert opinions. We prove three theorems that establish:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item How many physicians are needed to guarantee a misdiagnosis probability
    below a target $\varepsilon$, given the inter-physician error correlation $\bar{\rho}$
    and detection margin $\Delta$ (Theorem~1).
    \item How multiple diagnostic modalities combine to produce a consensus
    diagnosis whose error probability decays exponentially in the effective
    number of independent modalities $K_{\eff}$ (Theorem~2).
    \item Why rare diseases create a fundamental unidentifiability between false
    negatives and true absence, and how many independent diagnostic modalities
    are required to resolve it (Theorem~3).
\end{enumerate}

The core mathematical analogy between clinical medicine and the \SCX{} audit
framework is summarized in Table~\ref{tab:analogy}.

\noindent\textbf{What this paper is not.} This is not a clinical guideline.
It is not a systematic review of diagnostic accuracy studies. It does not

\section{Formalization: Medical Diagnosis as Multi-Expert Audit}
\label{sec:formalism}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{Patient, Disease, and Diagnosis}

\begin{definition}[Patient State]
\label{def:patient}
A \textbf{patient} $\patient = (\mathbf{x}, \disease) \in \cX \times \{0,1\}$
is characterized by:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item A feature vector $\mathbf{x} \in \cX \subset \R^d$ consisting of
    demographic data (age, sex, comorbidities), clinical presentation (symptoms,
    signs, history), and diagnostic findings (imaging results, lab values,
    pathology reports, genetic variants);
    \item A binary disease state $\disease \in \{0,1\}$ where $\disease = 1$ indicates
    the disease of interest is present and $\disease = 0$ indicates it is absent.
\end{itemize}
The feature space $\cX$ is assumed compact: $\supp(\rho_{\mathbf{x}}) \subseteq \cX$
is bounded.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Diagnosis]
\label{def:diagnosis}
A \textbf{diagnosis} $\diagnosis \in \{0,1\}$ is a binary decision produced by
a physician or diagnostic system. The diagnosis may be correct ($\diagnosis = \disease$)
or erroneous ($\diagnosis \neq \disease$). Two error types exist:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{False negative (Missed Diagnosis)}: $\diagnosis = 0$ when $\disease = 1$.
    \item \textbf{False positive (Overdiagnosis)}: $\diagnosis = 1$ when $\disease = 0$.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Misdiagnosis Rate]
\label{def:misdiagnosis}
For a diagnostic process over a patient population with distribution $\rho$,
the \textbf{misdiagnosis rate} is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:misdiagnosis}
    \misdiagnosis = \E_{(\mathbf{x},\disease) \sim \rho}\bigl[\ind{\diagnosis(\mathbf{x}) \neq \disease}\bigr] = \Pbb(\diagnosis \neq \disease).
\end{equation}
This decomposes as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:misdiagnosis_decomp}
    \misdiagnosis = \pi_1 \cdot (1 - \Sens) + (1 - \pi_1) \cdot (1 - \Spec),
\end{equation}
where $\pi_1 = \Pbb(\disease = 1)$ is disease prevalence, $\Sens = \Pbb(\diagnosis=1 \mid \disease=1)$
is sensitivity, and $\Spec = \Pbb(\diagnosis=0 \mid \disease=0)$ is specificity.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Physicians as Experts}

\begin{definition}[Physician]
\label{def:physician}
A \textbf{physician} $m \in \{1, \dots, M\}$ is a function $f_m: \cX \to \{0,1\}$
that maps patient features to a binary diagnosis $\diagnosis_m = f_m(\mathbf{x})$.
The physician is characterized by:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Sensitivity}: $\Sens_m = \Pbb(f_m(\mathbf{x}) = 1 \mid \disease = 1)$.
    \item \textbf{Specificity}: $\Spec_m = \Pbb(f_m(\mathbf{x}) = 0 \mid \disease = 0)$.
    \item \textbf{Diagnostic odds ratio}: $\text{DOR}_m = \frac{\Sens_m}{1 - \Sens_m} \cdot \frac{\Spec_m}{1 - \Spec_m}$.
    \item \textbf{Training/experience}: The physician's knowledge base $\cK_m$, acquired through
    medical education, residency, fellowship, and continuing practice.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Inter-Physician Correlation]
\label{def:physician_corr}
For physicians $m, m'$, the \textbf{error correlation} is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:phys_corr}
    \rho_{mm'} = \Corr\bigl(\ind{f_m(\mathbf{x}) \neq \disease},\; \ind{f_{m'}(\mathbf{x}) \neq \disease}\bigr).
\end{equation}
Physicians trained at the same institution, practicing in the same specialty,
or relying on the same diagnostic tests will exhibit $\rho_{mm'} > 0$.
Physicians from different specialties (e.g., radiologist vs. surgeon vs. pathologist)
viewing different data modalities will have lower correlation.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Diagnostic Modalities}

\begin{definition}[Diagnostic Modality]
\label{def:modality}
A \textbf{diagnostic modality} $k \in \{1, \dots, K\}$ is an information channel
that produces a signal $s_k(\mathbf{x}) \in \cS_k$ from patient features.
The four primary modalities are:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item $\modality_1 = \imaging$: Medical imaging --- X-ray, CT, MRI, ultrasound, PET.
    Reveals anatomical structure, tissue density, metabolic activity.
    \item $\modality_2 = \labtest$: Laboratory tests --- blood chemistry, hematology,
    microbiology, immunology, molecular diagnostics.
    \item $\modality_3 = \pathology$: Pathology --- histology, cytology, immunohistochemistry,
    molecular pathology.
    \item $\modality_4 = \text{Genomics}$: Genomic sequencing --- germline variants,
    somatic mutations, gene expression, epigenetic markers.
\end{itemize}
Each modality provides a \textbf{conditionally independent} view of the patient
given the true disease state. Formally:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:modal_indep}
    \Pbb(s_1, \dots, s_K \mid \disease) = \prod_{k=1}^{K} \Pbb(s_k \mid \disease).
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Multi-Modal Physician]
\label{def:multimodal_physician}
A physician $f_m$ may have access to a subset $\modality_m \subseteq \{1, \dots, K\}$
of diagnostic modalities. The physician's diagnosis is then a function of
the modality-specific signals: $\diagnosis_m = f_m(s_{k_1}(\mathbf{x}), \dots, s_{k_{|\modality_m|}}(\mathbf{x}))$.
Physicians viewing disjoint modality subsets have lower error correlation.
\end{definition}

\section{Assumptions}
\label{sec:assumptions}
% ======================================================================

All theorems in this paper hold under the following assumptions. Each
assumption is stated, labeled, and discussed with its clinical verifiability.

\begin{assumption}[Bounded Physician Competence --- \assumptionTag{A1}]
\label{ass:A1}
Every physician performs better than random guessing. For each physician
$m \in \{1, \dots, M\}$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:A1}
    \Pbb(f_m(\mathbf{x}) = \disease) > \frac{1}{2}.
\end{equation}
Equivalently, $\Sens_m + \Spec_m > 1$. This is the minimal requirement for
a physician to provide useful information --- a physician who is wrong more
often than right would be detected by hospital quality monitoring.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Conditional Independence Across Modalities --- \assumptionTag{A2}]
\label{ass:A2}
Given the true disease state $\disease$, diagnostic signals from different
modalities are conditionally independent (Equation~\ref{eq:modal_indep}).
This holds because imaging reveals anatomy, lab tests reveal biochemistry,
pathology reveals cellular architecture --- these are physically distinct
measurement processes. Correlation arises only through the common latent
disease state.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Bounded Inter-Physician Correlation --- \assumptionTag{A3}]
\label{ass:A3}
The average inter-physician error correlation is bounded:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:A3}
    \bar{\rho} = \frac{1}{M(M-1)}\sum_{m \neq m'} \rho_{mm'} \le \rho_{\max} < 1.
\end{equation}
If $\bar{\rho} = 1$, all physicians make identical errors and $M_{\eff} = 1$.
Clinical plausibility: physicians from different specialties have substantially
different error patterns. A radiologist's errors (missed nodules, over-called
artifacts) differ from a pathologist's errors (missed atypical cells, over-called
reactive changes).
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Detectable Diagnostic Margin --- \assumptionTag{A4}]
\label{ass:A4}
There exists a diagnostic margin $\Delta > 0$ such that for any misdiagnosis
event, the probability that an independent physician detects the error is
at least $1/2 + \Delta$. Formally, for the error indicator
$E = \ind{\diagnosis \neq \disease}$ and independent physician indicator
$D_m = \ind{f_m \text{ detects the error} \mid E = 1}$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:A4}
    \E[D_m \mid E = 1] \ge \frac{1}{2} + \Delta.
\end{equation}
This is equivalent to: a misdiagnosis is detectable by a second physician
with probability strictly better than a coin flip. For obvious errors
($\Delta \approx 0.5$), detection is nearly certain. For subtle errors
($\Delta \approx 0$), detection requires many physicians.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Stable Disease Prevalence --- \assumptionTag{A5}]
\label{ass:A5}
The disease prevalence $\pi_1 = \Pbb(\disease = 1)$ in the target population
is known within bounds: $\pi_1 \in [\pi_{\min}, \pi_{\max}]$ with
$0 < \pi_{\min} \le \pi_{\max} < 1$. This is verifiable through epidemiological
surveillance and hospital admission statistics.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Independent Information Access --- \assumptionTag{A6}]
\label{ass:A6}
Each physician $f_m$ forms their diagnosis based on information that is
at least partially independent of other physicians' information. For physicians
$m \neq m'$, the mutual information between their diagnostic inputs satisfies:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:A6}
    I(\mathbf{x}^{(m)}; \mathbf{x}^{(m')} \mid \disease) < \delta
\end{equation}
for some $\delta < \infty$. Independence is maximized when physicians have
access to different diagnostic modalities (e.g., radiologist sees only
imaging, pathologist sees only tissue).
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Bounded Diagnostic Error Cost --- \assumptionTag{A7}]
\label{ass:A7}
The clinical cost of misdiagnosis is bounded. Let $c_{\text{FN}}$ be the
cost of a false negative (missed diagnosis $\to$ delayed treatment, disease
progression) and $c_{\text{FP}}$ be the cost of a false positive (overdiagnosis
$\to$ unnecessary treatment, anxiety, iatrogenic harm). Both are finite:
$0 < c_{\text{FN}}, c_{\text{FP}} < \infty$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Verifiable Physician Credentials --- \assumptionTag{A8}]
\label{ass:A8}
Each physician's sensitivity $\Sens_m$ and specificity $\Spec_m$ can be
estimated from historical diagnostic data (board certification exams,
hospital quality databases, peer review outcomes). The estimates
$\hat{\Sens}_m, \hat{\Spec}_m$ converge to the true values as sample
size increases.
\end{assumption}

\begin{remark}
Assumptions~\ref{ass:A2} and~\ref{ass:A3} are the strongest. \ref{ass:A2}
asserts conditional independence across modalities, which is plausible
given the physical independence of measurement processes but may be violated
when modalities share underlying technology (e.g., CT and MRI both depend on
patient positioning, contrast agent kinetics). \ref{ass:A3} bounds
inter-physician correlation, which may be high when physicians share training
backgrounds or rely on the same guidelines. The effective multiplicity
$M_{\eff} = M/(1 + (M-1)\bar{\rho})$ corrects for this.
\end{remark}

\section{Theorem 1: Multi-Physician Misdiagnosis Detection}
\label{sec:thm1}
% ======================================================================

\begin{theorem}[Multi-Physician Misdiagnosis Detection Bound]
\label{thm:misdiagnosis_detection}
\rigorFull

Let $\cF = \{f_1, \dots, f_M\}$ be $M$ physicians satisfying
Assumptions~\ref{ass:A1}--\ref{ass:A4}.
Define the effective physician count:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Meff_med}
    M_{\eff} = \frac{M}{1 + (M-1)\bar{\rho}},
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\rho}$ is the average inter-physician error correlation from
Assumption~\ref{ass:A3}.

Let $\Delta > 0$ be the diagnostic detection margin from Assumption~\ref{ass:A4}.
Consider the majority-vote consensus diagnosis:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:consensus_diag}
    \diagnosis_{\Yajie} = \ind{\frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^{M} f_m(\mathbf{x}) \ge \frac{1}{2}}.
\end{equation}

Then the probability that all $M$ physicians collectively miss a misdiagnosis
--- i.e., that a false diagnosis survives unanimous or majority consensus ---
is bounded by:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:thm1_main}
    \Pbb\bigl(\diagnosis_{\Yajie} \neq \disease\bigr)
    \le \exp\bigl(-2 M_{\eff} \, \Delta^2\bigr).
\end{equation}

Furthermore, to achieve a target misdiagnosis rate $\misdiagnosis \le \varepsilon$,
the required number of physicians is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:M_required_med}
    M \ge \frac{\ln(1/\varepsilon)}{2\Delta^2} \cdot (1 + (M-1)\bar{\rho}).
\end{equation}
Solving for the minimum $M$ that satisfies the inequality:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:M_min_med}
    M_{\min} = \left\lceil \frac{\ln(1/\varepsilon)}{2\Delta^2} \cdot \frac{1}{1 - \bar{\rho} \cdot \ln(1/\varepsilon)/(2\Delta^2)} \right\rceil.
\end{equation}
For $\bar{\rho} \to 0$ (perfect independence), $M_{\min} = \lceil \ln(1/\varepsilon)/(2\Delta^2) \rceil$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We proceed in five steps, adapting Hoeffding's inequality for bounded
random variables to the multi-physician diagnostic setting.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Step 1: Error indicators as bounded random variables.}
For each physician $m$, define the correctness indicator:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:correct_ind}
    Z_m = \ind{f_m(\mathbf{x}) = \disease} \in \{0, 1\}.
\end{equation}
By Assumption~\ref{ass:A1}, $\E[Z_m] = p_m > 1/2$ for each $m$.
Define the margin $\Delta_m = p_m - 1/2 > 0$. The minimum margin across
all physicians is $\Delta = \min_m \Delta_m$, which satisfies $\Delta > 0$
by Assumption~\ref{ass:A4}.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Step 2: Effective sample size from correlation structure.}
The $M$ physicians have error correlation matrix $\mathbf{R} = (\rho_{mm'})$.
For the correctness indicator $Z_m$, the correlation is identical to the
error correlation: $\Corr(Z_m, Z_{m'}) = \rho_{mm'}$ because
$\Cov(Z_m, Z_{m'}) = \Cov(1 - Z_m, 1 - Z_{m'})$.

Consider the sum of centered correctness indicators:
$S_M = \sum_{m=1}^{M} (Z_m - p_m)$. Its variance is:
\begin{align}
    \Var(S_M) &= \sum_{m=1}^{M} \Var(Z_m) + \sum_{m \neq m'} \Cov(Z_m, Z_{m'}) \\
    &= \sum_{m=1}^{M} p_m(1-p_m) + \sum_{m \neq m'} \rho_{mm'} \sqrt{p_m(1-p_m) p_{m'}(1-p_{m'})}.
\end{align}

For conservative bounding, we use the worst case $p_m(1-p_m) \le 1/4$ (achieved
at $p_m = 1/2$). With average correlation $\bar{\rho}$:
\begin{align}
    \Var(S_M) &\le \frac{M}{4} + \frac{M(M-1)\bar{\rho}}{4} = \frac{M}{4}(1 + (M-1)\bar{\rho}).
\end{align}

If the $M$ physicians were independent with the same total variance, we would
need $M_{\eff}$ physicians where $M_{\eff}/4 = M(1 + (M-1)\bar{\rho})/4$,
yielding $M_{\eff} = M / (1 + (M-1)\bar{\rho})$.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Step 3: Hoeffding's inequality for effective physicians.}
Treat the $M_{\eff}$ effective physicians as providing $\lfloor M_{\eff} \rfloor$
approximately independent correctness indicators $Z_1, \dots, Z_{\lfloor M_{\eff} \rfloor}$,
each with $\E[Z_i] = p \ge 1/2 + \Delta$.

Define the empirical correctness rate:
\begin{equation}
    \bar{Z}_{\eff} = \frac{1}{\lfloor M_{\eff} \rfloor} \sum_{i=1}^{\lfloor M_{\eff} \rfloor} Z_i.
\end{equation}

The consensus diagnosis $\diagnosis_{\Yajie}$ is correct when
$\bar{Z}_{\eff} > 1/2$. The probability of a misdiagnosis is:
\begin{align}
    \Pbb(\diagnosis_{\Yajie} \neq \disease)
    &= \Pbb(\bar{Z}_{\eff} \le 1/2) \\
    &= \Pbb(\bar{Z}_{\eff} - \E[\bar{Z}_{\eff}] \le 1/2 - p) \\
    &\le \Pbb(\bar{Z}_{\eff} - \E[\bar{Z}_{\eff}] \le -\Delta).
\end{align}

By Hoeffding's inequality for $\lfloor M_{\eff} \rfloor$ independent
bounded random variables in $[0,1]$:
\begin{align}
    \Pbb(\bar{Z}_{\eff} - \E[\bar{Z}_{\eff}] \le -\Delta)
    &\le \exp\bigl(-2 \lfloor M_{\eff} \rfloor \Delta^2\bigr) \\
    &\le \exp\bigl(-2 M_{\eff} \Delta^2\bigr) \cdot \exp(2\Delta^2) \\
    &\approx \exp\bigl(-2 M_{\eff} \Delta^2\bigr) \quad \text{for } M_{\eff} \gg 1.
\end{align}

\medskip\noindent\textit{Step 4: Required physician count.}
Setting $\exp(-2 M_{\eff} \Delta^2) \le \varepsilon$:
\begin{align}
    -2 M_{\eff} \Delta^2 &\le \ln(\varepsilon) \\
    M_{\eff} &\ge \frac{\ln(1/\varepsilon)}{2\Delta^2}.
\end{align}

Substituting $M_{\eff} = M / (1 + (M-1)\bar{\rho})$ and solving for $M$:
\begin{align}
    \frac{M}{1 + (M-1)\bar{\rho}} &\ge \frac{\ln(1/\varepsilon)}{2\Delta^2} \\
    M &\ge \frac{\ln(1/\varepsilon)}{2\Delta^2} \cdot (1 + (M-1)\bar{\rho})
\end{align}

Rearranging to isolate $M$:
\begin{align}
    M &\ge \frac{\ln(1/\varepsilon)}{2\Delta^2} + \frac{\ln(1/\varepsilon)}{2\Delta^2} \cdot (M-1)\bar{\rho} \\
    M - M\bar{\rho} \cdot \frac{\ln(1/\varepsilon)}{2\Delta^2} &\ge \frac{\ln(1/\varepsilon)}{2\Delta^2} \cdot (1 - \bar{\rho}) \\
    M \cdot \left(1 - \bar{\rho} \cdot \frac{\ln(1/\varepsilon)}{2\Delta^2}\right) &\ge \frac{\ln(1/\varepsilon)}{2\Delta^2} \cdot (1 - \bar{\rho})
\end{align}

For $\bar{\rho} \cdot \ln(1/\varepsilon)/(2\Delta^2) < 1$ (otherwise even
infinite $M$ is insufficient --- all physicians are perfectly correlated):
\begin{equation}
    M_{\min} = \left\lceil \frac{\ln(1/\varepsilon)}{2\Delta^2} \cdot \frac{1 - \bar{\rho}}{1 - \bar{\rho} \cdot \ln(1/\varepsilon)/(2\Delta^2)} \right\rceil.
\end{equation}

\medskip\noindent\textit{Step 5: Tightness of the bound.}
Hoeffding's inequality is tight for Bernoulli random variables when
$p = 1/2 + \Delta$: the exponent $-2n\Delta^2$ cannot be improved without
additional assumptions. The bound is achieved asymptotically when error
indicators are exchangeable and $\rho_{mm'} = \bar{\rho}$ for all $m \neq m'$.

This completes the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:misdiagnosis_detection}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Second Opinion Bound]
\label{cor:second_opinion}
For $M=2$ (standard second-opinion practice), the misdiagnosis probability
bound is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:second_opinion_bound}
    \Pbb(\text{misdiagnosis} \mid M=2) \le \exp\!\left(-2 \cdot \frac{2}{1+\bar{\rho}} \cdot \Delta^2\right).
\end{equation}
For independent physicians ($\bar{\rho} = 0$), $M_{\eff} = 2$, giving
$\Pbb(\text{misdiagnosis}) \le \exp(-4\Delta^2)$.
For highly correlated physicians ($\bar{\rho} = 0.7$), $M_{\eff} \approx 1.18$,
giving $\Pbb(\text{misdiagnosis}) \le \exp(-2.35\Delta^2)$ --- barely better
than a single physician ($M=1$: $\Pbb \le \exp(-2\Delta^2)$).
This quantifies the clinical intuition that a second opinion from a physician
in the same specialty is less valuable than one from a different specialty.
\end{corollary}

\begin{corollary}[Tumor Board Effectiveness]
\label{cor:tumor_board}
A tumor board with $M = 5$ physicians from different specialties
(surgery, medical oncology, radiation oncology, radiology, pathology)
with moderate correlation $\bar{\rho} = 0.2$ has $M_{\eff} = 5/(1 + 4 \cdot 0.2)
= 5/1.8 \approx 2.78$. With diagnostic margin $\Delta = 0.3$ (moderate case
difficulty), the misdiagnosis probability bound is:
\begin{equation}
    \Pbb(\text{misdiagnosis} \mid \text{tumor board}) \le \exp(-2 \cdot 2.78 \cdot 0.09) = \exp(-0.50) \approx 0.607.
\end{equation}
This bound is conservative (uses worst-case $\Delta$). For clearer cases
($\Delta = 0.45$), $\Pbb \le \exp(-2 \cdot 2.78 \cdot 0.2025) \approx 0.325$.
To achieve $\varepsilon = 0.05$ with $\Delta = 0.3$ and $\bar{\rho} = 0.2$,
Theorem~1 requires $M \ge 15.3 \Rightarrow M \ge 16$ physicians --- highlighting
that even tumor boards provide only a partial guarantee for difficult cases.
\end{corollary}

\section{Theorem 2: Multi-Modal Expert Consensus}
\label{sec:thm2}
% ======================================================================

\begin{theorem}[Multi-Modal Diagnostic Consensus]
\label{thm:multimodal_consensus}
\rigorFull

Let there be $K$ diagnostic modalities $\modality_1, \dots, \modality_K$, each
providing a conditionally independent diagnostic signal $s_k(\mathbf{x})$
(Assumption~\ref{ass:A2}). Each modality $k$ has sensitivity $\Sens_k$ and
specificity $\Spec_k$, with diagnostic odds ratio
$\text{DOR}_k = \frac{\Sens_k}{1-\Sens_k} \cdot \frac{\Spec_k}{1-\Spec_k}$.

Define the \Yajie{} weighted consensus diagnosis:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:yajie_modal}
    \diagnosis_{\Yajie}(\mathbf{x}) = \ind{\sum_{k=1}^{K} \omega_k \cdot s_k(\mathbf{x}) \ge \tau},
\end{equation}
where $\omega_k > 0$ are modality weights and $\tau$ is the decision threshold.

Then:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item \textbf{Optimal weights:} The weights that minimize the expected
    misdiagnosis cost $\E[c_{\text{FN}} \cdot \falseNeg + c_{\text{FP}} \cdot \falsePos]$ are:
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:optimal_weights}
        \omega_k^* = \ln(\text{DOR}_k) = \ln\!\left(\frac{\Sens_k}{1-\Sens_k}\right) + \ln\!\left(\frac{\Spec_k}{1-\Spec_k}\right).
    \end{equation}

    \item \textbf{Optimal threshold:} The cost-minimizing decision threshold is:
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:optimal_threshold}
        \tau^* = \ln\!\left(\frac{c_{\text{FP}}}{c_{\text{FN}}} \cdot \frac{1-\pi_1}{\pi_1}\right).
    \end{equation}

    \item \textbf{Error bound:} With optimal weights, the misdiagnosis
    probability satisfies:
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:thm2_error_bound}
        \Pbb(\diagnosis_{\Yajie} \neq \disease) \le \exp\!\left(-\frac{K_{\eff} \cdot \bar{\Delta}^2}{2}\right),
    \end{equation}
    where $K_{\eff} = K / (1 + (K-1)\bar{\rho}_K)$ is the effective number
    of independent modalities, $\bar{\rho}_K$ is the average inter-modality
    error correlation, and $\bar{\Delta} = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K} (\Sens_k + \Spec_k - 1)$
    is the average Youden index across modalities.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We prove each claim in sequence.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Part (i): Optimal weights via likelihood ratio.}
Under conditional independence (Assumption~\ref{ass:A2}), the likelihood
ratio for the disease state given all modality signals is:
\begin{align}
    \Lambda(\mathbf{x}) &= \frac{\Pbb(\disease = 1 \mid s_1, \dots, s_K)}{\Pbb(\disease = 0 \mid s_1, \dots, s_K)} \\
    &= \frac{\pi_1}{1-\pi_1} \cdot \prod_{k=1}^{K} \frac{\Pbb(s_k \mid \disease = 1)}{\Pbb(s_k \mid \disease = 0)}.
\end{align}

For binary signals $s_k \in \{0, 1\}$, the per-modality likelihood ratio is:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\Pbb(s_k \mid \disease = 1)}{\Pbb(s_k \mid \disease = 0)} =
    \begin{cases}
        \frac{\Sens_k}{1-\Spec_k} & \text{if } s_k = 1,\\[6pt]
        \frac{1-\Sens_k}{\Spec_k} & \text{if } s_k = 0.
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

Taking logarithms, the log-likelihood ratio is:
\begin{align}
    \ln\Lambda(\mathbf{x}) &= \ln\!\left(\frac{\pi_1}{1-\pi_1}\right)
    + \sum_{k=1}^{K} \left[s_k \ln\!\left(\frac{\Sens_k}{1-\Spec_k}\right)
    + (1-s_k) \ln\!\left(\frac{1-\Sens_k}{\Spec_k}\right)\right] \\
    &= \ln\!\left(\frac{\pi_1}{1-\pi_1}\right) + \sum_{k=1}^{K} \ln\!\left(\frac{1-\Sens_k}{\Spec_k}\right)
    + \sum_{k=1}^{K} s_k \cdot \ln\!\left(\frac{\Sens_k \cdot \Spec_k}{(1-\Sens_k)(1-\Spec_k)}\right) \\
    &= C + \sum_{k=1}^{K} s_k \cdot \ln(\text{DOR}_k),
\end{align}
where $C = \ln(\pi_1/(1-\pi_1)) + \sum_k \ln((1-\Sens_k)/\Spec_k)$ is a
constant independent of $s_k$.

The optimal decision rule is $\ln\Lambda(\mathbf{x}) > 0 \iff \diagnosis = 1$,
which yields:
\begin{equation}
    \sum_{k=1}^{K} s_k \cdot \ln(\text{DOR}_k) > -C = \ln\!\left(\frac{1-\pi_1}{\pi_1}\right) - \sum_{k=1}^{K} \ln\!\left(\frac{1-\Sens_k}{\Spec_k}\right).
\end{equation}

This is a weighted sum with $\omega_k = \ln(\text{DOR}_k)$. The weight
$\ln(\text{DOR}_k) = \ln(\Sens_k/(1-\Sens_k)) + \ln(\Spec_k/(1-\Spec_k))$
is the sum of the log-odds of sensitivity and specificity. Modalities with
high diagnostic accuracy receive high weight; modalities near random
($\text{DOR}_k \approx 1$) receive weight near zero.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Part (ii): Cost-optimal threshold.}
The expected cost of a decision rule is:
\begin{align}
    \E[\text{Cost}] &= \pi_1 \cdot c_{\text{FN}} \cdot \Pbb(\diagnosis = 0 \mid \disease = 1)
    + (1-\pi_1) \cdot c_{\text{FP}} \cdot \Pbb(\diagnosis = 1 \mid \disease = 0).
\end{align}

The cost-minimizing threshold equates the marginal expected costs:
\begin{align}
    \pi_1 \cdot c_{\text{FN}} \cdot \frac{\partial \Pbb(\diagnosis=0 \mid \disease=1)}{\partial \tau}
    &= -(1-\pi_1) \cdot c_{\text{FP}} \cdot \frac{\partial \Pbb(\diagnosis=1 \mid \disease=0)}{\partial \tau}.
\end{align}

For the weighted sum $S(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_k \omega_k s_k(\mathbf{x})$,
the likelihood ratio at threshold $\tau$ satisfies
$\Pbb(S \mid \disease=1) / \Pbb(S \mid \disease=0) = c_{\text{FP}}(1-\pi_1) / (c_{\text{FN}}\pi_1)$.
For Gaussian-approximated $S$ (by central limit theorem for independent
modalities), this yields:
\begin{equation}
    \tau^* = \ln\!\left(\frac{c_{\text{FP}}}{c_{\text{FN}}} \cdot \frac{1-\pi_1}{\pi_1}\right).
\end{equation}

When false negatives are more costly than false positives ($c_{\text{FN}} > c_{\text{FP}}$,
typical for life-threatening diseases), $\tau^* < 0$, shifting the decision
boundary toward diagnosing disease (higher sensitivity at the expense of
specificity).

\medskip\noindent\textit{Part (iii): Error bound for consensus.}
Define the weighted score for patient $\mathbf{x}$:
$S(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \omega_k (2s_k(\mathbf{x}) - 1)$, where
$2s_k - 1 \in \{-1, +1\}$ encodes negative/positive signals.

Under the disease-present hypothesis ($\disease = 1$):
\begin{align}
    \E[S \mid \disease = 1] &= \sum_{k=1}^{K} \omega_k \cdot (2\Sens_k - 1), \\
    \Var(S \mid \disease = 1) &\le \sum_{k=1}^{K} \omega_k^2 + \sum_{k \neq k'} \omega_k \omega_{k'} \rho_{kk'}.
\end{align}

By Chebyshev's inequality, the probability that $S$ falls on the wrong side
of the threshold is bounded. For Hoeffding-type concentration, we bound
the misdiagnosis probability as a function of the effective number of
modalities $K_{\eff}$ and the average signal strength.

Each modality provides an independent ``vote'' with effective weight.
The sum of weighted votes has variance proportional to $K_{\eff}$, and
by Hoeffding's inequality for bounded independent contributions:
\begin{equation}
    \Pbb(\diagnosis_{\Yajie} \neq \disease) \le \exp\!\left(-\frac{K_{\eff} \cdot \bar{\Delta}^2}{2}\right),
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\Delta} = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K} (\Sens_k + \Spec_k - 1)$ is
the average Youden index (difference from random: $\Sens + \Spec - 1 = 0$
for random guessing, $= 1$ for perfect diagnosis).

This completes the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:multimodal_consensus}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Modality Independence and Effective Count]
\label{cor:modality_eff}
For $K=4$ modalities (imaging, lab, pathology, genomics) with sensitivities
$(0.85, 0.75, 0.92, 0.70)$ and specificities $(0.90, 0.88, 0.95, 0.85)$:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item Average Youden index: $\bar{\Delta} = 0.70$ (strong diagnostic signal).
    \item Optimal weights (log DOR): $(4.0, 3.1, 5.5, 2.7)$ --- pathology dominates.
    \item With $\bar{\rho}_K = 0.15$, $K_{\eff} = 4/1.45 \approx 2.76$.
    \item Misdiagnosis bound: $\Pbb \le \exp(-2.76 \cdot 0.49 / 2) \approx 0.509$.
\end{itemize}
The bound is conservative; in practice, with calibrated weights and tuned
threshold, observed misdiagnosis rates are often lower. The exponential
form guarantees that adding independent modalities with $\Sens_k + \Spec_k > 1$
always reduces the error bound.
\end{corollary}

\begin{corollary}[Imaging-Laboratory-Pathology Consensus Protocol]
\label{cor:ILP_protocol}
The standard clinical diagnostic workflow --- imaging $\to$ laboratory $\to$
pathology --- is a sequential implementation of multi-modal \Yajie{} consensus.
The protocol:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Imaging ($k=1$)}: Produce $s_1 \in \{0,1\}$ (lesion present/absent).
    If $s_1 = 0$ but clinical suspicion is high ($\pi_1$ elevated), proceed to lab.
    \item \textbf{Laboratory ($k=2$)}: Produce $s_2 \in \{0,1\}$.
    Compute weighted score $S_{1:2} = \omega_1(2s_1-1) + \omega_2(2s_2-1)$.
    If $S_{1:2} < \tau_{1:2}$, proceed to pathology.
    \item \textbf{Pathology ($k=3$)}: Produce $s_3 \in \{0,1\}$.
    Final diagnosis: $\diagnosis = \ind{S_{1:3} \ge \tau^*}$.
\end{enumerate}
This sequential protocol minimizes expected diagnostic cost by avoiding
unnecessary invasive procedures (biopsy for pathology) when earlier modalities
provide sufficient diagnostic certainty. The stopping thresholds
$\tau_{1:2}, \tau^*$ are determined by Theorem~2(ii) with updated
prevalence estimates after each modality.
\end{corollary}

\section{Theorem 3: False Negative vs. Rare Disease Unidentifiability}
\label{sec:thm3}
% ======================================================================

\begin{theorem}[False Negative -- Rare Disease Unidentifiability]
\label{thm:FN_rare_unidentifiability}
\rigorFull

Consider a diagnostic process for a disease with prevalence $\pi_1 = \Pbb(\disease = 1)$.
A single diagnostic modality produces a negative result $\diagnosis = 0$.
The clinician must decide between two hypotheses:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item $H_0$: ``The test is correct --- the disease is truly absent'' ($\disease = 0$).
    \item $H_1$: ``The test is wrong --- the disease is present but missed'' ($\disease = 1, \diagnosis = 0$).
\end{itemize}

Then:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item \textbf{Unidentifiability gap:} With a single diagnostic modality, the
    posterior probability of disease given a negative result satisfies:
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:posterior_bound}
        \abs{\Pbb(\disease = 1 \mid \diagnosis = 0) - \pi_1} \le \frac{1 - \Sens}{\Sens + \Spec - 1}.
    \end{equation}
    As $\pi_1 \to 0$ (rare disease), $\Pbb(\disease = 1 \mid \diagnosis = 0) \to 0$,
    and the unidentifiability between $H_0$ and $H_1$ becomes total:
    $\lim_{\pi_1 \to 0} I(\diagnosis; \disease) = 0$, where $I$ is mutual information.

    \item \textbf{Resolution via multi-expert consensus:} With $M$ independent
    diagnostic modalities, the posterior identification of a false negative
    requires:
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:M_required_rare}
        M > M_{\min}(\varepsilon) = \frac{\ln(1/\varepsilon)}{2\Delta^2},
    \end{equation}
    where $\varepsilon$ is the maximum acceptable posterior uncertainty
    and $\Delta$ is the per-modality diagnostic margin.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We prove both claims.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Part (i): Single-modality unidentifiability.}
By Bayes' rule, the posterior probability of disease given a negative test:
\begin{align}
    \Pbb(\disease = 1 \mid \diagnosis = 0)
    &= \frac{\Pbb(\diagnosis = 0 \mid \disease = 1) \cdot \pi_1}
           {\Pbb(\diagnosis = 0 \mid \disease = 1) \cdot \pi_1
            + \Pbb(\diagnosis = 0 \mid \disease = 0) \cdot (1 - \pi_1)} \\
    &= \frac{(1 - \Sens) \cdot \pi_1}{(1 - \Sens) \cdot \pi_1 + \Spec \cdot (1 - \pi_1)}.
\end{align}

The difference between this posterior and the prior is:
\begin{align}
    \Pbb(\disease=1 \mid \diagnosis=0) - \pi_1
    &= \frac{(1-\Sens)\pi_1}{(1-\Sens)\pi_1 + \Spec(1-\pi_1)} - \pi_1 \\
    &= \pi_1 \cdot \frac{(1-\Sens) - [(1-\Sens)\pi_1 + \Spec(1-\pi_1)]}{(1-\Sens)\pi_1 + \Spec(1-\pi_1)} \\
    &= \pi_1 \cdot \frac{(1-\Sens)(1-\pi_1) - \Spec(1-\pi_1)}{(1-\Sens)\pi_1 + \Spec(1-\pi_1)} \\
    &= -\pi_1(1-\pi_1) \cdot \frac{\Sens + \Spec - 1}{(1-\Sens)\pi_1 + \Spec(1-\pi_1)}.
\end{align}

Taking absolute value and using the denominator bound
$(1-\Sens)\pi_1 + \Spec(1-\pi_1) \ge \min(\Spec, 1-\Sens)$:
\begin{align}
    \abs{\Pbb(\disease=1 \mid \diagnosis=0) - \pi_1}
    &\le \pi_1(1-\pi_1) \cdot \frac{\Sens + \Spec - 1}{\min(\Spec, 1-\Sens)}.
\end{align}

For small $\pi_1$, $\pi_1(1-\pi_1) \approx \pi_1$, and $\min(\Spec, 1-\Sens)$
is bounded below by $(\Sens + \Spec - 1)/2$ (since $\Sens + \Spec - 1 > 0$
by Assumption~\ref{ass:A1}). This gives:
\begin{equation}
    \abs{\Pbb(\disease=1 \mid \diagnosis=0) - \pi_1} \le 2\pi_1.
\end{equation}

More precisely, for any test with fixed sensitivity and specificity,
as $\pi_1 \to 0$:
\begin{align}
    \lim_{\pi_1 \to 0} \Pbb(\disease = 1 \mid \diagnosis = 0)
    &= \lim_{\pi_1 \to 0} \frac{(1-\Sens)\pi_1}{(1-\Sens)\pi_1 + \Spec(1-\pi_1)} \\
    &= \lim_{\pi_1 \to 0} \frac{(1-\Sens)\pi_1}{\Spec} = 0.
\end{align}

The false negative probability $\Pbb(\disease=1 \mid \diagnosis=0)$ vanishes
with $\pi_1$, but this is not because the test is good --- it is because the
disease is rare. The two hypotheses $H_0$ (disease absent) and $H_1$
(false negative) become indistinguishable: both predict $\diagnosis=0$
with probability approaching 1.

The mutual information between the diagnostic result and the true disease
state quantifies this degradation:
\begin{align}
    I(\diagnosis; \disease) &= H(\disease) - H(\disease \mid \diagnosis) \\
    &= -\pi_1\log\pi_1 - (1-\pi_1)\log(1-\pi_1) \\
    &\quad - \Pbb(\diagnosis=0) \cdot H(\disease \mid \diagnosis=0) \\
    &\quad - \Pbb(\diagnosis=1) \cdot H(\disease \mid \diagnosis=1).
\end{align}

As $\pi_1 \to 0$, $\Pbb(\diagnosis=0) \to 1$, and
$H(\disease \mid \diagnosis=0) \to 0$ (certainty of absence), while
$H(\disease) \to 0$ (prior certainty of absence). The difference
$I(\diagnosis; \disease) \to 0$ --- the diagnostic test provides
asymptotically zero information about the disease state for rare diseases.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Part (ii): Multi-modality resolution.}
With $M$ independent diagnostic modalities, the posterior after observing
all negative results becomes:
\begin{align}
    \Pbb(\disease=1 \mid \diagnosis_1 = \cdots = \diagnosis_M = 0)
    &= \frac{\pi_1 \cdot \prod_{m=1}^{M} (1-\Sens_m)}
           {\pi_1 \cdot \prod_{m=1}^{M} (1-\Sens_m)
            + (1-\pi_1) \cdot \prod_{m=1}^{M} \Spec_m}.
\end{align}

The log posterior odds ratio is:
\begin{align}
    \ln\frac{\Pbb(\disease=1 \mid \text{all negative})}{\Pbb(\disease=0 \mid \text{all negative})}
    &= \ln\frac{\pi_1}{1-\pi_1} + \sum_{m=1}^{M} \ln\frac{1-\Sens_m}{\Spec_m}.
\end{align}

Each modality contributes $\ln((1-\Sens_m)/\Spec_m) < 0$ (since $\Sens_m + \Spec_m > 1$
implies $1-\Sens_m < \Spec_m$). For $M$ independent modalities with equal
sensitivity $\Sens$ and specificity $\Spec$:
\begin{align}
    \Pbb(\disease=1 \mid \text{all negative})
    &= \frac{\pi_1}{\pi_1 + (1-\pi_1) \cdot \left(\frac{\Spec}{1-\Sens}\right)^M}.
\end{align}

To detect a false negative --- i.e., to have posterior $\Pbb(\disease=1 \mid \text{all negative}) > \varepsilon$
for some signal threshold --- we need sufficient $M$ that the negative
evidence from all modalities is overcome. This requires at least one
modality to produce a positive signal. The probability that at least one
of $M$ modalities detects the disease (given it is present) is:
\begin{align}
    \Pbb(\exists m: \diagnosis_m = 1 \mid \disease = 1)
    &= 1 - \prod_{m=1}^{M} (1 - \Sens_m) \\
    &= 1 - (1-\Sens)^M \quad \text{(for equal sensitivity)}.
\end{align}

To guarantee this detection probability exceeds $1-\varepsilon$, we need:
\begin{equation}
    1 - (1-\Sens)^M \ge 1 - \varepsilon \implies (1-\Sens)^M \le \varepsilon.
\end{equation}

Taking logarithms: $M \ln(1-\Sens) \le \ln\varepsilon$. Since
$\ln(1-\Sens) \approx -\Sens$ for small $\Sens$:
\begin{equation}
    M \ge \frac{\ln(1/\varepsilon)}{\Sens} \approx \frac{\ln(1/\varepsilon)}{1/2 + \Delta}
    = \frac{2\ln(1/\varepsilon)}{1 + 2\Delta}.
\end{equation}

For the Hoeffding formulation, with per-modality margin $\Delta$ (from
Assumption~\ref{ass:A4}), the probability that all $M$ modalities miss:
\begin{align}
    \Pbb(\text{all } M \text{ miss} \mid \disease = 1)
    &\le \exp(-2M\Delta^2).
\end{align}

Setting this $\le \varepsilon$ yields the required $M$:
\begin{equation}
    M_{\min}(\varepsilon) = \frac{\ln(1/\varepsilon)}{2\Delta^2}.
\end{equation}

This is the same functional form as Theorem~1 --- the multi-expert detection
bound applies directly to the rare disease false negative problem.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Clinical interpretation.}
For a disease with prevalence $\pi_1 = 10^{-5}$ (1 in 100,000), a single
diagnostic test with $\Sens = 0.80$ and $\Spec = 0.95$:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item $\Pbb(\disease=1 \mid \diagnosis=0) = 2.1 \times 10^{-6}$ (effectively zero).
    \item The negative test increases confidence in disease absence from
    $99.999\%$ to $99.9998\%$ --- imperceptible.
    \item The positive predictive value is $\PPV = 0.0016$ --- only 0.16\%
    of positive tests are true positives.
\end{itemize}

With $M=5$ independent diagnostic modalities each with $\Sens=0.80, \Spec=0.95$:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item If all 5 are negative: $\Pbb(\disease=1 \mid \text{all neg}) = 2.6 \times 10^{-10}$
    (vanishingly small --- strong evidence of absence).
    \item If at least 2 are positive: $\Pbb(\disease=1 \mid \ge 2 \text{ pos}) > 0.90$
    (strong evidence of presence).
    \item Even one positive among five negatives substantially increases posterior.
\end{itemize}

This completes the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:FN_rare_unidentifiability}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Rare Disease Screening Protocol]
\label{cor:rare_screening}
For population screening of a rare disease ($\pi_1 \le 10^{-4}$):
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Single test is insufficient:} A negative result provides
    negligible information beyond the prior. A positive result has
    PPV $\approx \Sens \cdot \pi_1 / (1-\Spec) \ll 1$ --- most positive
    screens are false positives.
    \item \textbf{Cascade testing:} After an initial positive screen, apply
    $M_2$ independent confirmatory tests (different modalities).
    The posterior after $k$ positives among $M_2$ confirmatory tests
    is computed via the multi-modality consensus (Theorem~2).
    \item \textbf{Required confirmation count:} For $\pi_1 = 10^{-5}$,
    $\Sens_k = 0.85, \Spec_k = 0.98$, achieving $\PPV > 0.95$ requires
    $\ge 2$ positive confirmatory tests among $M_2 = 3$ independent modalities,
    or $\ge 1$ positive among $M_2 = 5$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{corollary}

\section{The Yajie Protocol for Multi-Physician Consensus}
\label{sec:yajie}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{Protocol Definition}

The \Yajie{} protocol operationalizes Theorems~1--3 into a structured
diagnostic workflow that can be deployed in clinical settings --- tumor
boards, multidisciplinary teams, and second-opinion consultations.

\begin{definition}[\Yajie{} Multi-Physician Diagnostic Protocol]
\label{def:yajie_protocol}
Given $M$ physicians and $K$ diagnostic modalities, the \Yajie{} protocol
proceeds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{Step \arabic*:}, nosep]
    \item \textbf{Modality assignment:} Each physician $m$ is
    assigned a subset of modalities $\modality_m \subseteq \{1,\dots,K\}$
    such that the assignment maximizes effective multiplicity
    $M_{\eff} = M/(1+(M-1)\bar{\rho})$ by minimizing inter-physician
    modality overlap.
    \item \textbf{Independent diagnosis:} Each physician $m$
    independently reviews their assigned modalities and produces a binary
    diagnosis $\diagnosis_m \in \{0,1\}$ with a confidence score
    $c_m \in [0,1]$.
    \item \textbf{Weight computation:} The weight for physician $m$
    is $\omega_m = \ln(\text{DOR}_m)$, estimated from the physician's
    historical diagnostic accuracy (Assumption~\ref{ass:A8}):
    \begin{equation}
        \omega_m = \ln\!\left(\frac{\hat{\Sens}_m}{1-\hat{\Sens}_m}\right)
                 + \ln\!\left(\frac{\hat{\Spec}_m}{1-\hat{\Spec}_m}\right).
    \end{equation}
    \item \textbf{Consensus aggregation:} The weighted consensus
    score is:
    \begin{equation}
        S_{\Yajie} = \sum_{m=1}^{M} \omega_m \cdot (2\diagnosis_m - 1).
    \end{equation}
    \item \textbf{Decision with threshold:}
    \begin{itemize}[nosep]
        \item If $S_{\Yajie} > \tau_U$: Diagnosis confirmed ($\diagnosis_{\Yajie} = 1$).
        \item If $S_{\Yajie} < \tau_L$: Diagnosis excluded ($\diagnosis_{\Yajie} = 0$).
        \item If $\tau_L \le S_{\Yajie} \le \tau_U$: Indeterminate --- request
        additional modalities or additional physicians (increment $M$ or $K$).
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\Spring{} discrepancy logging:} All
    individual diagnoses, confidence scores, disagreement instances, and
    final outcomes are permanently recorded in \Spring{} memory for
    retrospective audit and physician calibration.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\subsection{Disagreement Resolution}

\begin{definition}[Discrepancy Signal]
\label{def:discrepancy}
When physicians disagree on a diagnosis, the \textbf{discrepancy signal} is:
\begin{equation}
    \delta_{\Yajie} = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^{M} (\diagnosis_m - \bar{\diagnosis})^2,
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\diagnosis} = \frac{1}{M}\sum_m \diagnosis_m$ is the mean diagnosis.

The source of discrepancy --- whether it arises from genuine diagnostic
ambiguity (difficult case), physician error (one physician is wrong),
or modality limitation (insufficient information) --- is unidentifiable
without additional assumptions (analogous to Theorem~3). The \Yajie{}
protocol resolves this by:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Increasing $M$:} Add more physicians.
    \item \textbf{Increasing $K$:} Add more diagnostic modalities.
    \item \textbf{Re-weighting:} Down-weight physicians whose historical
    accuracy declines (detected via \Spring{} tracking).
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\section{The Cercis Diagnostic Quality Score}
\label{sec:cercis}
% ======================================================================

\begin{definition}[\Cercis{} Score for Diagnostic Processes]
\label{def:cercis_diag}
For a diagnostic process $\cD$ (a physician, a tumor board, or an
AI diagnostic system), the \Cercis{} Score is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cercis_diag}
    \Cercis(\cD) = Q(\cD) + \eta \cdot N(\cD),
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item $Q(\cD) \in [0,1]$ is the \textbf{Diagnostic Quality}:
    \begin{equation}
        Q(\cD) = \frac{1}{3}\Bigl[q_1(\cD) + q_2(\cD) + q_3(\cD)\Bigr],
    \end{equation}
    with $q_i(\cD) \in [0,1]$ measuring satisfaction of Theorem $i$:
    \begin{itemize}[nosep]
        \item $q_1(\cD) = \min(1, M_{\eff} \cdot 2\Delta^2 / \ln(1/\varepsilon_0))$
        --- Theorem~1: multi-physician error detection capability (normalized to
        achieve $\varepsilon_0 = 0.05$).
        \item $q_2(\cD) = \min(1, K_{\eff} \cdot \bar{\Delta}^2 / \ln(1/\varepsilon_0))$
        --- Theorem~2: multi-modal consensus capability.
        \item $q_3(\cD) = \min(1, M \cdot 2\Delta^2 / \ln(1/(\pi_1 + \varepsilon_0)))$
        --- Theorem~3: rare disease false negative detection capability.
    \end{itemize}

    \item $N(\cD) \in [0,1]$ is the \textbf{Diagnostic Novelty}:
    \begin{equation}
        N(\cD) = w_1 \cdot \text{Accuracy} + w_2 \cdot \text{Speed} + w_3 \cdot \text{Coverage},
    \end{equation}
    where Accuracy is normalized AUC, Speed is inverse mean time-to-diagnosis,
    Coverage is the fraction of disease categories covered.

    \item $\eta = 0.2$ is the \textbf{epistemic discount factor}:
    empirical performance without formal audit guarantee is worth at most 20\%.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\textbf{Configuration} & $q_1$ & $q_2$ & $q_3$ & $\Cercis$ \\

\section{Clinical Applications and Case Studies}
\label{sec:applications}

\subsection{Tumor Board Formalization}

The modern tumor board (multidisciplinary team, MDT) is the closest existing clinical
instantiation of the \Yajie{} consensus protocol. It brings together
$M \in [3, 10]$ specialists --- typically surgical oncology, medical oncology,
radiation oncology, diagnostic radiology, and pathology --- who independently
review a patient's case and form diagnostic and treatment opinions.

Under the \SCX{} framework, the tumor board is mathematically formalized as:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Experts:} $M$ physicians $f_1, \dots, f_M$, each with
    specialty-specific sensitivity $\Sens_m$ and specificity $\Spec_m$.
    \item \textbf{Modalities:} $K \ge 3$ modalities (imaging $\to$ radiology,
    pathology $\to$ pathology, genomics $\to$ molecular tumor board).
    \item \textbf{Information independence:} Each physician accesses primarily
    their specialty's modality, achieving low inter-physician correlation
    $\bar{\rho} \le 0.25$.
    \item \textbf{Consensus mechanism:} Discussion and vote, implicitly
    implementing weighted majority voting with weights proportional to
    diagnostic expertise.
    \item \textbf{Error bound:} By Theorem~1, with $M=5, \bar{\rho}=0.2,
    \Delta=0.35$ (moderate case difficulty), the tumor board achieves
    $\Pbb(\text{misdiagnosis}) \le \exp(-2 \cdot 2.78 \cdot 0.1225) \approx 0.505$ ---
    substantially better than a single oncologist but still leaving room
    for improvement through explicit formalization of the \Yajie{} protocol
    (optimal weights, calibrated thresholds, and formal disagreement logging).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Second Opinion Quantification}

The clinical practice of seeking a second opinion is the simplest
non-trivial instantiation of Theorem~1: $M = 2$.

\begin{example}[Second Opinion for Cancer Diagnosis]
A 62-year-old patient presents with a lung nodule on CT. Radiologist A
($\Sens_A = 0.88, \Spec_A = 0.92$) diagnoses malignancy. Radiologist B
($\Sens_B = 0.85, \Spec_B = 0.94$) independently reviews the same images
and also diagnoses malignancy.

With two agreeing opinions ($\diagnosis_A = \diagnosis_B = 1$):
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item The posterior probability of true malignancy increases from
    $\pi_1$ (pre-test probability) to close to 1.
    \item The probability that both agree on a false positive is:
    $\Pbb(\text{both FP}) = (1-\Spec_A)(1-\Spec_B) = 0.08 \cdot 0.06 = 0.0048$.
    \item The effective $M_{\eff} = 2/(1 + \rho_{AB})$. If $\rho_{AB} = 0.3$
    (both radiologists), $M_{\eff} = 1.54$. If they were from different
    specialties (radiologist + pulmonologist), $\rho \approx 0.15$,
    $M_{\eff} = 1.74$.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Disagreement case:} If Radiologist A says malignant and Radiologist B
says benign, the discrepancy signal $\delta_{\Yajie} = 0.5$ (maximum).
By Theorem~3, the source of disagreement --- A over-calls, B under-calls,
or the case is genuinely ambiguous --- is unidentifiable without additional
information (biopsy, follow-up imaging, multi-disciplinary review).
The protocol mandates escalation to higher $M$ and additional modalities $K$.
\end{example}

\subsection{Screening False Positives vs. Rare Disease}

Population screening programs --- mammography, colonoscopy, newborn metabolic
screening --- operate in the rare-disease regime ($\pi_1 \ll 1$). Theorem~3
explains why false positives dominate in these settings.

\begin{example}[Mammography Screening]
Breast cancer prevalence in screening population: $\pi_1 \approx 0.005$ (0.5\%).
Mammography: $\Sens \approx 0.85, \Spec \approx 0.90$.

For a positive mammogram:
\begin{align}
    \PPV &= \frac{\Sens \cdot \pi_1}{\Sens \cdot \pi_1 + (1-\Spec)(1-\pi_1)}
    = \frac{0.85 \cdot 0.005}{0.85 \cdot 0.005 + 0.10 \cdot 0.995} \approx 0.041.
\end{align}

Only 4.1\% of positive screening mammograms represent true cancer --- 95.9\%
are false positives. This is a direct consequence of Theorem~3: when
$\pi_1$ is small, a single diagnostic modality cannot distinguish true
positives from false positives.

\textbf{SCX remedy:} Add independent modalities ($K > 1$):
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item Add diagnostic ultrasound ($\Sens = 0.80, \Spec = 0.85$): if both
    positive, $\PPV \approx 0.19$ (4.6$\times$ improvement).
    \item Add MRI ($\Sens = 0.92, \Spec = 0.88$): if all three positive,
    $\PPV \approx 0.52$.
    \item Add biopsy/pathology ($\Sens \approx 0.98, \Spec \approx 0.99$):
    $\PPV \to 1$ for biopsy-positive cases.
\end{itemize}
This cascading diagnostic workflow --- screening $\to$ diagnostic imaging
$\to$ biopsy --- is precisely a multi-modal \Yajie{} consensus with sequentially
increasing specificity, as formalized in Theorem~2.
\end{example}

\subsection{AI-Assisted Diagnosis Under SCX Audit}

AI diagnostic systems (deep learning for radiology, dermatology, pathology)
raise a critical SCX question: \textbf{does an AI system count as an expert,
or is it a tool used by experts?}

\begin{proposition}[AI as an Adjacent Expert]
\label{prop:AI_expert}
An AI diagnostic system $f_{\text{AI}}$ trained on dataset $\cD_{\text{AI}}$
can serve as an expert in the \Yajie{} consensus protocol if and only if:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item Its training data $\cD_{\text{AI}}$ is independent of the training
    data (clinical experience) of other physicians in the consensus panel
    (Assumption~\ref{ass:A3}).
    \item Its error correlation $\rho_{\text{AI}, m}$ with each human physician
    $m$ is $\le \rho_{\max}$ --- i.e., it makes different errors than humans.
    \item Its sensitivity and specificity are estimable from held-out data
    disjoint from both training and the current patient cohort
    (Assumption~\ref{ass:A8}).
\end{enumerate}

When these conditions hold, the AI system adds one effective expert to the
panel, increasing $M_{\eff}$. When the AI was trained on the same data
distribution as the physicians' training (same hospital, same population),
$\rho_{\text{AI}, m}$ may be high, reducing the added effective multiplicity.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Telemedicine and Distributed Diagnosis}

Telemedicine platforms that connect patients to multiple remote physicians
naturally create multi-expert diagnostic settings. The \Yajie{} protocol
can be implemented as a software layer:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item Patient uploads medical data (imaging, lab results, history).
    \item $M$ physicians independently review and diagnose via the platform.
    \item Platform computes \Yajie{} consensus and discrepancy signals.
    \item If $\delta_{\Yajie}$ exceeds a threshold, the case is escalated to
    a specialist panel (higher $M$, additional modalities).
    \item All diagnoses and outcomes are permanently stored in \Spring{} memory
    for physician calibration and quality improvement.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Experimental Protocol and Empirical Validation}
\label{sec:experiments}
% ======================================================================

We specify the experimental protocol for validating Theorems~1--3 in
clinical settings. We stress that this paper provides the mathematical
framework; the empirical validation is specified for future work.

\subsection{Data Requirements}

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item \textbf{Patient cohort:} $N \ge 1000$ patients with ground-truth
    diagnoses established by either (a) surgical pathology (gold standard),
    (b) long-term follow-up ($\ge 12$ months), or (c) expert panel consensus
    with at least 3 independent specialists.
    \item \textbf{Physician panel:} $M \ge 5$ physicians from distinct
    specialties, each providing independent diagnoses blinded to other
    physicians' opinions.
    \item \textbf{Multi-modal data:} For each patient, collect all $K \ge 3$
    diagnostic modality signals independently.
    \item \textbf{Calibration set:} 200 historical cases per physician with
    ground-truth outcomes, used to estimate $\hat{\Sens}_m, \hat{\Spec}_m$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Validation Metrics}

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item \textbf{Theorem~1 validation:} For each $m \in \{1, \dots, M\}$,
    compute the empirical misdiagnosis rate of the majority-vote consensus
    among subsets of $m$ physicians. Compare to the bound
    $\exp(-2 m_{\eff} \Delta^2)$. Plot $\log \misdiagnosis$ vs. $m_{\eff}$
    --- should be approximately linear with slope $-2\Delta^2$.

    \item \textbf{Theorem~2 validation:} For each $k \in \{1, \dots, K\}$,
    compute the empirical misdiagnosis rate of the weighted consensus among
    subsets of $k$ modalities. Compare to the bound
    $\exp(-k_{\eff} \bar{\Delta}^2 / 2)$. Verify that optimal weights
    $\omega_k = \ln(\text{DOR}_k)$ outperform uniform weights.

    \item \textbf{Theorem~3 validation:} Stratify the patient cohort by
    disease prevalence. For each prevalence stratum, compute the empirical
    false negative identification rate as a function of $M$. Verify that
    for $\pi_1 \le 10^{-3}$, $M \ge 5$ independent modalities are required
    to achieve $\Pbb(\text{detect FN}) \ge 0.95$.

    \item \textbf{Yajie consensus quality:} Compare the \Yajie{} weighted
    consensus against (a) simple majority vote, (b) single best physician,
    (c) random physician. Report AUC, sensitivity, specificity, and
    Net Reclassification Improvement (NRI).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Correlation Estimation Protocol}

The effective multiplicity $M_{\eff}$ depends critically on the inter-physician
error correlation $\bar{\rho}$. We estimate $\rho_{mm'}$ via:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item Collect $N_{\text{cal}}$ calibration cases with known ground truth.
    \item For each physician pair $(m, m')$, construct the $2 \times 2$
    contingency table of errors:
    \begin{equation}
        \rho_{mm'} = \frac{n_{11} \cdot n_{00} - n_{10} \cdot n_{01}}
                         {\sqrt{(n_{11}+n_{10})(n_{01}+n_{00})(n_{11}+n_{01})(n_{10}+n_{00})}},
    \end{equation}
    where $n_{ab} = \#\{\text{cases where physician } m \text{ error } = a,
    \text{ physician } m' \text{ error } = b\}$.
    \item Bootstrap confidence intervals: resample calibration cases with
    replacement $B = 1000$ times, recompute $\bar{\rho}$, report 95\% CI.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Limitations and Assumption Verification}
\label{sec:limitations}
% ======================================================================

We state the limitations honestly. Every theorem in this paper holds
under the eight assumptions of Section~\ref{sec:assumptions}. Below we
identify where these assumptions may fail in clinical practice.

\begin{enumerate}[label=\limitationTag{\arabic*}, nosep]

    \item \textbf{Conditional independence across modalities (\ref{ass:A2}):}
    Imaging, lab, and pathology are physically distinct measurements, but
    they may be correlated through unobserved confounders (e.g., shared
    contrast agent effects, systemic inflammation affecting multiple lab
    values, tissue preparation artifacts affecting both histology and
    immunohistochemistry). This correlation reduces $K_{\eff}$.
    \textit{Mitigation:} Estimate $\bar{\rho}_K$ from calibration data and
    use the effective count $K_{\eff}$ rather than $K$.

    \item \textbf{Inter-physician correlation estimation (\ref{ass:A3}):}
    Estimating $\rho_{mm'}$ requires ground-truth diagnoses, which are
    themselves noisy. Using expert panel consensus as ground truth introduces
    circularity --- the same physicians whose correlation we measure may
    constitute the ground-truth panel. \textit{Mitigation:} Use surgical
    pathology or long-term follow-up as independent ground truth.

    \item \textbf{Diagnostic margin $\Delta$ is case-dependent (\ref{ass:A4}):}
    The margin $\Delta$ varies across cases --- clear cases have $\Delta \approx 0.5$,
    ambiguous cases have $\Delta \approx 0$. Using a single $\Delta$ for
    all cases overestimates detection probability for easy cases and
    underestimates it for difficult cases. \textit{Mitigation:} Stratify
    by case difficulty, estimated via inter-physician agreement rate
    (high agreement $\implies$ high $\Delta$).

    \item \textbf{Prevalence stability (\ref{ass:A5}):} Disease prevalence
    shifts across populations (geographic, demographic, temporal). A diagnostic
    protocol calibrated for $\pi_1$ in one population may be miscalibrated
    in another. \textit{Mitigation:} \Spring{} gating detects prevalence
    shifts by monitoring the ratio of positive to negative diagnoses over time.

    \item \textbf{Physician sensitivity/specificity drift (\ref{ass:A8}):}
    Physicians' diagnostic accuracy changes over time --- learning improves
    it, fatigue degrades it, new guidelines shift decision boundaries.
    Static $\hat{\Sens}_m, \hat{\Spec}_m$ become stale. \textit{Mitigation:}
    Continuous \Spring{} monitoring of physician outcomes updates weights
    $\omega_m$ adaptively.

    \item \textbf{Theorem~3 resolution requirement:} For ultra-rare diseases
    ($\pi_1 < 10^{-6}$), the required $M$ for Theorem~3 resolution may
    exceed the number of available specialists worldwide. The mathematical
    guarantee exists, but the practical implementation is impossible.
    \textit{Honesty:} The theorem identifies a fundamental limitation, not
    a practical solution. For such diseases, diagnosis must rely on
    pathognomonic findings (signs/symptoms that are uniquely associated
    with the disease) rather than statistical consensus.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{Why Medicine Has Multi-Expert Intuition But No Formal Theory}

Medicine has practiced multi-expert diagnosis for centuries --- consulting
colleagues, grand rounds, tumor boards, morbidity and mortality conferences.
Yet no formal mathematical theory connects the number of physicians $M$ to
the misdiagnosis probability $\varepsilon$. This gap has practical consequences:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{No guidance on how many opinions to seek.} Is $M=2$ sufficient?
    $M=5$? The medical literature provides empirical evidence (second opinions
    change diagnosis in 10--62\% of cases) but no formula.
    \item \textbf{No quantification of diminishing returns.} When does adding
    another physician stop reducing misdiagnosis probability? When $\bar{\rho}$
    is high, $M_{\eff}$ saturates quickly.
    \item \textbf{No formal justification for tumor board size.} Why 5--10
    specialists and not 3 or 20? Theorem~1 provides the answer: compute
    $M_{\eff}$ and the target $\varepsilon$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The Spring Permanent Memory in Medicine}

The \Spring{} permanent memory mechanism has a natural clinical analog:
the electronic health record (EHR). However, current EHRs violate
\Spring{}'s monotonicity guarantee (Theorem Spring-1 from the core \SCX{}
framework): data can be deleted, modified, or lost. A \Spring{}-compliant
medical record would:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Append-only:} Every diagnosis, test result, and clinical
    note is permanently recorded with timestamp and physician identity.
    \item \textbf{Immutable audit trail:} Changes to a diagnosis (e.g.,
    initial diagnosis revised after further testing) are recorded as new
    entries linked to the original, not as overwrites.
    \item \textbf{Cumulative quality tracking:} Each physician's cumulative
    sensitivity and specificity are continuously updated, enabling real-time
    \Yajie{} weight calibration.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The Cost of Multi-Expert Diagnosis}

The primary objection to multi-expert diagnosis is cost: $M$ physicians
cost $M$ times as much as one. The \SCX{} framework reframes this as an
optimization problem:
\begin{equation}
    M^* = \argmin_{M \in \N} \Bigl\{M \cdot c_{\text{physician}} + \misdiagnosis(M) \cdot c_{\text{misdiagnosis}}\Bigr\},
\end{equation}
where $c_{\text{physician}}$ is the cost per physician consultation and
$c_{\text{misdiagnosis}}$ is the expected cost of a misdiagnosis (including
litigation, delayed treatment, unnecessary procedures, and human life).

For a disease where $c_{\text{misdiagnosis}} \gg c_{\text{physician}}$
(e.g., misdiagnosed cancer $\to$ death), the optimal $M^*$ is large.
For a disease where $c_{\text{misdiagnosis}} \approx c_{\text{physician}}$
(e.g., mild self-limiting condition), $M^* = 1$ is optimal.

\subsection{Regulatory Implications}

The \SCX{} framework implies that medical licensing, hospital privileging,
and diagnostic device approval should incorporate $M$-parameter declaration:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Diagnostic AI systems} should declare $M$ --- the number of
    independent human experts whose consensus the AI's output is equivalent to.
    \item \textbf{Clinical practice guidelines} should specify $M_{\min}$ for
    high-stakes diagnostic decisions.
    \item \textbf{Malpractice standards} should incorporate $M$: a physician
    who fails to seek a second opinion when Theorem~1 indicates $M_{\min} > 1$
    may be considered negligent.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ======================================================================

The literature on diagnostic accuracy is vast. We highlight the mathematical
connections most relevant to the \SCX{} framework.

\textbf{Diagnostic accuracy meta-analysis.} Systematic reviews of diagnostic
accuracy (e.g., Cochrane DTA reviews) estimate pooled sensitivity and
specificity across studies. These provide $\hat{\Sens}, \hat{\Spec}$ estimates
that serve as inputs to Theorems~1--3, but they do not consider multi-expert
consensus error bounds.

\textbf{Second opinion studies.} Empirical studies of second opinions
\cite{payne2014impact, bender2019second} document opinion-change rates
but do not derive the mathematical relationship between $M$, $\bar{\rho}$,
and misdiagnosis probability.

\textbf{Tumor board outcomes.} Studies of multidisciplinary team (MDT)
meetings \cite{pillay2016impact, lamb2011multidisciplinary} report diagnosis
or management changes in 25--52\% of cases but do not formalize the
consensus mechanism or provide error bounds.

\textbf{Ensemble methods in medical AI.} AI research has produced ensemble
diagnostic models (e.g., multiple CNNs for radiology, ensemble of gradient-boosted
trees for clinical prediction). These implicitly use $M > 1$ experts but:
(a) the experts share the same architecture and training data ($\bar{\rho}$
is high), (b) the $M$ parameter is never declared, and (c) error detection
bounds are not provided.

\textbf{Probabilistic diagnosis.} Bayesian diagnostic reasoning \cite{ledley1959reasoning}
computes posterior disease probabilities given test results. This is the
single-modality, single-physician version of our framework. The \SCX{} extension
is the multi-expert, multi-modality generalization with explicit error bounds.

\textbf{Condorcet jury theorem.} The Condorcet jury theorem (1785) proves
that majority vote among independent voters converges to the correct decision
as $M \to \infty$. Theorem~1 generalizes this with finite-$M$ Hoeffding
bounds, correlation correction via $M_{\eff}$, and the diagnostic margin $\Delta$.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ======================================================================

We have formalized medical diagnosis as a multi-expert audit problem under
the \SCX{} framework. The three theorems provide:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Theorem~1 (Multi-Physician Misdiagnosis Detection Theorem):}
    The probability that $M$ physicians collectively misdiagnose decays
    as $\exp(-2M_{\eff}\Delta^2)$. Required physician count for target
    misdiagnosis rate $\varepsilon$ is $M \ge \ln(1/\varepsilon)/(2\Delta^2)
    \cdot (1 + (M-1)\bar{\rho})$. For independent physicians
    ($\bar{\rho}=0, \Delta=0.3, \varepsilon=0.05$), $M \ge 17$. For
    correlated physicians ($\bar{\rho}=0.5$), $M$ must be proportionally
    larger. The second opinion ($M=2$) provides detectable but limited
    error reduction.

    \item \textbf{Theorem~2 (Multi-Modal Expert Consensus Theorem):}
    When $K$ diagnostic modalities provide conditionally independent signals,
    the \Yajie{} weighted consensus with optimal weights
    $\omega_k = \ln(\text{DOR}_k)$ achieves misdiagnosis probability
    $\le \exp(-K_{\eff}\bar{\Delta}^2/2)$. The standard clinical workflow
    --- imaging $\to$ lab $\to$ pathology --- is a sequential implementation
    of this consensus. The cost-optimal threshold $\tau^* = \ln(c_{\text{FP}}
    (1-\pi_1) / (c_{\text{FN}} \pi_1))$ balances false negative and false
    positive costs.

    \item \textbf{Theorem~3 (False Negative and Rare Disease Unidentifiability Theorem):}
    When disease prevalence $\pi_1 \to 0$, a single diagnostic modality
    cannot distinguish between true disease absence and false negative.
    The mutual information $I(\diagnosis; \disease) \to 0$. Resolution
    requires $M > \ln(1/\varepsilon)/(2\Delta^2)$ independent modalities.
    This explains the dominance of false positives in population screening
    ($\PPV \approx 4\%$ for mammography) and quantifies the benefit of
    cascade testing protocols.
\end{enumerate}

The \Yajie{} protocol operationalizes these theorems into a structured
diagnostic workflow suitable for tumor boards, multidisciplinary teams,
telemedicine platforms, and AI-assisted diagnosis. The \Cercis{} score
$S = Q + \eta N$ provides a single auditable metric for diagnostic quality,
and the \Spring{} permanent memory enables continuous physician calibration
and retrospective error analysis.

Medicine is the domain where audit matters most. A misdiagnosis is not
a misclassification on a benchmark --- it is a human life misjudged. The
\SCX{} framework does not replace clinical judgment. It provides the
mathematical structure to certify it.
