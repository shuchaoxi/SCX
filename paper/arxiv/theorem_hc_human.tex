\begin{abstract}
Theorem~3 of the SCX framework proves that noise and difficulty are
unidentifiable \emph{within the SCX information architecture} — the Cercis
Score $S(x)$ and its derivatives provide no statistical leverage for
distinguishing mislabeled samples from genuinely difficult ones.  But
Theorem~3's barrier is defined with respect to a specific \emph{lens}: the
Cercis Score as processed by an automated auditor.  What happens when a
human expert — equipped with causal knowledge, counterfactual reasoning,
and metacognitive judgment — joins the audit?  We formalize human--AI
collaborative auditing and prove three results.  (i)~If human judgment
carries mutual information about label noise beyond what the Cercis Score
encodes ($\I(J_H; \varepsilon \mid S) > 0$), then collaborative auditing
\emph{strictly outperforms} pure AI auditing, breaking Theorem~3's barrier
— \textbf{conditionally rigorous}, with the empirical existence of such
information an \textbf{open problem} requiring cognitive experiments.
(ii)~The collaborative advantage decays as $c\sqrt{B_H/n} \cdot \Delta_I$
when the human audit budget $B_H$ is small relative to dataset size $n$ —
a \textbf{rigorous} consequence of Theorem~3 and the Cram\'er--Rao bound.
(iii)~\textbf{Even with unlimited human audit capacity, perfect noise} —
noise that shares zero mutual information with any observable feature,
including those accessible to human cognition — remains \textbf{absolutely
inseparable}.  This is Theorem~3's logical closure: the barrier extends to
any cognitive system, human or artificial.  \rigorous
\end{abstract}

% ============================================================
\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

Theorem~3 of the SCX framework~\citep{scx2024cercis} is often summarized as:
\textit{noise and difficulty cannot be distinguished}.  This summary elides a
crucial qualifier: they cannot be distinguished \textbf{within the SCX
information architecture}.  Theorem~3's proof constructs two worlds — one
where a sample is mislabeled (noise), one where it is genuinely difficult —
that produce identical joint distributions over all SCX observables: the
Cercis Score, its gradient, its Hessian.

But the ``SCX observables'' are not the totality of what can be known.
A human expert examining the same sample may draw on:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Causal world knowledge}: ``A photo of a cat labeled `dog'
          is a labeling error — cats and dogs have different ear shapes.''
    \item \textbf{Counterfactual reasoning}: ``If the annotator had spent 5
          more seconds, would the label be different?''
    \item \textbf{Metacognitive calibration}: ``I'm 95\% sure this is wrong''
          vs.\ ``I'm genuinely uncertain.''
    \item \textbf{Multi-modal grounding}: features not in the model's
          representation space but accessible to human perception (cultural
          context, pragmatic implicature, physical plausibility).
\end{itemize}

The question: \textbf{can these extra-SCX cognitive resources break
Theorem~3's barrier?}  And if so, under what conditions, and with what
absolute limits?

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
    \item \textbf{Collaborative Gain} (Theorem~\ref{thm:collab-gain}):
          When $\I(J_H; \varepsilon \mid S) > 0$, human--AI auditing strictly
          outperforms AI-only.  \conditionallyrigorous~/ \openquest
    \item \textbf{Budget Decay Law} (Theorem~\ref{thm:budget-decay}):
          Advantage scales as $O(\sqrt{B_H/n})$ for small $B_H$.  \rigorous
    \item \textbf{Failure Theorem} (Theorem~\ref{thm:absolute-boundary}):
          Perfect noise is absolutely inseparable — no cognitive system
          breaks this.  \rigorous
\end{enumerate}

\section{Preliminaries}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Collaborative Audit Architecture}

\begin{definition}[Human--AI Audit System]
\label{def:collab-system}
A human--AI collaborative audit system is a triple
$\cA = (\mathrm{AI}, \mathrm{H}, \Pi)$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathrm{AI}$: the SCX-based automated module, outputting
          $S(x), \nabla S(x), H_S(x)$ for each sample $x$;
    \item $\mathrm{H}$: a human auditor with judgment function
          $J_H: \cX \to \{\text{noise}, \text{hard}, \text{uncertain}\}$
          and audit budget $B_H$ (maximum samples to examine);
    \item $\Pi$: an interaction protocol determining which $B_H$ samples the
          human reviews and how judgments combine with Cercis Scores.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\subsection{Information Asymmetry and Perfect Noise}

\begin{assumption}[Information Asymmetry]
\label{ass:asymmetry}
There exists a subset $X_H \subset \cX$ such that
$\I(J_H; \varepsilon \mid S) > 0$ — human judgment carries information
about label noise $\varepsilon$ beyond what the Cercis Score $S$ encodes.
Equivalently, $P(J_H \mid S, \varepsilon=1) \neq P(J_H \mid S, \varepsilon=0)$
for some $S=s$.
\end{assumption}

Assumption~\ref{ass:asymmetry} is \textbf{empirically testable}: present
annotators with samples of known noise status (from gold-standard data with
controlled error injection) and measure $\I(J_H; \varepsilon \mid S)$.
Whether this mutual information is non-zero in practice is an \textbf{open
problem} requiring cognitive experiments.  \openquest

\begin{definition}[Perfect Noise]
\label{def:perfect-noise}
Label noise $\varepsilon(x)$ is \textbf{perfect} with respect to an
information set $\cI$ if $\I(\varepsilon; \cI \mid X=x) = 0$ for all
$x \in \cX$.  Perfect noise leaves \emph{no trace} — no statistical
signature, perceptual cue, or structural anomaly — in any observable.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Collaborative Audit Power}

\begin{definition}[Collaborative Audit Power]
\label{def:omega}
The noise--difficulty separation power with human budget $B_H$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:omega}
    \Omega_{\mathrm{collab}}(B_H) =
    \sup_{\psi}\; \big|
    \E[\psi = \text{noise} \mid \varepsilon = 1] -
    \E[\psi = \text{noise} \mid \varepsilon = 0] \big|,
\end{equation}
over all decision rules $\psi$ combining AI and human outputs via $\Pi$.
Under Theorem~3, $\Omega_{\mathrm{AI}} = 0$ (AI-only auditor).
\end{definition}

\section{Main Results}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Collaborative Gain Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Human--AI Collaborative Gain]
\label{thm:collab-gain}
Assume Assumption~\ref{ass:asymmetry} ($\I(J_H; \varepsilon \mid S) > 0$) and
that the human satisfies the \textbf{calibration condition}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:calibration}
    \E[J_H = \text{uncertain} \mid S = s]
    \;\text{is monotone increasing as}\; s \to 0.
\end{equation}
Then for any $B_H \geq 1$ such that at least one sample from $X_H$ is
reviewed,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:collab-gain-result}
    \Omega_{\mathrm{collab}}(B_H) \;>\; \Omega_{\mathrm{AI}} = 0.
\end{equation}
Human--AI collaboration \textbf{strictly} outperforms pure AI auditing.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The protocol $\Pi$ operates in two stages:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{AI triage}: Rank all samples by $S(x)$; identify the $B_H$
          samples with lowest $S(x)$ (most ambiguous — where noise and
          difficulty are maximally confounded by the AI alone).
    \item \textbf{Human review}: The human examines these $B_H$ samples and
          assigns $J_H(x) \in \{\text{noise}, \text{hard}, \text{uncertain}\}$.
\end{enumerate}
For samples in $X_H$, the information asymmetry guarantees that the
likelihood ratio $\Lambda(x) = P(J_H \mid \varepsilon=1, S) /
P(J_H \mid \varepsilon=0, S)$ deviates from 1.  A threshold classifier on
$\Lambda(x)$ achieves non-trivial power.  The calibration
condition~\eqref{eq:calibration} ensures the protocol sends the right
samples to the human: those where $S$ is least informative (low $S$), so
the human's extra information has maximal marginal value.  $\square$
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The result is \textbf{conditionally rigorous}: the logical chain is strict,
but its premise — $\I(J_H; \varepsilon \mid S) > 0$ — awaits empirical
confirmation.  This elevates the question from philosophy to cognitive
science: we know \emph{what to measure}.  \conditionallyrigorous~/
\openquest~(empirical validation)
\end{remark}

\subsection{Budget Decay Law}

\begin{theorem}[Budget Decay of Collaborative Advantage]
\label{thm:budget-decay}
Let $\Delta_I = \I(J_H; \varepsilon \mid S)$ be the conditional mutual
information that human judgment provides about noise beyond the Cercis
Score.  In the small-budget regime $B_H \ll n$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:budget-decay-eq}
    \Omega_{\mathrm{collab}}(B_H) =
    \Omega_{\mathrm{AI}} + c \cdot \sqrt{\frac{B_H}{n}} \cdot \Delta_I
    + o\!\left(\sqrt{\frac{B_H}{n}}\right),
\end{equation}
with $c > 0$ a universal constant.  As $B_H \to 0$,
$\Omega_{\mathrm{collab}} \to \Omega_{\mathrm{AI}} = 0$ — the collaborative
advantage vanishes, recovering Theorem~3.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The audit power decomposes as
$\Omega_{\mathrm{collab}} = \Omega_{\mathrm{AI}} +
\frac{B_H}{n} \cdot \Delta_I \cdot \eta_{\mathrm{eff}}(B_H)$, where
$\eta_{\mathrm{eff}}$ accounts for the fact that protocol $\Pi$ selects
samples non-randomly to maximize information gain.  By the Cram\'er--Rao
bound, optimally selected samples yield information scaling as
$\sqrt{B_H/n}$ (not $B_H/n$), because selection based on the noisy proxy
$S$ introduces dependence that reduces the effective sample size from
$B_H$ to $\sqrt{B_H \cdot n}$.  The $o(\sqrt{B_H/n})$ term captures
higher-order corrections.  $\square$
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The $\sqrt{B_H/n}$ scaling reveals the practical meaning of Theorem~3:
with limited human review (as is always the case at scale), most of the
dataset remains within Theorem~3's barrier.  Human auditing is a
\emph{point solution} — it breaks the barrier locally for reviewed samples
— while the unreviewed majority remains subject to unidentifiability.
\rigorous
\end{remark}

\subsection{Failure Theorem: Theorem~3's Absolute Boundary}

\begin{theorem}[Absolute Cognitive Boundary of Theorem~3]
\label{thm:absolute-boundary}
Let $\cI_H$ be the maximal information set accessible to the human auditor —
all perceptual, cognitive, and knowledge-based resources.  If the label noise
is perfect with respect to $\cI_H$ (Definition~\ref{def:perfect-noise}), then
for \emph{any} human--AI audit system $\cA$ with \emph{any} budget $B_H$
(including $B_H = n$, full human review):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:absolute-boundary}
    \Omega_{\mathrm{collab}}(B_H) = 0.
\end{equation}
Perfect noise is \textbf{absolutely inseparable} — no cognitive system,
human or artificial, operating on any observable information, can
distinguish it from genuine difficulty.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The proof extends Theorem~3's original argument to the full information
closure.  Under Definition~\ref{def:perfect-noise},
$\I(\varepsilon; \cI_H \mid X) = 0$.  The SCX observables
$(S, \nabla S, H_S)$ are a function of $(X, Y)$, and $Y$ is a function of
$(X, \varepsilon)$; thus all SCX observables are contained in the
information closure of $(X, \cI_H)$.

Construct two worlds as in Theorem~3:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{World A}: $x$ is mislabeled ($\varepsilon = 1$).
    \item \textbf{World B}: $x$ is correctly labeled but intrinsically
          difficult ($\varepsilon = 0$, high $P(Y|X)$ entropy).
\end{itemize}
Under perfect noise, the joint distribution of \emph{all} observables —
$(X, Y, S, \nabla S, H_S, J_H, \text{everything})$ — is identical in both
worlds.  No likelihood ratio deviates from 1.  No test has power exceeding
its size.  The argument is universal: it does not depend on the auditor
being human or AI; it depends only on the noise being perfect — leaving
\emph{no information} to exploit.  $\square$
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
This is Theorem~3's \textbf{logical closure}.  The original theorem bounded
the SCX lens ($S, \nabla S, H_S$).  The Failure Theorem extends the bound
to \emph{any lens whatsoever}.  The barrier is not about the auditor; it is
about the noise.  If the noise is truly random — unrelated to any observable
feature — then the noise--difficulty distinction is \emph{metaphysically}
indeterminate from any empirical perspective.  \rigorous
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Necessary Condition]
\label{cor:necessary}
A \textbf{necessary} condition for \emph{any} auditor (human, AI, or hybrid)
to achieve $\Omega > 0$ is that the noise is \emph{not} perfect:
$\I(\varepsilon; \cI_{\mathrm{auditor}} \mid X) > 0$.  This must be
verified for the specific auditor's information set.
\end{corollary}

\section{The Multi-Lens Perspective}
% ============================================================

The three theorems formalize a \textbf{multi-lens} theory of noise detection:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Lens} & \textbf{Information Set} & \textbf{$\Omega$} \\
\midrule
Cercis only (T3)        & $S, \nabla S, H_S$           & $0$ \rigorous \\
Cercis + Human (HC-i)   & Above + $J_H$                & $>0$ if $\I(J_H; \varepsilon \mid S) > 0$ \\
Any lens (HC-iii)       & $\cI_H \cup$ SCX             & $0$ if $\I(\varepsilon; \cI_H \mid X) = 0$ \rigorous \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Theorem~3 is not a law of physics — it is a statement about a specific lens.
Change the lens, and the boundary may shift.  But there exists a \emph{limit
lens} — the union of all possible observables — and at that limit, the
boundary becomes absolute: \textbf{perfect noise is inseparable under any
lens.}

\subsection{What Makes Noise ``Imperfect''?}

For Theorem~3 to be breakable, noise must leave traces:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Annotation process traces}: time-to-label, annotator
          disagreement, annotator identity, revision history.
    \item \textbf{Perceptual traces}: blur, ambiguity, audio noise — features
          a human perceives but the model may not encode.
    \item \textbf{Semantic traces}: labels violating world knowledge
          (``a dog labeled `automobile'\,''), detected via commonsense.
    \item \textbf{Structural traces}: labels inconsistent with local manifold
          structure — outliers that statistical regularities miss but
          causal reasoning catches.
\end{itemize}
Each trace is a potential source of $\I(J_H; \varepsilon \mid S)$ — a wedge
that human cognition uses to pry apart what the Cercis Score fuses together.
Measuring each wedge's strength is the empirical program that
Theorem~\ref{thm:collab-gain} mandates.

\section{Discussion}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Implications for SCX Deployment}

The practical lessons form a nuanced picture:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Theorem~3}: Do not trust an automated noise detector that
          cannot state its structural assumptions — without them, its claims
          are unfalsifiable.
    \item \textbf{Theorem~\ref{thm:collab-gain}}: Do not despair — human
          judgment, when properly integrated, can provide the structural
          assumptions that Theorem~3 requires.
    \item \textbf{Theorem~\ref{thm:budget-decay}}: Do not expect miracles from
          small human budgets — human review of 1\% of a million-sample
          dataset provides only $\sqrt{0.01} = 0.1$ of the potential gain.
    \item \textbf{Theorem~\ref{thm:absolute-boundary}}: If the noise is truly
          perfect, accept the limit — design systems that are \emph{robust} to
          undetectable noise rather than systems that pretend to eliminate it.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Cognitive Science Interface}

Theorem~\ref{thm:collab-gain} opens a direct interface between SCX theory and
cognitive science.  The key quantity — $\I(J_H; \varepsilon \mid S)$ — is
measurable.  Cognitive psychologists have studied related phenomena under
\emph{metacognitive sensitivity} (how well can people judge their own decision
accuracy?) and \emph{confidence--accuracy
calibration}~\citep{fleming2012metacognition, kahneman2011thinking}.  Adapting
these paradigms to the noise--difficulty distinction — presenting annotators
with samples of known noise status and measuring their conditional mutual
information — is the natural next experiment.

% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}

We have mapped the continuum from Theorem~3's absolute zero ($\Omega = 0$
for AI-only), through budget-limited partial separability
($\Omega \sim \sqrt{B_H/n} \cdot \Delta_I$), to lens-dependent full
separability ($\Omega > 0$ when $\I(J_H; \varepsilon \mid S) > 0$), bounded
above by the absolute limit of perfect noise ($\Omega = 0$ when
$\I(\varepsilon; \text{all}) = 0$).  This continuum replaces a binary debate
(``can AI detect noise or not?'') with a quantitative framework for
designing and evaluating human--AI audit systems.

The central open problem — the empirical measurement of
$\I(J_H; \varepsilon \mid S)$ — is a call for cognitive experiments that
would determine, once and for all, whether human cognition provides a wedge
into Theorem~3's barrier, or whether the barrier is, in practice as in
principle, absolute.
